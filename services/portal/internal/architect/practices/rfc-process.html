RFC Process — collaborative design before code

A written proposal open for structured comment from across the organisation before a significant decision is accepted.

TL;DR

An RFC (Request for Comments) is a written proposal for a significant technical decision, opened for structured commentary before acceptance or rejection. The format originates from IETF's 1969 series of internet standards and is now used by Rust, React, Node.js, and most large engineering organisations. The RFC separates "what should we do" (RFC) from "here is what we decided" (ADR). Without an RFC process, significant changes get decided in hallway conversations and critical concerns surface only after implementation begins — when feedback is most expensive.

What it is

An RFC is a written proposal for a significant change, opened for structured commentary before it is accepted or rejected. The format traces back to 1969, when Steve Crocker wrote RFC 1 for the ARPANET. The IETF still publishes internet standards as RFCs. In software engineering, the term has been adopted by major projects (Rust, React, Node.js, Kubernetes) as a way to make significant design decisions transparent and collaborative.

A typical RFC contains:

  • Summary — a one-paragraph TL;DR for readers who will not read the full document.
  • Motivation — why are we proposing this? What problem does it solve?
  • Proposed solution — the design in enough detail to evaluate it.
  • Alternatives considered — two or three other approaches with their trade-offs.
  • Open questions — what the author wants feedback on specifically.
  • Risks & unresolved issues — what could go wrong; what is intentionally left for later.

The comment window (typically 1–2 weeks) is async-first — feedback is posted as inline comments or in a discussion thread. After the window closes, the author revises and the decision is made. The accepted RFC is archived; the resulting decision is recorded as an ADR.

Why we need it

Without an RFC process, significant decisions get made in hallway conversations or Slack threads. Engineers across timezones miss the discussion. Critical concerns surface only after implementation begins, when feedback is most expensive to incorporate. Decisions feel arbitrary to the engineers who were not in the room.

An RFC fixes both problems. The written proposal forces precision — writing makes vagueness visible. The async commentary window includes engineers across timezones who would otherwise be excluded. By the time the implementation starts, the major design questions have been debated and resolved in writing. The RFC trail also creates institutional memory: a year later, the team can understand why a design was chosen and what was considered.

How others do it

Rust

The Rust project's RFC process, started in 2014, is the canonical example for open-source. RFCs live in github.com/rust-lang/rfcs as Markdown files. A new RFC is a PR. The comment window is open-ended but typically 7–14 days for "final comment period." A Final Comment Period (FCP) gives one week's warning before merge. The Rust core team makes the final call. Every major Rust feature since 1.0 has an RFC. The process is used by 700+ contributors and has produced 3000+ RFCs.

What we take: The "Final Comment Period" mechanic is high-value. After active discussion ends, give one week's warning before closing the RFC. The week catches stakeholders who missed the active discussion.

Source: github.com/rust-lang/rfcs

React

The React team adopted an RFC process in 2018 after the Hooks proposal was met with both enthusiasm and concern. RFCs are published at github.com/reactjs/rfcs and are opened for community comment before features are added to React. Hooks (RFC 0068), Concurrent Mode (RFC 0118), and Server Components (RFC 0188) all went through public RFC commentary. The React team treats RFCs as the primary mechanism for community input on framework direction.

What we take: An RFC process is also a community-management tool. Opening proposals for comment generates engagement and surfaces concerns the core team cannot anticipate alone.

Source: github.com/reactjs/rfcs

Uber

Uber's internal RFC process, called "Engineering Standards Council" (ESC), reviews company-wide technical proposals affecting multiple teams. RFCs are required for: new shared infrastructure, new programming language adoption, changes to core service contracts, and any decision affecting more than 3 teams. The ESC includes principal engineers from each platform area. Each RFC has a stated "blast radius" describing which teams are affected and which need to vote.

What we take: Define the "blast radius" of each RFC at the start. The reviewer list is derived from the blast radius. Avoids over-soliciting feedback from teams that are not affected and under-soliciting from teams that are.

Source: Uber Engineering — Decision-making at scale

Anatomy

Scope Trigger

Defines when an RFC is required vs. just an ADR. Typical RFC triggers: affects more than 2 teams, introduces a new programming language or framework, changes a public API contract, or requires more than 4 engineer-weeks to implement. Below this bar, an ADR is sufficient.

Required Sections

Summary, Motivation, Proposed Solution, Alternatives Considered, Open Questions, Risks. The Alternatives section is non-negotiable — an RFC that proposes only one solution is not yet an RFC, it is a decision in disguise. At least two alternatives must be evaluated with their trade-offs.

Comment Window

A defined period (7–14 days) during which comments are collected. Async-first — feedback as PR comments or in a linked discussion. Synchronous review meetings are supplementary, not primary. The window has a published end date so contributors know when feedback must arrive.

Final Comment Period (FCP)

A 1-week warning before the RFC closes. After active discussion ends, the author labels the RFC "Final Comment Period" with the planned closing date. Catches stakeholders who were unavailable during active discussion. Without an FCP, decisions can feel rushed to engineers who were on PTO during the comment window.

Decision & ADR

After the FCP, the decision-maker (architect, tech lead, or council) accepts or rejects with a written rationale. An accepted RFC is archived to the RFCs repo. The resulting decision is recorded as an ADR in the codebase. The ADR cites the RFC; the RFC cites the ADR — bidirectional linking preserves the trail.

Common pitfalls

Pitfall Why it happens What to do instead
RFC process applied to trivial decisions Teams over-apply the process. Every small choice goes through a 2-week RFC. The overhead becomes a tax on small improvements and the process loses credibility. Define the scope trigger explicitly. Decisions below the trigger use an ADR (no comment window). RFCs reserved for cross-team, high-blast-radius, or hard-to-reverse decisions. Most decisions should be ADRs, not RFCs.
RFCs that never converge — endless discussion No defined comment window or FCP. Discussion continues indefinitely. The proposal becomes a "perpetual draft." Engineers stop engaging because nothing ever resolves. Set the comment window deadline at RFC opening. Run an explicit FCP. The decision-maker has authority to close the RFC after FCP regardless of whether all concerns are resolved. Unresolved concerns become Open Questions in the resulting ADR.
Single-option RFCs (proposal disguised as RFC) The author already knows what they want and writes an RFC that only describes their preferred option. The Alternatives section is perfunctory. The "comment window" is theatre — the decision was already made. Reject RFCs without genuine alternatives. The Alternatives section must include two or more options the author seriously considered. If only one option was considered, write an ADR instead — be honest about the decision being already made.
