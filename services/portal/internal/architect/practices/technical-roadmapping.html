Technical Roadmapping — align tech with business

A rolling 12-month view of platform investments and capability builds, aligned to business outcomes so executives can make informed trade-offs.

TL;DR

A Technical Roadmap is a rolling 12-month plan of infrastructure investments, platform migrations, and capability builds — each tied to a business outcome and a cost-of-delay estimate. Unlike a feature roadmap, it visualises the "below the waterline" work that enables product velocity: observability platforms, database migrations, auth rewrites, and tech debt paydowns. Without a visible technical roadmap, engineering debt accumulates invisibly until a crisis forces an expensive rewrite at the worst possible time.

What it is

A Technical Roadmap is a structured, time-horizoned plan of platform investments and capability builds. It is distinct from a product roadmap (which tracks features) and a sprint backlog (which tracks tasks). The technical roadmap tracks the "below the waterline" work: observability improvements, infrastructure migrations, security hardening, tech debt paydown, and new platform capabilities.

Each item on the roadmap has:

  • Business outcome — what business goal does this enable? ("Enables 10× traffic scale", "Reduces P1 incident rate by 50%")
  • Cost of delay — what is the cost per month of not doing this? Forces prioritisation by business value, not technical preference.
  • Owner — the engineer or team accountable for delivery.
  • Status — Now (this quarter) / Next (next 1–2 quarters) / Later (future intent).

The Now/Next/Later format (popularised by Janna Bastow and Intercom) is preferred over a Gantt chart for technical roadmaps because it is less brittle and communicates confidence appropriately — Later items are intent, not commitment.

Why we need it

Without a visible technical roadmap, engineering debt accumulates invisibly. Product managers and executives see only feature delivery. The platform work that enables product velocity — observability, database health, auth reliability — is invisible until it fails in production at the worst possible moment. The quarterly "we cannot ship this feature because the auth service needs to be rewritten first" conversation becomes a crisis instead of a planned trade-off.

A visible roadmap with cost-of-delay estimates changes the conversation. "This auth rewrite costs $30k/month in delayed features if we don't do it in Q2" is a business conversation, not a technical complaint. Executives who can see the trade-off can make informed decisions. Architects who cannot frame technical work in business terms lose the resource allocation argument every time.

How others do it

Intercom

Intercom popularised the "Product Roadmap is Not a Feature Factory" philosophy and uses Now/Next/Later for both product and technical roadmaps. Their technical roadmap is maintained by the VP of Engineering and shared with the product leadership team quarterly. Items on the roadmap are explicitly linked to product strategy outcomes — "enable real-time features" links to a WebSockets platform item. Without that link, platform work competes with features in a zero-sum game.

What we take: Technical roadmap items must be linked to product outcomes to compete for resources. "We need to migrate the database" loses to "we need to ship checkout v2" unless you can show what checkout v2 is blocked by.

Source: Intercom — Product strategy means saying no

Netflix

Netflix publishes its engineering strategy through the Tech Blog and has described the "full cycle developer" model where engineers own infrastructure as well as features. Netflix's technical roadmap is decentralised — each product team owns a 12-month technical roadmap for their domain — but an Architecture Council reviews cross-cutting concerns quarterly. The Cloud Migration (from physical data centres to AWS, 2008–2016) is the most documented long-running technical roadmap execution: 8 years, communicated consistently as a business-enablement story.

What we take: Long-running migrations require a multi-year roadmap communicated quarterly. The narrative must stay consistent: "this enables the business to do X" — not "we are paying down technical debt."

Source: Netflix Tech Blog — Cloud migration rationale

Will Larson (Staff Engineer framework)

Will Larson's An Elegant Puzzle (2019) and Staff Engineer (2021) formalise the technical roadmap as a core staff engineer deliverable. Larson's "write an engineering strategy" model defines three layers: technical vision (multi-year), technical roadmap (12-month), and technical work (quarterly). The roadmap's primary output is a prioritised list of bets with a written rationale for each — documented enough to survive the architect who wrote it changing roles.

What we take: Write the roadmap so it survives your absence. The rationale for each item must be explicit enough that a new architect can continue the work without re-deriving the reasoning.

Source: lethain.com — Good engineering strategy is boring

Anatomy

Now / Next / Later Horizon

Now = this quarter (committed, owned, estimated). Next = next 1–2 quarters (directional, may shift). Later = 6–18 months (intent, likely to change). Never put more than 5 items in Now — commitment is the bottleneck, not ideas. Later items signal strategic direction, not delivery promises.

Business Outcome Link

Each item must state the business outcome it enables in one sentence. Format: "[Technical initiative] enables [business outcome] by [mechanism]." Example: "SLO rollout enables 99.9% uptime SLA for enterprise customers by giving the team a shared definition of healthy." Items without a business outcome are not roadmap items — they are tasks.

Cost-of-Delay Estimate

An estimate of the business cost per month of not doing this initiative. Forces prioritisation by value rather than technical elegance. Even a rough estimate ($10k/month in delayed feature capacity) is more useful than no estimate. Use CD3 (Cost of Delay Divided by Duration) for prioritisation when multiple items compete for the same team.

Monthly CTO Review

The roadmap is reviewed monthly with the CTO or engineering leadership. Agenda: (1) status of Now items, (2) any items moving from Next to Now or being de-prioritised, (3) new items entering the radar. Shared with product leadership quarterly — enough for product to align feature sequencing around technical dependencies.

Common pitfalls

Pitfall Why it happens What to do instead
Technical roadmap items without business outcome links The roadmap is written by engineers for engineers. Items are described in technical terms ("migrate from MongoDB to PostgreSQL") with no business rationale. Product leadership sees a list of engineering vanity projects and does not prioritise them. For every technical item, write the sentence: "This enables [business outcome] by [mechanism]." Refuse to add items to the roadmap without this sentence. It is harder to write than it looks — which surfaces items that lack a business case.
Roadmap treated as a commitment rather than a direction signal Executives interpret the Now/Next/Later roadmap as a date-based commitment. When Next items slip, the architect is held accountable for breaking promises that were never intended as commitments. Label confidence levels explicitly. Now items are commitments with estimates. Next items are directional (50% likely). Later items are intent (no commitment). Communicate this framing in the first roadmap presentation and repeat it consistently.
Roadmap never updated — becomes stale within a quarter The roadmap is created at the start of the year and then forgotten. By month 3, the business context has changed, three Now items have been de-prioritised, and new urgent items have emerged. The roadmap is fiction. Add roadmap review to the monthly engineering leadership calendar. Budget 30 minutes per session. The roadmap must reflect the current reality, not the year's original plan.
