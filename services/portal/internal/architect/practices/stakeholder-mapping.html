Stakeholder Mapping — communicate by audience

Tailor each communication's format and depth to the audience's power and interest so architecture work becomes visible and funded.

TL;DR

Stakeholder Mapping categorises every audience by influence and interest using a Power-Interest grid (Mendelow, 1991). For each quadrant the architect defines a communication strategy: what to share, how often, and in what format. Architects who skip this end up writing 30-page design docs for audiences that needed a 3-slide deck. Stakeholder mapping is the difference between technical excellence and organisational influence — architecture work that is not communicated at the right level of abstraction stays invisible, and invisible work does not get funded.

What it is

Stakeholder Mapping is a structured process for identifying every audience affected by an architectural initiative and defining how to communicate with each. The canonical tool is the Power-Interest grid, attributed to Aubrey Mendelow (1991), which places stakeholders on two axes:

  • Power — how much influence does this stakeholder have over the initiative's success? Can they approve or block resources? Does their endorsement determine adoption?
  • Interest — how much does this stakeholder care about the outcome? Are they actively affected day-to-day?

The grid produces four quadrants, each with a recommended communication strategy:

  • High Power, High Interest — Manage Closely. Detailed design docs, weekly syncs, decision involvement.
  • High Power, Low Interest — Keep Satisfied. Monthly 1-pager, quarterly executive review, no surprises.
  • Low Power, High Interest — Keep Informed. Tech talks, public RFCs, public ADRs. They become advocates.
  • Low Power, Low Interest — Monitor. Quarterly newsletter or all-hands mention. Minimal investment.

Why we need it

Architects who do not map stakeholders default to one of two failure modes. Either they over-communicate to everyone (long design docs for executives who needed three slides) or under-communicate to everyone (a Slack message announcing a major architectural change to people who needed a meeting). Both fail.

The Power-Interest grid forces deliberate communication. Architecture work that is not communicated to High-Power stakeholders does not get funded — even if it is technically correct. Architecture work that is not communicated to High-Interest engineers is not adopted — even if it is mandated. The grid prevents communication from being a side effect of "whoever happens to ask" and turns it into a deliberate strategy.

How others do it

Will Larson — Staff Engineer model

Will Larson's Staff Engineer (2021) explicitly identifies "communicating to executives" as one of the core staff engineer skills. The book defines four staff engineer archetypes (Tech Lead, Architect, Solver, Right Hand) and notes that all four require executive-stakeholder communication. Larson's specific guidance: write executive summaries as standalone artefacts that can be read in 3 minutes, with the technical detail in linked appendices. The summary answers "what business outcome does this enable?" before any technical content.

What we take: Write the executive summary as a standalone deliverable. If the executive only reads the first paragraph, they should still know what to fund and what to expect. The technical detail is a linked appendix.

Source: StaffEng — Will Larson

Camille Fournier — Manager / Architect playbook

Camille Fournier (former CTO of Rent The Runway, author of The Manager's Path) writes extensively about the "manager-as-translator" role and applies the same logic to architects. Her guidance: an architect is a translator between engineering and business. The translation requires understanding the business stakeholder's actual decision context — what they will and won't approve, what their constraints are, what language they use. Stakeholder mapping is the input to that translation work.

What we take: For each High-Power stakeholder, document their typical decision context: their KPIs, their constraints, their preferred format. Use this profile when writing communications for them. It is faster to write one tailored 1-pager than to discover after a rejection that the executive needed a different frame.

Source: Camille Fournier — elidedbranches.com

Nielsen Norman Group (UX research foundation)

NN/g's stakeholder analysis methodology, derived from UX research practice, formalises stakeholder mapping for product and engineering teams. NN/g recommends a two-axis grid (Power × Interest) and a stakeholder interview round to validate each stakeholder's actual interest level — assumptions about interest often differ from reality. Stakeholders who appear High-Interest in an org chart may be low-interest in practice, and vice versa.

What we take: Validate the stakeholder map with a brief 15-minute interview per High-Power stakeholder. Ask: "What level of involvement do you actually want?" The answer is often less than the architect assumed.

Source: NN/g — Stakeholder analysis

Anatomy

Power-Interest Grid

A 2×2 grid plotting each stakeholder by Power (low/high) and Interest (low/high). Drawn for each significant architectural initiative. Stored in the design doc. Max 12 stakeholders on one grid — beyond that the map becomes unreadable.

Communication Strategy per Quadrant

For each quadrant, define: (1) what content to share, (2) what format, (3) what cadence. High-Power/High-Interest gets the full design doc with weekly updates. Low-Power/Low-Interest gets a quarterly newsletter mention. Document the strategy alongside the grid.

Stakeholder Profile

For each High-Power stakeholder: name, role, their KPIs/OKRs, their typical objection patterns, their preferred format (slide deck / 1-pager / verbal briefing / Slack thread). Compiled from past interactions. Living document — updated as the relationship matures.

Asymmetric Investment

Time investment per stakeholder is proportional to their Power × Interest score, not equal across all stakeholders. A CTO who can approve $500k in headcount deserves 5× the communication investment of a peer team that is mildly affected. This asymmetry is the point of the practice.

Common pitfalls

Pitfall Why it happens What to do instead
One-size-fits-all communication (same doc for everyone) The architect writes a 20-page design doc and shares the link with everyone. Executives skim the first page and bounce. Engineers read everything. The doc is mis-pitched for both audiences. Write the audience-specific summary first (1 page for executives) and the full design doc separately (10–20 pages for engineers). The summary links to the doc, not the other way around.
Assuming Interest level rather than asking Engineers assume the CTO is High-Interest in their initiative when in reality the CTO has 12 competing concerns and is Medium-Interest at most. The architect over-invests in CTO communication and under-invests in the actual decision-maker (a director). Validate Interest with a 15-minute stakeholder interview. Ask: "How much detail do you want, and how often?" The answer recalibrates the strategy. Re-validate annually.
Stakeholder map drawn once and never updated The map is created at the start of an initiative and never revisited. Six months in, the CTO has left, two new VPs have joined, and the map is fiction. Communication continues on auto-pilot to people who no longer matter. Revisit the map at each architecture retrospective (quarterly). Add new stakeholders. Demote stakeholders whose Interest or Power has changed. The map is a living artefact, not a one-time deliverable.
