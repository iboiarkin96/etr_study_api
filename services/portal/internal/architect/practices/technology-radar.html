Technology Radar — portfolio of tech choices

A living map of the technology portfolio reviewed quarterly so the team can see which tools to invest in and which to stop using.

TL;DR

A Technology Radar is a visual assessment of the tech portfolio across four rings: Adopt (use in production), Trial (use in a project to learn), Assess (explore with a spike), Hold (don't start new projects with this). Pioneered by ThoughtWorks in 2010. Reviewed quarterly by the architecture guild and published as a static page, it makes technology choices transparent, prevents stack drift, and gives new hires a fast orientation to the team's technology philosophy.

What it is

A Technology Radar is a structured way to communicate technology choices across an engineering organisation. It was popularised by ThoughtWorks, who have published their public radar every 6 months since 2010. The radar covers four quadrants (Tools, Platforms, Languages & Frameworks, Techniques) and four rings:

  • Adopt — technologies the team recommends for broad use. Used in production, well understood, low risk.
  • Trial — technologies worth pursuing in a low-risk project. The team has experience with them. Ready to use at scale with some caution.
  • Assess — technologies worth exploring with a PoC or spike to understand the implications. Promising but not yet proven.
  • Hold — technologies that should not be started in new projects. Typically: abandoned libraries, security risks, or things the team has evaluated and rejected.

The team's internal radar is maintained as a JSON file (Zalando format) and rendered as a static HTML page. Reviewed quarterly at the architecture guild session. Each item has a short rationale.

Why we need it

Without a radar, the stack quietly accumulates every library someone tried on a weekend project. A new engineer asking "which HTTP client should I use?" gets five different answers from five engineers. Libraries in Hold status (abandoned, insecure, replaced) continue to appear in new services because nobody communicated the decision to stop using them.

The radar makes technology choices explicit and visible. A new hire can read the radar on day one and understand the team's technology philosophy in 20 minutes. Hold decisions are documented with rationale, not just institutional memory. The quarterly review creates a forcing function for the team to reflect on emerging technologies and deprecate ones that are not working.

How others do it

ThoughtWorks (originator)

ThoughtWorks has published the Technology Radar every 6 months since 2010. Each edition covers ~80–100 items across four quadrants. The editorial process involves 50+ senior ThoughtWorks technologists reviewing and debating each item. The ThoughtWorks radar has placed items like Docker (2014, Assess), Kubernetes (2017, Trial), and Infrastructure as Code (2011, Adopt) on the radar years before mainstream adoption — making it a reliable leading indicator for the industry.

What we take: The editorial process matters as much as the output. A radar produced by one architect is less valuable than one produced by a 5-engineer guild session. Schedule the quarterly session; the debate is the product.

Source: ThoughtWorks Technology Radar

Zalando

Zalando open-sourced the Tech Radar renderer (github.com/zalando/tech-radar) as a static HTML/JS page that reads a JSON config. Adopted by hundreds of companies to host their own internal radars. Zalando uses the radar to coordinate technology choices across 2000+ engineers and 150+ teams. Their internal radar is reviewed by a central architecture board quarterly and includes a "why" for every Hold item.

What we take: The Zalando renderer is the fastest way to publish an internal radar. Start with the JSON format; it takes one afternoon to populate with current technologies.

Source: github.com/zalando/tech-radar

Spotify

Spotify's architecture chapter maintains an internal "Golden Path" — a curated stack of Adopt-level technologies that are supported by the platform team with templates, documentation, and on-call. Technologies outside the Golden Path can be used but are unsupported. The Golden Path concept is an operationalised version of the Adopt ring: not just "this is good" but "this comes with platform support, a template, and documentation."

What we take: Elevate Adopt items to "Golden Path" status with operational support. An Adopt item without a template or documentation is only half as useful as one with both.

Source: Spotify Engineering — Golden Paths

Anatomy

JSON Source File

A tech-radar.json file in the repo root, following the Zalando format. Each entry: name, ring (adopt/trial/assess/hold), quadrant (tools/platforms/languages/techniques), description (2–3 sentences of rationale). Versioned in git; changes reviewed in PRs.

Quadrant Structure

Four quadrants covering the full technology landscape: Tools (CI, monitoring, editors), Platforms (cloud services, databases, brokers), Languages & Frameworks (programming languages, web frameworks, ORMs), Techniques (patterns, practices, methodologies). Each quadrant should have 5–15 items at any time.

Quarterly Review Session

A 30-minute session with the architecture guild and tech leads. Agenda: (1) review new technology proposals (max 5 per session), (2) review items that have changed status, (3) retire any items that are no longer relevant. Output: updated JSON committed to the repo. Next session scheduled immediately after this one.

Hold Rationale

Every Hold item must have a written rationale in the description field. "We moved to X because Y" or "This library is abandoned as of [date]." Without rationale, Hold entries are questioned and sometimes ignored. The rationale is the decision record for the Hold — treat it with the same rigour as an ADR.

Common pitfalls

Pitfall Why it happens What to do instead
Radar never updated after the initial population The radar is created with enthusiasm, published, and then never reviewed. After 12 months it is stale and nobody trusts it. The quarterly review was not scheduled. Schedule the quarterly review as a recurring calendar event on the day the radar is first published. The next review date appears on the radar page itself.
Too many items in Assess (laundry list radar) Engineers propose every interesting technology they read about. The Assess quadrant fills with 40 items; nobody has time to spike them all. The radar becomes a wish list, not a decision tool. Cap Assess at 5 items at any time. Promoting to Assess requires a committed spike owner and a due date. Items in Assess for more than 6 months without a spike are moved to Hold or retired.
Radar owned by one architect, not the team The CTO or lead architect populates the radar alone. Engineers feel no ownership. The radar reflects one person's preferences rather than the team's collective experience. Run the quarterly review as a group session. Any engineer can propose an item. Final ring placement is by guild consensus with the architect having a tiebreaker vote — not unilateral authority.
