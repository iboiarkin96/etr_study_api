How decisions are made — instruments, gates, ratification

The ADR and RFC corpora show what we decided; this page is the missing process layer — which instrument a decision routes to, which gates it must clear before «Accepted», and how a ratified decision is later revisited. Mechanics of statuses live in ADR 0018; step-by-step recipes live in the authoring handbook. This page holds the rules those pages assume.

Why this page exists

A decision corpus without a documented path to «Accepted» has ambiguous authority: a reader cannot tell a ratified decision from a well-formatted opinion. Top-tier engineering organisations treat the decision process as a first-class document — who proposes, what review it must survive, and when the record becomes immutable. Everything below existed here as tribal knowledge; now it is written down and citable in review.

Step 0 — pick the instrument

Most decision-hygiene failures are routing failures: a product rule frozen as an ADR, a one-line choice inflated into an RFC. Route first, write second.

The path to «Accepted» — gates, not vibes

An ADR moves from idea to ratified record through four gates. Skipping a gate is how a corpus fills with plausible-but-unchallenged decisions.

  1. Gate 1 — instrument check. The routing table above says an ADR is the right shape. If the draft grows «Component A / Component B / Migration plan» sections, it is an RFC — restart there.
  2. Gate 2 — substance. The draft names at least two real alternatives with rooted rejection reasons (tradeoff disclosure), and the Consequences section names a cost we accept, not only wins. An ADR whose alternatives section could be deleted without loss fails this gate.
  3. Gate 3 — adversarial pass after a cooling-off period. Minimum 48 hours between finishing the draft and ratifying it. Re-read the draft as the engineer who has to reverse this decision in a year: what would they wish the record had said? Anything more than one person is affected by goes to a tagged discussion Issue ([ADR] title) and waits for their response.
  4. Gate 4 — ratification. Merge PR linked, Ratification block filled (Issue, PR URL, acceptance date), data-adr-weight moved to Accepted — the mechanics of ADR 0018. From this moment the record is immutable.

RFC review contract

  • Open questions close before «Accepted». An RFC ratified with open questions is a draft wearing a badge. Each open question ends as a paragraph with an answer, a link to the ADR that ratified it, or an explicit «deferred, tracked in backlog item-N».
  • At least three industry references. Papers, vendor docs, or mature OSS projects that informed the design — an RFC that cites nothing was designed in a vacuum.
  • Review window scales with blast radius. A subsystem-shape RFC stays in «in review» for at least a week of calendar time before ratification, even when the author is confident — especially when the author is confident.

Revisiting a ratified decision

  • Never edit an accepted ADR. Supersession is two actions in one PR: write the new ADR and mark the old one Superseded with a link to its replacement. Doing only one of the two leaves the corpus lying in one direction or the other.
  • «Last reviewed» is a promise. Every ADR carries a last-reviewed date; a major architectural change (storage swap, new service, new deployment model) triggers a re-read of the ADRs it touches. A decision record nobody re-reads decays into folklore.
  • Contradiction is data. When reality contradicts a ratified ADR (a measurement, an incident, an integrator's usage), the contradiction routes back through this page: new ADR, supersede the old, cite the evidence.

Honest constraints — the solo-maintainer model

This portal currently has one regular maintainer. A review quorum of one is not a review — the gates above are the compensating controls: routing check, substance bar, and a mandatory cooling-off pass replace the second reviewer's fresh eyes with time-shifted ones.

The moment a second regular contributor joins, Gate 3 upgrades automatically: ADR ratification requires an approval from someone other than the author, and the cooling-off window becomes a floor, not the whole control.
