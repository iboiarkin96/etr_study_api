Portal operating model — top-level section with a self-serve hub

The portal grew too many moving parts to hold in one head — ADRs, templates, gates, workflows, paired-edit rules. This ADR adds a top-level operating-model/ section that gathers the rules in one place, plus two pre-commit checks that turn coupling-discipline from «remember this» into «the check refuses to forget».

Context

The portal has many pages and many rules, but no single place that joins them. To add a page, an author needs to know where it goes, what labels it must carry, which automated checks will run, and which other files have to change at the same time. Each of those is documented somewhere; together they are not. The result is that the operating model lives in one person's head, and new contributors can't start without asking.

The same shape has names in other companies: GitLab calls it «Way of Working», Stripe and Shopify call it «Operating Model», Backstage calls it a «TechDocs landing». What they share is one self-serve hub that synthesises the rules instead of re-explaining them.

Decision

Adopt a top-level Operating model section with ten router pages and two new pre-commit checks. The section is parallel to onboarding/ and handbook/, not nested under either.

  1. D1 — Top-level placement. The section lives at services/portal/internal/operating-model/. Not nested under handbook/, because the audience is wider than writers and the surface is meta — rules about the portal itself, not writing craft.
  2. D2 — Router, not replacement. Operating-model pages synthesise and link out. Deep narrative stays in the handbook; this section adds incoming links and the connective tissue.
  3. D3 — Ten pages, no more. Adding an eleventh requires a successor ADR. The page list is below.
  4. D4 — Five enforceable rules. The hub home declares five rules every page must follow (where it goes, what it declares, before-commit checks, copy-from-template, paired edits). Each rule is enforced by an automated check; deviations need a written exception.
  5. D5 — One pre-commit check closes the template-twin coupling loop. check_template_twins refuses one-sided edits to page templates that have a paired design example.
  6. D6 — Extend the HTML lint for required labels. Every page declares six labels on <body>; the lint asserts the three required ones (page-type, service, status) are present and inside the allowed list. Without this, Rule 4 above is convention, not check.
  7. D7 — The «what's new» feed is hand-authored. Each entry answers four questions: when, what changed, why, what changes for the reader. Auto-generation from commits loses the last column, which is the one that actually does work for the reader.

Scope

  • In scope: the ten pages, the two pre-commit checks, the HTML-lint extension, the sidebar entry, and a few cross-links from related pages (onboarding cold-start, SA process, authoring index).
  • Out of scope: rewriting existing handbook content. The operating-model section adds incoming links; the handbook stays where it is and keeps its own shape.
  • Out of scope: editing auto-generated catalog views. Those refresh from page metadata, not by hand.
  • Out of scope: coupling rules for non-portal artefacts (the API spec, deployment configs, etc.). The coupling map starts with what's enforceable today and grows one row at a time when a new pattern earns enforcement.

Alternatives considered

Nest the hub under handbook/sa/authoring/

rejected

The audience is wider than writers — engineers adding runbooks, testers adding checklists, SREs adding postmortems all need the routing tree and the gate map too. Hiding the section behind «SA · Authoring» drops discoverability sharply for non-writers. The hub is a front door, not a sub-page.

Auto-generate the «what's new» feed from git history

rejected

A commit log answers «what changed». It doesn't answer «why» or «what changes for you» — the two columns that make the feed useful. An autogen feed also weakens the meta-change check, whose whole job is to make the author write that last column.

Replace overlapping handbook pages with the hub

rejected

The handbook pages are deep narrative; the hub pages are routers. Different audiences, different shapes, different update cadences. Merging them collapses both. The router/deep-dive split is cleaner — each surface can evolve on its own clock.

Consequences

What we get

  • One entry point for «what are the rules and what's my next step».
  • Coupling loops (meta-change ↔ feed, template ↔ design example) move from memory rule to automated check. Adding a future coupling pattern is one row in the table and one line in the check.
  • The HTML-lint becomes real, which makes Rule 4 enforced rather than aspirational.
  • The handbook stays clean — the operating-model section points at it, doesn't rewrite it.

What it costs

  • The feed has to be written by hand. The check refuses commits to shared surfaces without a feed entry. We accept this because the throughput is low (a handful per month) and the forcing function is the whole point.
  • One more top-level sidebar node. Some readers will never need the hub once they know the system. We accept this because onboarding hubs are exactly what top-level nodes are for.
  • Coupling checks run on commit, not in the cloud build. Pre-commit catches drift at the moment it's cheapest to fix; the existing PR review is the backstop if someone bypassed the local hook.

Compatibility and migration

Additive change — no existing page moves out of the handbook, no existing nav entry is renamed. The operating-model section is a new top-level node parallel to onboarding/ and handbook/; pages that point into the handbook continue to resolve.

  • Backwards-compatible URLs. No existing handbook URL is broken by this ADR. The operating-model pages add incoming links to the handbook, not the other way round.
  • Pre-commit gate opt-out. The two new checks can be skipped locally with SKIP=check-meta-changes-logged,check-template-twins git commit for emergency fixes; the cloud build catches the same coupling on PR.
  • Rollback strategy. Removing the operating-model/ folder, the nav-tree node, and the two pre-commit hooks reverts the section to a clean state — the handbook is untouched. The HTML-lint extension is independent and stays.

Implementation

Shipped. The ten pages, two pre-commit checks, and the HTML-lint extension are live as of 2026-06-13. Per-page tone, cross-links, and discoverability fixes continue as ongoing work; structural changes to the section would need a successor ADR.

Validation

The decision is validated when a new author opens the hub and reaches «their next step» in three minutes or less, without asking another engineer where to look. Concrete signals to watch:

  • Routing-tree first-touch. A new page lands in the right folder on the first try (no relocation commit in PR review). Tracked anecdotally during the next four onboarding sessions.
  • Coupling-gate fires before push. Both pre-commit checks (check-meta-changes-logged, check-template-twins) fail at least once during normal authoring within the first month — if neither ever trips, either nothing coupled changed or the gate is misconfigured.
  • Section trims itself. If two adjacent operating-model pages duplicate content for more than a week, the section is overgrown — merge or delete. Three sub-pages were retired on 2026-06-13 against this signal.

Updates

2026-06-13 — Three refinements in one day. The page list was first trimmed (the process-glossary was retired into the pages where its terms are actually used), then expanded twice (three orphan handbook pages — the IA contract, the domain model, and cross-service workflows — moved out of handbook/sa/ and into the operating-model section, where they belong; a fourth page, reader-personas, moved later the same day for the same reason). D3 went from seven to six to nine to ten pages. The decision itself didn't change — each was a refinement of where the boundary sits, not a reversal.

The hub home also gained a «three-minute mental model» section with two diagrams and three short paragraphs, and the lifecycle page replaced its bulleted list with a visual timeline. Both motivated by the same idea: if the hub can't be explained in three minutes, the model isn't in the reader's head.
