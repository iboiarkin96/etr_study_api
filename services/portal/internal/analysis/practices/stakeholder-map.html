Stakeholder map · personas · JTBD

A list of stakeholders, three or four user personas, JTBD statements, and a Power-Interest grid — refreshed every quarter.

TL;DR

A product designed for 'everyone' is designed for no one. Personas keep the docs and the UI honest about the real reader. The stakeholder map keeps communications targeted instead of broadcast to the whole company every time something ships.

What it is

Three small artefacts that travel together:

  • Stakeholder list. Internal and external parties with a real interest in the project. Role, goal, influence, information needs.
  • Personas. Three or four named user archetypes. Each has a one-paragraph profile and one or two Jobs To Be Done (JTBD) statements.
  • Power-Interest grid. Two-by-two grid that tells the team how much attention each stakeholder needs (manage closely, keep informed, keep satisfied, monitor).

Why we need it

Decisions made without personas tend to drift towards the loudest voice in the room. A persona is a stand-in for an absent reader — the doc author asks 'would Lina the integrator follow this?' and the wording sharpens.

The stakeholder map saves the team from over-communicating. Not every stakeholder needs every release note. The map tells you who needs a heads-up and who is fine reading the changelog quarterly.

How others do it

Short tour of how teams known for the practice apply it. Each card is one company, what they do, and what we take from it.

Intercom

Public 'Jobs To Be Done' evangelism; their book 'Intercom on Jobs-to-be-Done' is a free reference for the JTBD format.

What we take: JTBD statements force precision. 'Lina wants better docs' is wishful; 'when Lina debugs a webhook, she wants the example payload' is actionable.

Source: Intercom on Jobs-to-be-Done (free book)

Bain & Company

Refined the JTBD framework into 30 'fundamental jobs' that any product can be mapped against.

What we take: A small fixed list of job archetypes prevents teams from inventing a new persona for every feature.

Source: Bain — The Elements of Value

Atlassian

Public 'Personas' template in the Confluence and Jira product documentation; widely copied in agile teams.

What we take: A persona that fits on one card (name + photo + 3 frustrations + 1 JTBD) is more used than a 4-page persona doc.

Source: Atlassian — Personas template

The repeated lesson: small personas (one card), tight JTBD statements ('when…I want…so that…'), and a Power-Interest grid for stakeholders. Big persona decks are a vanity exercise.

Anatomy

The parts a complete practice has, with one sentence on each. None of these are optional.

Persona name

One short name. 'Lina, the integrator' is more memorable than 'persona 3'.

Goal

One sentence on what the persona wants from the system.

JTBD

Job-To-Be-Done statement. 'When X, I want Y, so that Z.'

Frustrations

Two or three friction points. The doc/UI design responds to these.

Stakeholder

Distinct from persona. Sponsors, regulators, downstream teams, customer success.

Power × Interest

The 2×2 grid. Drives the cadence and depth of communication.

How it works in this project

The map will live at internal/analysis/stakeholders.html. The source is small YAML (stakeholders.yaml + personas.yaml); CI renders the HTML grid and the persona cards.

Personas appear in two more places: at the top of every Design Doc (the author names the personas the doc serves) and in the audit reports. This stops personas from becoming a slide nobody reads.

Deep dive — the theory you need

The Power-Interest grid (Mendelow)

Aubrey Mendelow's 2×2 grid is the most-used stakeholder framework. Two axes:

  • Power — how much the stakeholder can affect the project (vetoes, budget, sign-off).
  • Interest — how much the stakeholder cares about the project's outcome.

Four quadrants, four communication strategies:

Low interestHigh interest
High powerKeep satisfied — brief on outcomesManage closely — full involvement
Low powerMonitor — newsletter, no actionKeep informed — regular updates

JTBD format — 'When/I want/so that'

The canonical JTBD statement has three parts:

When [situation],
I want to [motivation],
so that I can [expected outcome].

Example: When Lina hits an error response from our API, she wants the error code, the offending field, and an example fix, so that she can resolve it without opening a support ticket.

The When clause is the most-skipped and the most-important — it grounds the job in a real situation. A JTBD without a When is a feature wishlist.

Trade-offs — what this practice costs

  • Personas without research are fiction. A persona invented in a workshop is worse than no persona because the team trusts it. Either back personas with user interviews (5–8 per persona) or label them 'hypotheses'.
  • Quarterly refresh demands time. Walking 4 personas + 8 stakeholders is a 90-minute session. Couple with another quarterly ritual (risk register, postmortem-AI review) to keep the calendar load reasonable.
  • One persona doesn't fit all features. 'Lina the integrator' may not care about the in-app feed feature; the feature owner needs a different persona. Persona-per-feature is too much; persona-per-product-surface is usually right.

Lifecycle

draft → accepted → cited in design docs → refreshed quarterly

Personas start as a Design Doc that argues for the three or four archetypes. Once accepted, every new Design Doc cites at least one persona. Quarterly review tunes the personas — adds frustrations the team has heard, removes ones that turned out wrong.

Common pitfalls

PitfallWhy it happensWhat to do instead
Personas are decorativeSlides at the kickoff, never seen again.Personas are referenced via data-persona-id='lina' in Design Docs; CI counts citations per persona per quarter and reports a dashboard.
Too many personasEight personas means no real focus.Cap at 4 personas per product surface; if you need a fifth, the team is likely combining two product surfaces — split the discussion before adding.
JTBD that's a wish'Lina wants better docs.'Real JTBD: 'When Lina debugs a webhook, she wants the example payload, so she can reproduce it locally in 60 seconds.'
Stakeholder map is alphabeticalNo prioritisation; team treats them all the same.Place the Power-Interest grid at the top of stakeholders.html; write the comms cadence (weekly / monthly / quarterly) next to each tier.

Worked example

Persona card

Lina, the integrator

Role. Senior backend engineer at a partner; builds an integration against our API.

Goal. Ship a working integration in under two days; minimal back-and-forth with our team.

JTBD. When Lina hits an error response, she wants the error code, the field that caused it, and an example fix, so that she can resolve it without opening a support ticket.

Frustrations.

  • Errors that say 'something went wrong' without a code.
  • Docs that don't show example payloads.
  • Auth flows where the first 401 is the only feedback.

Design implication. Every public error has a stable code, a message, and a link to the matching doc page. The auth flow has a debug endpoint that returns why a token was rejected.

Outcome. Every new Design Doc cites at least one persona via the data-persona-id tag; reviewers reject docs that don't; a citation-count dashboard per persona surfaces which personas are actually shaping decisions and which are decorative.
