Reliability Tiering — not every service deserves a pager

A formal tier system maps every service to its business criticality, drives proportional SLO targets, DR requirements, and on-call response, and prevents both gold-plating internal tools and under-resourcing critical paths.

TL;DR

Reliability tiering classifies every service into 3–4 tiers (Tier 0 = business-critical; Tier 1 = important; Tier 2 = best-effort) with explicit per-tier commitments: SLO target, maintenance window, DR RTO/RPO, on-call response time, and alerting routing. The tier drives the investment — a Tier 0 service gets multi-zone redundancy and a 5-minute pager response; a Tier 2 service gets best-effort infra and a Slack notification. Tiering makes the trade-off explicit and non-negotiable.

What it is

Reliability tiering is the practice of formally classifying every service into a small number of tiers based on business impact, and then applying per-tier standards for:

  • SLO target: The reliability percentage the team commits to maintaining.
  • On-call response time: How quickly an on-call engineer must acknowledge a page — ranging from 5 minutes for Tier 0 to next business day for Tier 2.
  • Maintenance windows: When the service is allowed to be taken offline for maintenance, and how much notice is required.
  • DR requirements: Recovery Time Objective (RTO) and Recovery Point Objective (RPO) for the service in a disaster scenario.
  • Alerting routing: Where alerts go — PagerDuty for Tier 0, email for Tier 1, Slack for Tier 2.

The tier registry lives in the service catalog and is updated when a service's business criticality changes.

Why we need it

Without tiering, every service is implicitly treated as equally critical. The result: critical services are under-resourced because the team is spending DR budget on internal tools, and non-critical services are over-engineered for reliability that the business doesn't need or pay for.

Tiering also clarifies the paging decision. The question "should this alert page someone at 3 am?" is hard to answer without a tier. With a tier, the answer is procedural: Tier 0 alerts page; Tier 2 alerts go to Slack.

How others do it

PagerDuty

PagerDuty's own internal tier system uses four tiers: P0 (mission-critical — their core paging product), P1 (important — integrations and mobile app), P2 (supporting — internal analytics), P3 (best-effort — internal tools). Tier assignments are reviewed by product and engineering annually and whenever a service's business criticality changes materially.

What we take: Tier assignment is a product decision with engineering input — not a pure engineering decision. Product knows which services generate the most revenue or support the most critical customer journeys; engineering knows the technical cost of higher reliability commitments. Both inputs are required.

Source: PagerDuty — Service Tiers

Netflix

Netflix uses a two-tier approach with a hard distinction between "critical path" (services that directly affect stream start) and "non-critical path" (everything else). Critical-path services receive engineering investment proportional to their blast radius: redundancy across 3 AWS regions, automatic failover, and chaos engineering testing every month. Non-critical-path services are treated as best-effort.

What we take: The simplest effective tier system is two tiers — critical path and non-critical path. More granularity is useful at scale; at smaller scale, a two-tier system is easier to maintain and harder to game.

Source: Netflix Tech Blog

Anatomy

Tier definition document

Written criteria for each tier: revenue impact, customer-facing exposure, number of dependent services. Criteria must be objective enough that two engineers independently assign the same tier to the same service at least 90% of the time.

Per-tier standards table

One table: tier vs. SLO target, response time, maintenance window, RTO, RPO, alerting routing. Published in the service catalog. No exceptions without an escalation.

Tier registry in service catalog

Every service has an assigned tier, a named engineering owner, a last-review date, and a tier justification (one sentence). Updated when a service launches or its criticality changes.

Annual tier review

Review all tier assignments annually with product. Services grow, their criticality changes, and a Tier 2 service may have become Tier 0 through 12 months of product decisions. Stale tier assignments are more dangerous than no tier system at all.

Common pitfalls

PitfallWhy it happensWhat to do instead
Everything is Tier 0Every team wants their service treated as critical.Set a capacity limit: no more than 20% of services can be Tier 0. Scarcity forces honest prioritisation. If every service is critical, none is.
Tier assigned, standards not enforcedThe tier registry exists but alerting routing still goes to PagerDuty for everything.Automate tier enforcement: alerting routing, maintenance window enforcement, and DR drill scheduling should all read from the tier field in the service catalog.
Tier never reviewed after assignmentInitial tier was assigned at service launch; nothing triggers a review.Add tier review to the annual architecture review cadence. When a service's revenue or user dependency changes significantly, trigger an ad hoc review.
