Game Days & DR — practise failure before failure practices you

Scheduled, controlled failure injection and disaster recovery exercises that validate runbooks, on-call procedures, and system assumptions — before a real incident reveals that the DR plan has never actually worked.

TL;DR

A game day is a scheduled exercise where the team intentionally injects a failure into a service (or simulates one via tabletop), executes the runbook, measures the response, and produces a written report of gaps found. Distinct from chaos engineering (which is automated and continuous), game days are human-run, scenario-based exercises that validate people and process as much as they validate systems. DR (disaster recovery) exercises specifically test the recovery procedure for catastrophic failures: regional outage, data corruption, key compromise. Target cadence: quarterly game days for Tier 0/1 services; annual DR test for all services. Every game day produces at least one runbook update or system improvement. A game day with no findings means the scenario was not challenging enough.

What it is

A game day is a scheduled, controlled exercise where the engineering team intentionally injects a failure scenario — or simulates one via tabletop — and executes the response procedure as if it were a real incident. Unlike automated chaos engineering (which runs continuously against production or staging), game days are human-run, scenario-based exercises designed to test the people and process as much as the system.

Three formats:

  • Tabletop exercise: A facilitator describes a scenario ("the primary database has failed over to the replica and the replica is degraded"). Team members narrate what they would do, what they would check, who they would call. The facilitator injects complications. No systems are touched — the exercise is purely about decision-making and process. Lowest blast radius, highest process coverage. Good for validating incident command and communication procedures.
  • Staged injection: A failure is injected into a staging environment (or a pre-production canary) and the team executes the full response procedure using real tools. The team doesn't know the injection scenario in advance — they respond as if it were production. Tests runbooks, tooling, and team coordination. Moderate blast radius, high technical coverage.
  • DR exercise: The team validates the disaster recovery procedure for a catastrophic failure: regional failover, backup restoration from a specific date, key rotation under incident conditions. These are production-impacting by definition — they are scheduled during low-traffic windows with customer communication. High blast radius; validates that the DR plan actually works.

Why we need it

DR plans that are never tested are fiction. Every untested assumption in a DR plan is a potential 2-hour extension of a real disaster. Common discoveries in first DR tests: the backup restore procedure takes 4 hours, not 45 minutes as documented; the credentials for the backup system expired 6 months ago; the failover procedure assumes a service that was deprecated; the on-call who wrote the runbook left the company. Game days find these gaps when the stakes are controlled, not during a real P0.

Game days also build muscle memory. Teams who have executed a rollback under game day conditions execute it faster and with less decision overhead during real incidents. The cognitive cost of "what am I supposed to do here?" is eliminated when the procedure has been practiced.

How others do it

Amazon Web Services

Amazon's internal game day practice, which seeded AWS's "operational excellence" principle, uses a "wheel of misfortune" for tabletop exercises — a random scenario generator that produces failure combinations the team has not explicitly planned for. The randomisation prevents the "we've already trained for this" bias that makes planned scenarios less valuable. Their game day rule: the facilitator is an SRE who does not belong to the team — external facilitation prevents the team from unconsciously steering toward scenarios they know they can handle.

What we take: External facilitation. A game day run by the team that designed the system is a demonstration, not a test. The facilitator should be someone who doesn't know the system well enough to steer toward safe scenarios.

Source: Amazon Builders' Library — Fire Drills

Slack

Slack runs "Failure Friday" — a weekly scheduled session where a random team runs a 30-minute tabletop game day, facilitated by an SRE. The scenarios are not random — they are drawn from a backlog of "what-if" questions submitted by engineers. The backlog drives the scenario selection. After each session, the findings are published in #game-days and the runbook updates are merged within 48 hours. The weekly cadence means every team participates 2–3 times per year without the overhead of scheduling a special event.

What we take: Make game days low-overhead and high-frequency rather than high-overhead and quarterly. A 30-minute tabletop run weekly beats a half-day exercise run once a year in terms of total coverage and team participation.

Anatomy

Scenario design

Scenarios are drawn from three sources: past incidents (replay and test the improved procedure), near-misses (events that didn't cause an outage but revealed a weakness), and hypothetical worst-cases based on the service's dependency graph. Each scenario has a stated hypothesis ("we believe our on-call can detect and remediate a database primary failure within 15 minutes") and a falsifying condition ("detection took more than 5 minutes" or "the runbook step 3 failed").

Blast radius control

Staged injection scenarios must have an explicit blast radius scope: which services are affected, which environments, and the abort procedure that stops the injection immediately if something unexpected happens. No game day injection should risk customer data or production SLO for more than 5 minutes. For Tier 0 services, injections require engineering lead sign-off and are never run during peak traffic hours.

Findings documentation

Every game day produces a written report within 48 hours: the scenario, the hypothesis, the findings (what worked, what didn't), and the resulting action items (runbook updates, system improvements, monitoring gaps). Published to the same channel as postmortems. The report is the artifact — a game day with no report doesn't count.

DR exercise schedule

Annual DR test for every service: restore from the most recent backup, validate the restore, run the failover procedure. Tier 0 services: bi-annual DR test. DR test results are submitted to the engineering lead as part of the annual reliability review. Services that have not run a DR test in 18 months are flagged for compliance review.

Common pitfalls

PitfallWhy it happensWhat to do instead
Game day with no findings treated as success"The system handled the scenario perfectly."A game day with no findings means the scenario was not challenging enough. Increase scenario difficulty until findings appear. The goal is not to pass — it is to discover gaps before production does. A "passed" game day is a wasted exercise unless the team has evidence that they tested their actual failure modes.
DR plan validated by documentation review, not execution"We reviewed the DR plan last year and it looked correct."Execute the DR plan annually. Reading a plan and executing it are different activities. Restore from backup. Failover the primary. Measure the actual RTO. Every gap that appears during execution is a gap that would have appeared during a real disaster, at 3× the pressure.
Game days run only by senior engineers"The junior engineers won't know what to do."Junior engineers are the highest-value participants in a game day — they reveal the tribal knowledge that is not written down. Include them, observe where they get stuck, and update the runbooks to eliminate the need for prior knowledge. A runbook that requires a senior engineer to execute is not a runbook.
