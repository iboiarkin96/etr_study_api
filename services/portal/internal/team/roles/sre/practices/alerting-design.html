Alerting Design — fewer alerts, each one actionable

Alert on symptoms, not causes. Every alert has a runbook, a severity, and an owner. Alert noise is a first-class metric — alert fatigue kills on-call effectiveness faster than alert gaps.

TL;DR

Good alerting design follows three rules: alert on symptoms (SLO burn rate, user-visible error rate) not causes (CPU at 90%, disk at 80%); every alert has a runbook link, a named owner, and a documented response time; alert quality is measured by pages per engineer per week and false-positive rate. Google's target: every alert is actionable within 5 minutes. Target fewer than 5 pages per on-call engineer per shift. Any alert that fires and requires no action should be deleted or demoted to a notification.

What it is

Alerting design is the principled approach to deciding which conditions should wake someone up, which should create a ticket, and which should only appear on a dashboard. A well-designed alert has five mandatory properties:

  • Symptom-based: It fires on user-visible impact (high error rate, SLO budget burning fast) — not on internal resource usage (CPU at 90%, disk at 80%). CPU at 90% might be fine; users returning 5xx is never fine.
  • Actionable: The on-call engineer knows exactly what to do when it fires. If the response is "wait and see," it's not a page — it's a dashboard annotation.
  • Documented: Every alert has a runbook link in its annotation. The runbook is the first thing the on-call opens — it should be there before the engineer needs it.
  • Owned: A named service and team is responsible for the alert. Ownerless alerts are silenced and forgotten.
  • Reviewed: Alert rules are reviewed quarterly for accuracy — false positive rate, actionability, and continued relevance.

Why we need it

Alert fatigue is the most common on-call pathology. When engineers acknowledge 60 alerts per shift and most are noise, they stop treating alerts as signals. The psychological term is "alarm fatigue" — the same phenomenon documented in ICU nursing. When the one real alert fires, it gets acknowledged and silenced with the same muscle memory as the 59 noisy ones before it.

Fewer alerts, each meaningful, each actionable — this is the engineering target. Google's internal target is that every alert be actionable within 5 minutes. If it can't be resolved in 5 minutes, the on-call can't close the alert before the next one fires, creating an alert queue that degrades shift quality.

How others do it

Google SRE (burn-rate alerts)

Google's SRE Workbook specifies multi-window, multi-burn-rate alerts as the correct approach for SLO-based alerting. The algorithm: alert fires when both the 1-hour burn rate exceeds 14.4× and the 6-hour burn rate exceeds 6×. The two-window approach catches both fast and slow budget depletion while eliminating false positives from transient spikes that the single-window approach fires on.

What we take: Stop writing threshold alerts on raw metrics. Adopt multi-window burn-rate alerts for every SLO. The precision improvement — fewer false positives, earlier real warnings — is significant enough to justify the migration effort.

Source: Google SRE Workbook — Alerting on SLOs

PagerDuty

PagerDuty's "Operational Maturity Model" defines alert quality metrics: mean time to acknowledge (MTTA), mean time to resolve (MTTR), and "noise ratio" (alerts that require no action / total alerts). Their recommendation: any team with a noise ratio above 30% should run an alert audit before adding new alerts — new alerts on a noisy foundation make the signal-to-noise ratio worse, not better.

What we take: Measure alert quality explicitly. Pages per engineer per shift, MTTA, and noise ratio. Review these metrics monthly. A team that doesn't measure alert quality doesn't know whether its alert improvements are working.

Source: PagerDuty Incident Response Guide

Anatomy

Alert audit

Classify every existing alert as "actionable", "informational", or "noise." Delete noise. Demote informational to dashboard annotations. The audit takes one half-day sprint for a team with 50 alerts and immediately reduces on-call noise.

Runbook link requirement

Every alert rule must have a runbook URL in its annotations before it's merged to the alert repository. CI blocks alert PRs without a runbook link. "Runbook: TODO" is treated the same as no runbook — the alert doesn't ship until the runbook exists.

Alert quality dashboard

Pages per engineer per week, MTTA (mean time to acknowledge), MTTR (mean time to resolve), and noise ratio. Reviewed in the monthly SRE retrospective. Target: under 5 pages per on-call engineer per shift.

Quarterly alert review

Review every alert against its noise ratio over the past quarter. Any alert with a false-positive rate above 20% is either retuned or deleted. New alerts are only added after the team's noise ratio is below the target threshold.

Common pitfalls

PitfallWhy it happensWhat to do instead
Alerting on causes (CPU, disk) instead of symptomsInfrastructure metrics are easy to instrument; user-visible symptoms require SLO definition first.Alert on the four golden signals and SLO burn rate. CPU at 90% is not a reason to page unless it's also causing latency degradation — and if latency is degrading, the latency alert fires.
Alert added, never reviewedAdding alerts is easy; removing them feels risky.Every alert has a "review by" date set when it's created. After 90 days without a genuine trigger, it is automatically deleted unless explicitly renewed. Entropy favours alert accumulation; the process must actively fight it.
No owner on an alert"The platform team owns all alerts" — no specific person.Every alert has a named service and a named team in PagerDuty. Escalation policy routes to the right on-call. An alert without an owner is an alert that will be silenced when it becomes inconvenient.
