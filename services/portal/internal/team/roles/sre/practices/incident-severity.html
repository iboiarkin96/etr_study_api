Incident Severity Framework — the response must be proportional to the impact

A shared classification system that maps customer and business impact to severity levels, driving proportional response: who is notified, how fast, through which channels, and what escalation path applies.

TL;DR

An incident severity framework maps 4 severity levels (P0–P3) to explicit qualifying criteria (customer impact, scope, revenue effect) and per-severity response SLAs: acknowledgement time, notification targets, communication frequency, and postmortem requirement. Without a shared framework, every engineer classifies incidents differently — P0-level impact sometimes gets a Slack message; minor internal degradation sometimes triggers 3 am pages. Shared criteria make the response proportional, predictable, and analysable across incidents over time.

What it is

An incident severity framework is a classification system that maps incidents to severity levels based on objective criteria. Each level specifies:

  • Qualifying criteria: The specific conditions that put an incident at this severity — customer scope (all users / 20% of users / internal only), business impact (complete service outage / major feature degraded / minor degradation), and revenue effect.
  • Response SLA: Maximum time to acknowledge the page at this severity level.
  • Notification targets: Who gets notified — on-call only, team lead, VP, CEO.
  • Communication frequency: How often status updates go out during the incident — every 15 minutes for P0, every 30 minutes for P1.
  • Postmortem requirement: P0 and P1 always require a postmortem. P2 and P3 are optional.

Why we need it

Without a shared framework, severity classification is personal. The engineer who classifies every problem as P0 generates executive panic about minor issues; the engineer who never classifies anything above P2 lets serious customer-facing issues go unescalated for hours. Both patterns are harmful — and both occur in organisations without explicit criteria.

Shared criteria also enable post-incident analysis. "We had 12 P1 incidents this quarter, 8 from the same root cause" is a meaningful pattern that drives engineering investment. Without consistent classification, the data is too noisy to aggregate.

How others do it

PagerDuty

PagerDuty's incident response guide defines severity levels with explicit customer impact language: SEV-1 = "critical issue affecting many customers"; SEV-2 = "major functionality broken for subset of customers"; SEV-3 = "minor issue with workaround"; SEV-4 = "cosmetic, no user impact." Their calibration exercise: quarterly meeting where engineers independently classify 10 historical incidents and compare results. Disagreements reveal ambiguities in the criteria that need clarification.

What we take: Run calibration exercises quarterly — not just write the criteria and assume everyone interprets them the same way. Calibration is how you discover that "significant revenue loss" means different things to different people, and why the P0 criterion needs a specific dollar threshold.

Source: PagerDuty Severity Levels

Atlassian

Atlassian uses a "customer impact" first approach to severity classification — the primary criterion is always "how many customers are affected and how severely?" Internal engineering impact (a degraded build pipeline, a slow internal tool) can only reach P2 at most, regardless of engineering discomfort. The rule prevents the "this is a P1 because it's embarrassing for us" classification that inflates severity for incidents with no customer impact.

What we take: Customer impact is the primary axis. A Tier 0 engineering system that's slightly slow but has no customer-visible effect is not a P0. Severity frameworks that allow internal engineering embarrassment to drive high severity classifications lose credibility with customers and executives quickly.

Source: Atlassian — Incident Severity Levels

Anatomy

Customer impact criteria

Primary classification axis. Three dimensions: scope (% of users affected), depth (complete failure vs. degraded vs. minor), and duration (how long has it been occurring). All three must be assessable within 5 minutes of the incident start without additional investigation.

Response SLA per severity

P0: 5-minute ACK; P1: 30-minute ACK; P2: next business day; P3: sprint backlog. SLAs are tracked and reported monthly — an unacknowledged P0 that breaches the 5-minute SLA is itself an incident to investigate.

Escalation matrix per severity

P0: on-call + engineering lead + VP + CEO briefed within 15 minutes. P1: on-call + engineering lead. P2: on-call only. The escalation path is automatic based on severity — the on-call engineer doesn't decide whether to escalate to the VP; the severity level decides it.

Calibration exercises

Quarterly 30-minute meeting: present 5–10 historical incidents, have all engineers classify them independently, compare and discuss disagreements. Disagreements identify ambiguous criteria that need sharper language.

Common pitfalls

PitfallWhy it happensWhat to do instead
Severity inflation ("everything is a P1")Engineers prefer high severity to justify urgency.Track severity distribution over time. If 60% of incidents are P1, the criteria are wrong. A healthy distribution is roughly 5% P0, 15% P1, 50% P2, 30% P3. Deviation from this suggests calibration drift.
Severity downgraded retroactively"It turned out to be minor, so we'll call it P3."Severity is classified based on the information available at declaration time. Never reclassify downward after resolution — this distorts the historical data and erodes engineer accountability for escalation decisions.
Framework exists, but engineers still free-classifyThe framework is in a wiki; it's not integrated into the incident workflow.Integrate severity classification into the incident declaration form (PagerDuty, Jira). The engineer must select a severity from a dropdown — the form shows the criteria for each level. Classification is not optional.
