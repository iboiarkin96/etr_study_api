Architecture Review — structured change governance

A lightweight, async-first review of significant changes that catches global problems while preserving team autonomy.

TL;DR

Architecture Review is a lightweight governance process — an Architecture Review Board (ARB) or design review forum — that evaluates significant changes before they reach production. It is not a heavyweight gate but an async-first review with a defined scope (changes crossing service boundaries, new dependencies, data model changes), a checklist, and a 48-hour SLA. The goal is to catch global problems (security gaps, cross-team coupling, data model conflicts) while preserving team autonomy for local decisions. Done well, the review catches issues in the design phase when they cost minutes to fix.

What it is

Architecture Review is a structured forum for evaluating significant design changes before they reach production. Three components define a working review process:

  • Scope checklist — defines when a review is required. Typical triggers: introduces a new shared dependency, changes a service-to-service contract, modifies the data model in a way that affects another team, introduces a new programming language or runtime, or has expected operational cost above $1k/month.
  • Reviewer group — a rotating group of 3–5 senior engineers and the architect. For high-impact changes, security and SRE representatives are included. The reviewer pool is published; engineers know who to expect feedback from.
  • SLA — published response times. Typical: 48 hours for first feedback, 5 days for a decision. Without an SLA the review becomes a black hole that engineers learn to avoid.

The review input is a 2-page design doc plus a checklist (security, observability, data privacy, cost, NFRs). The output is either Approve, Approve-with-changes, or Revise. Reject is rare and reserved for proposals that fail a non-negotiable constraint.

Why we need it

Without a review process, teams make autonomous decisions that look locally correct but create global problems. Two teams add the same dependency in different versions. Three teams choose different approaches to data partitioning that prevent future migrations. Security gaps that would be obvious to a security reviewer are merged because no security reviewer was involved. These issues are caught months later when the cost to fix is 10–100× higher.

A lightweight review process — emphasis on lightweight — catches these global issues at design time. The 48-hour async SLA means engineers do not wait days for feedback. The defined scope means most local decisions skip review entirely. The result is faster decisions for local work and better decisions for cross-cutting work.

How others do it

Netflix

Netflix's approach is "freedom and responsibility" — there is no central architecture board. Instead, principal engineers across the company act as informal reviewers for major decisions in their domain. The Architecture Council reviews only the highest-impact decisions: new platform investments, organisational-scale technology choices, and changes to the developer experience. Netflix's tech blog has documented this approach in multiple posts on "Full Cycle Development" and "Paved Roads."

What we take: Distinguish between team-local decisions (no review needed) and cross-cutting decisions (review required). The reviewer for a service-level change is the team's principal engineer, not a central board.

Source: Netflix Tech Blog — Full Cycle Developers

Google

Google uses "readability" review for code patterns and "design review" for architecture. Design reviews are async-first, conducted on the design doc PR. Reviewers are explicitly named in the doc. For cross-team designs, an "architecture review" with senior staff engineers is convened. Google's process has been documented externally in the SRE book and in talks: review is a quality function, not a gate, and reviewers are accountable for response time.

What we take: Name reviewers explicitly in the design doc, not generically ("the team"). Named reviewers respond; unnamed reviewers assume someone else will respond.

Source: Google SRE Book

Atlassian

Atlassian operates an architecture review process called "Tech Spec Review" with a published 48-hour SLA and a public reviewer roster. Atlassian's blog has documented the process: each Tech Spec has a stated "impact radius" (team / department / company) and is reviewed by reviewers from that scope. For company-scope specs, the Atlassian Engineering Leadership Team reviews. The 48-hour SLA is tracked as a team metric; reviewer responsiveness is part of the senior engineer performance signal.

What we take: Make reviewer responsiveness a measurable team metric. SLAs without measurement degrade to "best effort" and lose their value as a forcing function.

Source: Atlassian Engineering blog

Anatomy

Scope Trigger Checklist

A checklist that engineers run against their change to determine whether review is needed. Triggers: shared dependency change, cross-team contract change, data model change affecting another team, new language/runtime, > $1k/month operational cost, or security-sensitive change. Below the trigger: no review needed.

Design Doc Input

A 2-page document (max) submitted as a PR. Required sections: problem, proposed solution, alternatives, security considerations, operational cost, rollout plan. Reviewers comment inline on the PR. Conversations are public, not in DMs. The review trail is auditable.

Reviewer Roster

A published list of 3–5 senior engineers plus rotating subject-matter experts (security, SRE, data). The architect is always a reviewer. Each design doc lists the named reviewers from the roster. Reviewer assignment is transparent — no "I assumed someone else would review."

SLA Commitment

48 hours for first feedback, 5 days for decision. SLAs are public and tracked. Reviewers who consistently miss the SLA are rotated off the roster. Authors whose proposals consistently fail the checklist get coaching, not blame — the checklist is a learning tool, not a gate.

Decision Outcomes

Approve (no changes needed), Approve-with-changes (specific edits required but no second review), Revise (substantive changes, second review needed), Reject (rare — only for proposals violating non-negotiable constraints like security or compliance). The outcome is recorded in the design doc PR.

Common pitfalls

Pitfall Why it happens What to do instead
Review process applied to every change ("review theatre") Teams over-apply the scope. Every change goes through review regardless of impact. The reviewer queue grows, the SLA slips, and engineers route around the process. Publish the scope checklist explicitly. Most changes should NOT trigger review. If > 30% of changes trigger review, the scope is too broad — narrow it.
Review becomes a heavyweight gate (slow decisions) What started as a 48-hour async review evolves into a weekly committee meeting with 10 attendees. Decisions take 3 weeks instead of 3 days. Engineers learn to avoid the process. Defend the SLA aggressively. Async-first by default; sync meetings only when async cannot resolve. Cap reviewer count at 5. If a meeting is needed, time-box it to 30 minutes.
Reviewers approve everything to be agreeable Senior engineers feel social pressure to approve their colleagues' proposals. Critical concerns are softened or skipped. The review becomes performative. Define "non-negotiable" constraints (security, data privacy, compliance) that reviewers must enforce. Train reviewers to give specific, direct feedback. Make it culturally acceptable to ask "have you considered X?" even when the answer might be uncomfortable.
