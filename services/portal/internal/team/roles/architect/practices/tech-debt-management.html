Tech Debt Management — visible and prioritised

Catalogue, quantify, and prioritise technical liabilities so the team can make informed trade-offs instead of paying interest invisibly.

TL;DR

Tech Debt Management is the practice of cataloguing, quantifying, and prioritising technical liabilities in a structured register. Ward Cunningham coined the term in 1992 to explain quick-and-dirty code to non-engineers — shipping fast incurs "debt" that must later be paid back with "interest" in the form of slower future development. Each debt item has an owner, an interest rate (days/sprint of slowdown), and a payoff cost. Reviewed monthly with engineering leadership. Invisible tech debt slows every feature; visible debt becomes a decision the team makes consciously.

What it is

Tech Debt Management is the discipline of treating technical liabilities as a structured backlog rather than as a vague complaint. The register is a shared list of debt items, each captured with five attributes:

  • Description — what is the debt? Specific enough that someone new to the codebase can understand the problem.
  • Interest rate — how much does this debt cost per sprint in slowdown? Measured in engineer-days of delay caused by working around the debt. Estimate based on team observation, not exact measurement.
  • Payoff cost — how many engineer-days to fully resolve? Includes implementation, testing, and rollout.
  • Owner — the team or engineer accountable for tracking and (eventually) paying off this debt. Without an owner, debt items go stale.
  • Priority — derived from interest-to-payoff ratio. High-interest, low-payoff items are the quickest wins.

The register is reviewed monthly with engineering leadership. A target of 15–20% of sprint capacity is allocated to debt payoff — enough to make steady progress without blocking feature delivery.

Why we need it

Invisible tech debt slows every feature. A team that spends 30% of every feature's time working around a broken auth abstraction is paying interest constantly — but nobody knows because the interest is amortised across all work. When the architect says "we should refactor auth," it sounds like discretionary cleanup. When the architect says "the broken auth abstraction costs us 3 engineer-days per sprint across the team — that is 36 days per year, which pays back the 20-day fix in two months," it becomes a business case.

The register also creates accountability. Without it, every senior engineer has a mental list of debt items and complains about a different subset. With it, the team's worst debts are visible to product leadership and prioritised against features in a transparent forum. Debt that gets ignored is debt that no one bothered to add to the register — fix that first.

How others do it

Ward Cunningham (originator)

Ward Cunningham coined the "technical debt" metaphor in 1992 in an OOPSLA experience report. The original metaphor was about explaining quick-and-dirty code to non-engineers using a financial analogy: shipping fast incurs debt that must later be repaid with interest. Cunningham later clarified that the metaphor applies specifically to deliberate debt — code shipped knowing it would need rework — not accidental design mistakes. Martin Fowler later extended the model with the "tech debt quadrant" (deliberate vs inadvertent × prudent vs reckless) which clarifies the four flavours of debt teams accumulate.

What we take: Distinguish deliberate debt (shipped knowingly) from inadvertent debt (design mistakes discovered later). They have different owners and different urgency. Inadvertent debt should be fixed; deliberate debt should be paid back according to plan.

Source: Fowler — Technical Debt

ThoughtWorks

ThoughtWorks has published extensively on tech debt management, particularly in their Tech Radar and consulting practice. Their recommended approach: a single shared debt register, monthly reviews with leadership, and 15–20% sprint capacity allocated to debt payoff. They distinguish "tech debt" (manageable, known) from "tech wasteland" (so much accumulated debt that the system is unmaintainable) — the threshold is roughly when the team spends more time working around debt than building features.

What we take: 15–20% sprint allocation to debt payoff is the industry standard. Less and debt grows faster than it is paid; more and feature velocity suffers. Calibrate based on the current debt load.

Source: ThoughtWorks — Managing technical debt

Stripe

Stripe explicitly invests in paying down tech debt as a strategic asset, not a cleanup activity. Their engineering principles include "leave the campsite cleaner than you found it" and their internal practice is to allocate dedicated debt-payoff sprints periodically rather than only opportunistic refactoring. Stripe also publishes engineering "investment ratios" (% to features vs % to platform vs % to debt) as part of their planning rhythm — making the trade-off explicit at the leadership level.

What we take: Publish an explicit "investment ratio" (e.g. 60% features / 25% platform / 15% debt) at the team level. Makes the trade-off visible and gives the architect a tool to justify debt work.

Source: Stripe Engineering Principles

Anatomy

Debt Register

A shared document (spreadsheet, GitHub issue list with a 'tech-debt' label, or a Linear project) with one row per debt item. Required columns: description, interest, payoff, owner, priority, status. Stored in a known location; linked from the team's README.

Interest Rate Estimate

Engineer-days per sprint of slowdown caused by this debt. Estimated by the team — exact measurement is impractical. The estimate forces the question: "how much does this actually cost?" An item with no measurable interest is not really debt; it is preference.

Payoff Cost Estimate

Engineer-days to fully resolve. Includes implementation, migration, and rollout. Underestimates here cause repeated debt-payoff failures — be generous. Add 30% buffer for unforeseen complications.

Monthly Review

A 30-minute monthly review with engineering leadership. Reports: top 5 debt items by interest, items paid off this month, items added this month, total debt cost trend. Without this ritual the register is invisible to leadership.

Allocation Discipline

Each sprint reserves 15–20% capacity for debt payoff. The capacity is protected — features that take more than expected do not cannibalise debt time. Without protection, debt time is the first to be sacrificed under feature pressure.

Common pitfalls

Pitfall Why it happens What to do instead
"Tech debt" used to label anything an engineer dislikes Engineers add personal preferences (different framework, different style) to the debt register. The list grows; the priority signal weakens; leadership stops trusting the register. Require an interest rate estimate for every entry. Items without a measurable interest cost (days/sprint of slowdown) are rejected. Preferences without cost are preferences, not debt.
Debt payoff sprints that get cancelled under pressure A debt-payoff sprint is scheduled and then cancelled because "we have to ship this feature." Debt grows; the next sprint repeats the pattern; debt becomes structural. Protect the 15–20% allocation in every sprint, not in dedicated debt sprints. Continuous small payoff is less cancellable than periodic dedicated sprints. If a feature needs more time, it cuts scope, not debt time.
Register that grows without items being removed Items are added but never closed. After a year the register has 200 items, half irrelevant, and nobody reviews it. The signal-to-noise ratio is too low to act on. Quarterly cleanup: review all open items, close ones no longer relevant (system removed, refactored elsewhere). Aim for a register of 30–50 items per team — manageable and meaningful.
