Capacity Planning & FinOps — design for cost

Make cloud spend a deliberate engineering decision rather than a CFO surprise.

TL;DR

Capacity planning estimates future resource needs (compute, storage, bandwidth) based on growth projections and measured utilisation. FinOps (Financial Operations for cloud), formalised by the FinOps Foundation in 2019, combines engineering and finance disciplines to optimise cloud spend. Together they prevent both over-provisioning (wasted money) and under-provisioning (outages at scale). Architects who cannot answer "can we handle 10× traffic for Black Friday at what cost?" become blockers when the board asks the question.

What it is

Capacity Planning and FinOps are two complementary disciplines that together govern cloud resource usage:

  • Capacity Planning — estimating future resource needs from current utilisation and projected growth. Outputs: a per-service plan that answers "if we grow 5× over the next year, what will we need?"
  • FinOps — the financial-operations practice of running cloud at scale. The FinOps Foundation (founded 2019, now under the Linux Foundation) defines a three-phase maturity model: Inform (visibility into spend), Optimise (action on findings), Operate (continuous governance).

Together they cover the full lifecycle: design-time decisions (instance types, region choices), operational tagging and reporting, ongoing optimisation (reserved instances, rightsizing, savings plans), and budget alerts that catch unexpected cost growth before it shocks the next finance review.

Why we need it

Cloud bills that surprise the CFO kill startup growth. A 3× cost increase quarter-over-quarter, discovered at month-end, derails the next funding conversation and forces emergency cost-cutting that prioritises optics over architecture. Equally, under-provisioning creates outages at peak load — the architect who cannot model capacity becomes a blocker when the board asks "can we handle 10× traffic on launch day?"

FinOps and capacity planning prevent both failure modes. Tagging gives the finance team visibility into which products and teams generate cost. Capacity plans turn capacity into a deliberate decision. Unit costs (cost per request, cost per active user) become a KPI that engineering and finance share. The architect who can frame technical choices in unit-economics terms becomes a strategic asset to leadership.

How others do it

FinOps Foundation

The FinOps Foundation, established in 2019 and now under the Linux Foundation, defines the canonical FinOps framework. Their three-phase model (Inform / Optimise / Operate) and twelve principles are the industry standard. The Foundation has a free certification programme (FinOps Practitioner) and publishes the annual State of FinOps report based on hundreds of practitioner surveys. The framework is vendor-neutral but most strongly used in AWS, GCP, and Azure deployments.

What we take: Start at the Inform phase: tag everything before trying to optimise anything. The first quarter of FinOps work is almost entirely visibility — without tagging, optimisation has no target.

Source: FinOps Foundation framework

Google SRE — capacity planning

The Google SRE Book's chapter on Non-Abstract Large System Design includes a detailed capacity planning methodology: load testing as the canonical input, headroom factors (typical 2× over peak), and the role of resource forecasting in incident postmortems. Google has documented how capacity planning prevents outages at planetary scale and how the same techniques apply at startup scale with simpler inputs.

What we take: Build a 2× headroom factor into capacity plans by default. Real-world peaks consistently exceed forecasts. The headroom is the difference between a stretching system and an outage.

Source: Google SRE — Non-Abstract Design

Spotify

Spotify's FinOps practice is publicly documented in their engineering blog and conference talks. Spotify achieved 25%+ year-over-year cost efficiency improvements by implementing aggressive tagging, team-level cost ownership, and unit-cost KPIs (cost per monthly active user). Each Squad sees its own cost dashboard and is responsible for its spend. The Squad model from Team Topologies aligns with FinOps team-level ownership — each Squad is also a cost centre.

What we take: Push cost ownership down to the team level. A team that owns its cost makes architectural choices that consider cost; a team that doesn't see its cost optimises only for performance and convenience.

Source: Spotify Engineering blog

Anatomy

Resource Tagging

Every cloud resource tagged with: team, service, environment, cost-centre. Tags enforced at provisioning time (Terraform module, AWS Config rule). Untagged resources are flagged for removal weekly. Without tagging, FinOps is impossible — you cannot optimise what you cannot attribute.

Capacity Plan per Service

A document per critical service: current utilisation (peak and average), 6-month and 12-month projection, headroom factor (2× default), trigger points for scaling. Plain Markdown or spreadsheet — sophistication of format matters less than discipline of regular updates.

Unit Cost KPI

Cost per request, per active user, per transaction. Tracked monthly. Trend matters more than absolute value — unit cost going up means the system is becoming less efficient as it grows. Unit cost going down means architectural decisions are paying off.

Weekly Cost Review

A 15-minute weekly check by the architect or designated FinOps lead. Reviews: spend trend, top 5 cost growers, unexplained anomalies. Forecast variance against budget. Catches cost surprises within days, not at month-end.

Budget Alert

AWS Budgets / GCP Budgets configured per team with alerts at 80% and 100% of monthly budget. Alerts go to the team owner and the architect. Without alerts, cost surprises arrive at month-end when the bill is final.

Common pitfalls

Pitfall Why it happens What to do instead
Reserved Instance / Savings Plan commitments without right-sizing first The team buys 3-year reserved instances to capture the discount, but the underlying workloads are over-provisioned. The team locks in the wrong size for 3 years. Right-size first (correct the instance types to match actual utilisation), then buy reservations on the right-sized footprint. Right-sizing typically reduces 20–40% before reservations even enter the picture.
Capacity plans created once at launch and never updated The team writes a capacity plan at launch with a 12-month projection. Six months in, real growth diverges from projection. Nobody updates the plan. The original plan is fiction. Schedule a quarterly capacity review per critical service. Compare projection to reality. Re-forecast based on actual growth. The plan is a living document, updated quarterly.
Cost ownership centralised in a FinOps team A central FinOps team owns all cost decisions. Engineering teams have no visibility into their own cost. The FinOps team becomes a bottleneck and is perceived as adversarial. Push cost ownership to product/engineering teams. Each team sees its own dashboard. The FinOps function provides tooling, training, and benchmarks — not decisions. Decisions live with the teams that make the architectural choices.
