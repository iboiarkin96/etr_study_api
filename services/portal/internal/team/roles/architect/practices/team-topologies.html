Team Topologies — design the org for fast flow

Make Conway's Law explicit and intentional: the architecture is the team structure.

TL;DR

Team Topologies, defined by Matthew Skelton and Manuel Pais in their 2019 book, identifies four fundamental team types — Stream-aligned, Platform, Enabling, and Complicated Subsystem — and three team interaction modes (Collaboration, X-as-a-Service, Facilitating). The model makes Conway's Law explicit: the system architecture inevitably mirrors the team structure. When team boundaries do not match the desired architecture, engineers work against the grain and every cross-team dependency is a coordination tax. Team Topologies gives the architect a vocabulary to redesign the structure deliberately.

What it is

Team Topologies is a model for designing software-team structure based on the realisation (Mel Conway, 1968) that "organisations design systems whose structure mirrors their communication structure." The four team types address different roles in the system:

  • Stream-aligned team — owns a value stream end-to-end. Aligned to a single, valuable flow of work (a product, feature set, or domain). This is the default team type; most teams should be stream-aligned.
  • Platform team — provides internal services (CI, observability, deployment) consumed by stream-aligned teams. Treats its outputs as a product with users, not as infrastructure to maintain.
  • Enabling team — grows capability in stream-aligned teams (security, performance, accessibility expertise). Time-limited engagements; the goal is to make the stream team self-sufficient.
  • Complicated Subsystem team — owns a component requiring deep specialism (mathematical models, codecs, ML systems). Justified only when the cognitive cost is too high for a stream-aligned team.

Three interaction modes describe how teams work together: Collaboration (high-bandwidth, short-term), X-as-a-Service (one team consumes another's product with minimal coordination), and Facilitating (one team helps another build capability). The strategic goal is to maximise X-as-a-Service interactions and minimise Collaboration mode — Collaboration is expensive coordination tax.

Why we need it

When team structure does not match the desired architecture, engineers work against the grain. A team responsible for "the checkout flow" but whose product depends on five other teams' services has five times the coordination cost of a team that owns the entire flow. Every architectural improvement requires negotiation across team boundaries. Conway's Law is not a complaint — it is a constraint that the architecture has to work with.

Team Topologies gives the architect a vocabulary to redesign the structure deliberately. "We need to convert this Collaboration interaction to X-as-a-Service" is an actionable change that reduces coordination cost. Without the vocabulary, the same conversation devolves into vague complaints about meetings and process. Architects who can shape team structure unlock architectural improvements that would be impossible if the team structure were treated as fixed.

How others do it

Matthew Skelton & Manuel Pais (originators)

Skelton and Pais published Team Topologies in 2019 (IT Revolution Press). The book synthesises decades of practice from DevOps, Conway's Law, and cognitive load research into a concrete model. The accompanying website (teamtopologies.com) provides workshops, certifications, and case studies. The model has been adopted at companies from 50 to 100,000 engineers — the patterns scale because they are based on cognitive load constraints rather than process.

What we take: The cognitive load argument is the most important contribution. Teams have a fixed cognitive bandwidth; structure work so that no team exceeds it. When a team is consistently overloaded, the answer is usually a different topology, not more headcount.

Source: Team Topologies — official site

Spotify (Squad model)

Spotify's Squad-Tribe-Chapter-Guild structure (popularised in 2014, before Team Topologies was published) anticipated many of the Team Topologies patterns. Squads are stream-aligned teams owning a value stream. Tribes group related Squads. Chapters are functional disciplines (e.g. all backend engineers across Squads) that share knowledge. The model has been widely copied (often poorly — see the "Spotify Model Considered Harmful" critiques) but the underlying principle of stream-aligned teams with X-as-a-Service consumption of platform services is sound.

What we take: Copy the underlying principles, not the surface labels. "Squad" is a name; the meaningful question is whether the team is stream-aligned and has end-to-end ownership.

Source: Spotify Engineering Culture

Conway's Law / Mel Conway (foundational)

Mel Conway's 1968 paper "How Do Committees Invent?" introduced what is now called Conway's Law: "Any organization that designs a system will produce a design whose structure is a copy of the organization's communication structure." Originally rejected by Harvard Business Review for "lack of scientific evidence", the law has since been empirically validated by studies from MIT and others. Team Topologies operationalises Conway's Law as a design tool — sometimes called the "Inverse Conway Maneuver": design the team structure to produce the desired architecture.

What we take: Use the Inverse Conway Maneuver deliberately. Before changing the architecture, change the team boundaries to match the target architecture. The architecture will follow within 6–12 months automatically.

Source: Fowler — Conway's Law

Anatomy

Team Type Mapping

Each team has an explicit type assignment: Stream-aligned / Platform / Enabling / Complicated Subsystem. The default is Stream-aligned; the other types must justify their existence. Stored in a team registry alongside team membership and on-call rotation.

Interaction Mode Map

A diagram showing each pair of interacting teams and the interaction mode (Collaboration / X-as-a-Service / Facilitating). Collaboration interactions are explicitly bounded in time — "we will collaborate for the next 8 weeks to build this together, then transition to X-as-a-Service."

Cognitive Load Assessment

A regular (quarterly) check on each team's cognitive load using a 1–5 scale. Items in scope: services owned, technologies used, on-call burden, cross-team dependencies. Teams scoring 4+ are overloaded and need scope reduction or topology change. The assessment is the data behind structure changes.

Platform-as-Product Mindset

Platform teams treat their outputs as products with users (stream teams). Adoption metrics, internal NPS, documentation quality, and onboarding time are tracked. Platforms that have low adoption are not "doing platform wrong" — they are products without product-market fit.

Annual Topology Review

A once-yearly review of the topology with engineering leadership. Inputs: cognitive load data, dependency-map analysis, retrospectives across teams. Output: changes to team types, interaction modes, or team boundaries for the next year. Without this ritual, topology drifts and accumulates mismatches.

Common pitfalls

Pitfall Why it happens What to do instead
Renaming teams without changing how they work The org adopts Team Topologies labels (Stream-aligned, Platform) without changing interaction modes, dependencies, or scope. Nothing actually changes. The exercise is theatre. Change the work before changing the labels. Identify the most painful Collaboration interaction in the current topology and convert it to X-as-a-Service. The label change comes after, as documentation of what already happened.
Too many Platform / Enabling / Complicated Subsystem teams The org perceives non-stream-aligned teams as more senior or more important. Engineers gravitate to those teams. The proportion of stream-aligned teams shrinks. Direct customer value delivery slows. Maintain a published ratio: roughly 80% stream-aligned, 10–15% platform, 5% enabling, <5% complicated subsystem. Variations are fine, but a 30% platform-team ratio is a warning sign.
Platform teams that don't treat their outputs as products The platform team builds tools and forces stream teams to use them. There is no adoption metric, no internal NPS, no documentation effort. Stream teams resent the platform and try to bypass it. Platform team's success metric is stream-team adoption and satisfaction, not "lines of platform code shipped." Run quarterly internal NPS surveys. Optional adoption — make the platform attractive enough that stream teams choose it.
