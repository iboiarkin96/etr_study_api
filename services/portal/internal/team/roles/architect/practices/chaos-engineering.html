Chaos Engineering — break it before users do

Verify resilience by intentionally injecting failures, surfacing the cascading dependencies a real outage would otherwise reveal.

TL;DR

Chaos Engineering is the practice of intentionally injecting failures into production or staging environments to verify that the system handles them gracefully. Pioneered by Netflix's Chaos Monkey in 2011, the discipline follows the scientific method: define steady state, form a hypothesis, limit the blast radius, run the experiment, analyse the result, and remediate. Systems that are never tested under failure conditions accumulate hidden dependencies. Chaos Engineering surfaces them before a real outage does — in a controlled experiment instead of at 3 am on a Sunday.

What it is

Chaos Engineering is a structured discipline for testing system resilience. The Principles of Chaos Engineering (Casey Rosenthal et al., 2017) define it as "the discipline of experimenting on a system in order to build confidence in the system's capability to withstand turbulent conditions in production." Five principles guide the practice:

  • Build a hypothesis around steady-state behavior — start with a quantitative definition of "normal" (orders per minute, p99 latency).
  • Vary real-world events — failures that actually happen in production: instance failures, network partitions, dependency slowness, expired certs.
  • Run experiments in production — only the production environment exhibits real traffic patterns and real failure modes. Staging is a starting point, not the destination.
  • Automate experiments to run continuously — a single experiment finds a single issue. Automated experiments find issues as they appear.
  • Minimise blast radius — every experiment is bounded to limit damage if the hypothesis is wrong.

Game Days (planned, manual experiments) are the entry point. Continuous automated experiments (Chaos Monkey-style tools like Gremlin, AWS Fault Injection Service, Litmus) are the mature state.

Why we need it

Systems that are never tested under failure conditions accumulate hidden dependencies. A team believes their service handles database failover; in reality the connection pool was misconfigured three months ago and the service hangs for 60 seconds on a failover. Nobody knows until a real failover at 3 am on a Sunday.

Chaos Engineering surfaces these issues in controlled conditions. The team runs an experiment, the service hangs, the team fixes the connection pool — in working hours, with the full team available, with no customer impact. Companies that adopt Chaos Engineering see a measurable reduction in production incident severity because the failure modes are discovered in experiments rather than in the real outage that would otherwise have surfaced them.

How others do it

Netflix (originator)

Netflix created Chaos Monkey in 2011 after migrating from on-prem to AWS. The tool randomly terminates production instances during business hours to force engineers to build resilient services. Netflix later extended this to Chaos Kong (regional failures), Latency Monkey (network slowness), and the Simian Army (a suite of chaos tools). Chaos Monkey is open-sourced at github.com/Netflix/chaosmonkey. Netflix's resilience to AWS regional outages (December 2012 Christmas Eve outage being the most famous test) is directly attributable to years of Chaos Monkey-induced engineering.

What we take: Start with instance termination as the first experiment type. It is the simplest, has the broadest applicability, and surfaces the most issues per hour invested. Graduate to network partitions and dependency slowness after instance termination is solved.

Source: Netflix Chaos Monkey

Principles of Chaos Engineering (industry consortium)

The Principles of Chaos Engineering, published at principlesofchaos.org in 2017, was authored by a working group from Netflix, Google, Microsoft, and other companies running chaos at scale. The principles became the canonical definition of the discipline. The associated book Chaos Engineering: System Resiliency in Practice (Rosenthal & Jones, 2020, O'Reilly) is the reference text. The community runs the annual ChaosConf and maintains the open-source Litmus toolchain (now a CNCF project).

What we take: Use the five principles as a checklist for every experiment design. The most common failure mode is skipping the "minimise blast radius" principle — running an experiment with no kill switch and no bounded impact.

Source: Principles of Chaos Engineering

AWS Fault Injection Service

AWS launched Fault Injection Service (FIS) as a managed chaos engineering service in 2021. It provides built-in actions for terminating EC2 instances, throttling network bandwidth, stopping RDS instances, and injecting latency. The service includes safety mechanisms (stop conditions on CloudWatch alarms) that automatically halt experiments when blast radius exceeds the configured threshold. AWS FIS lowered the operational bar for adopting chaos engineering — teams no longer need to build the experiment infrastructure.

What we take: Use a managed chaos tool (AWS FIS, Gremlin, Harness Chaos) rather than building your own. The managed safety mechanisms (auto-stop on alarms) are valuable enough on their own to justify the cost.

Source: AWS Fault Injection Service

Anatomy

Steady State Metric

A quantitative baseline for "the system is healthy". Typically the same SLI used for the SLO (e.g. success ratio, p99 latency). Defined before the experiment. The experiment compares post-injection metrics to the baseline.

Hypothesis Statement

A specific, falsifiable prediction. "When we terminate one EC2 instance in the order-service auto-scaling group, the success rate will remain above 99% within 60 seconds." Vague hypotheses produce vague experiments.

Blast Radius Bound

Explicit limits on the experiment's impact. Examples: only one instance at a time, only one AZ, only during business hours, with a kill switch wired to CloudWatch alarms. The blast radius is documented in the experiment plan, agreed by the team, and enforced by tooling.

Game Day Ritual

A scheduled, manual chaos experiment with the team present. Typical cadence: monthly per service for the first 6 months, then quarterly. Game Days are the entry point and the training ground. Continuous automated experiments come later, after the team has run 5–10 Game Days successfully.

Action Item Tracking

Every experiment produces 1–3 action items: misconfigurations to fix, missing circuit breakers to add, alerting gaps to close. Action items are tracked to completion before the next Game Day. An experiment without action items typically did not test anything interesting; the hypothesis was too easy.

Common pitfalls

Pitfall Why it happens What to do instead
Running experiments without a kill switch The team injects a failure and discovers the blast radius is larger than expected. There is no automated stop. The experiment becomes a real outage. Configure auto-stop on a CloudWatch alarm or equivalent before starting. The experiment halts automatically when the steady-state metric degrades past a threshold. Test the kill switch in advance.
Starting in production before staging is stable The team is eager to "run chaos in prod" and skips staging experiments. The first production experiment causes a customer-visible incident. Run the experiment in staging at least three times successfully before promoting to production. Staging experiments reveal the obvious failures; production experiments reveal the subtle ones. Skip neither.
Experiments designed to confirm, not test The hypothesis is "the system handles this failure" and the experiment is designed to succeed. The team congratulates themselves; nothing is learned. Frame hypotheses to be falsifiable. The experiment should be capable of producing a negative result. Design experiments around scenarios the team is genuinely uncertain about, not scenarios the team is confident in.
