Architecture Retrospective — learn from the system

A quarterly review of architectural health that makes it safe to revise yesterday's decisions before they become tomorrow's incidents.

TL;DR

An architecture retrospective is a quarterly structured review of the system's architectural health: which decisions aged well, which aged poorly, what new constraints emerged, and what the team would do differently. Distinct from a team retrospective — it focuses on the system, not the process. Output: an updated tech debt register and one ADR per decision reversed. Architecture without feedback loops accumulates regret. Retrospectives make it safe to say "we were wrong in ADR 0017" and convert that admission into a learning artefact rather than a recurring frustration.

What it is

An architecture retrospective is a quarterly structured review that examines the system itself — not the team's process. The agenda has four sections covering 90 minutes:

  • Aged well (20 min) — which architectural decisions from prior quarters are still serving us? Which choices are we glad we made? Celebrate them. This is not just morale — it identifies patterns to repeat.
  • Aged poorly (30 min) — which decisions are causing friction now? Which assumptions have been invalidated? Which constraints have changed? This is the meat of the session.
  • New constraints (30 min) — what has changed in the world since the last retrospective? New regulatory requirements, new scale, new product directions, new technologies that change the trade-off space.
  • Actions (10 min) — convert findings into concrete next steps: new ADRs to author, items to add to the tech debt register, design docs to schedule.

Participants: the architect, 2–4 senior engineers, optionally a product or engineering leadership representative. The session is documented; the actions are tracked and reviewed at the next retrospective.

Why we need it

Architecture without feedback loops accumulates regret. The team makes decisions in year 1 based on the information available at that time. By year 2 the context has changed: traffic is 10×, the team is larger, the product has pivoted, new technologies are available. The decisions that fit year 1's reality may not fit year 2's. Without a retrospective ritual, these mismatches become sources of chronic friction that nobody names.

The retrospective creates safety for revising decisions. Saying "we were wrong in ADR 0017" feels like admitting failure. The retrospective frame transforms it into a learning artefact: "given what we knew then, that was a reasonable decision; given what we know now, here's the updated decision." The trail (old ADR → retrospective notes → superseding ADR) preserves the learning rather than hiding it. Teams that retrospect on architecture build institutional learning; teams that don't repeat the same mistakes every 18 months.

How others do it

Will Larson — engineering strategy retrospectives

Will Larson writes about "engineering strategy retrospectives" as a distinct ritual from team retrospectives. His guidance: review the strategy quarterly, hold it to the standards of an engineering decision (was the reasoning sound at the time? does it still apply?), and update the strategy document explicitly. Strategies that are never reviewed quietly become dogma. Larson's essay "Good engineering strategy is boring" argues that the boring strategies (clearly articulated, reviewed, updated) outperform brilliant strategies that are never revisited.

What we take: Document the strategy explicitly and review it on a schedule. The discipline of writing it down and revisiting it produces better strategy than brilliance does.

Source: lethain.com — Good engineering strategy is boring

Martin Fowler — patterns of distributed systems

Martin Fowler's blog and books document the pattern of "evolutionary architecture" — the explicit acknowledgement that architectures must change over time and that the team needs feedback loops to know when and how. Fowler argues that fitness functions provide continuous feedback at the technical level; architecture retrospectives provide periodic feedback at the strategic level. Together they form the feedback loop that prevents architecture from ossifying.

What we take: Fitness functions and architecture retrospectives are complementary feedback mechanisms. Fitness functions catch drift continuously at fine granularity; retrospectives catch drift periodically at strategic granularity.

Source: Fowler — Patterns of distributed systems

Norm Kerth — Project Retrospectives

Norm Kerth wrote Project Retrospectives: A Handbook for Team Reviews in 2001, formalising the retrospective practice for software projects. Kerth's "Prime Directive" (Regardless of what we discover, we understand and truly believe that everyone did the best job they could, given what they knew at the time...) is the foundational psychological safety statement for retrospectives. The same Prime Directive applies to architecture retrospectives — past architects made the best decisions they could given their context; the retrospective is for learning, not blame.

What we take: Open every architecture retrospective with Kerth's Prime Directive. It establishes the psychological safety necessary for honest analysis of past decisions.

Source: Retrospective Wiki — Prime Directive

Anatomy

Quarterly Cadence

Once per quarter, 90 minutes. More frequent reviews become noise; less frequent reviews miss too many context shifts. The cadence is scheduled in advance for the year — the next retrospective date is set at the end of the current one.

Pre-Read Document

A 1-page pre-read prepared by the architect 48 hours before the session. Lists: ADRs from the last quarter, major incidents, scope/scale changes, new constraints encountered. Gives participants context so the session can start at analysis rather than recap.

Prime Directive Opening

The architect opens the session with Kerth's Prime Directive: "Regardless of what we discover, we understand that everyone did the best job they could, given what they knew at the time, their skills, and the resources available." Establishes psychological safety for honest discussion of past decisions.

Output Artefacts

Two concrete outputs: (1) updated tech debt register with new items and re-prioritised existing items, (2) one ADR per architectural decision being reversed or amended. ADRs that supersede existing ADRs link back to the retrospective notes as their context.

Distribution

The retrospective notes (sanitised if needed) are shared with all engineers. Other teams should know what is being reconsidered. Distribution also creates accountability — the team has publicly committed to specific actions and will be asked about them next quarter.

Common pitfalls

Pitfall Why it happens What to do instead
Retrospective becomes a complaint session without actions The "aged poorly" section dominates. Participants vent frustrations but no concrete next steps are agreed. The next retrospective surfaces the same complaints. Time-box the discussion sections. Reserve the final 10 minutes for actions: ADRs to write, items to add to the debt register, design docs to schedule. Actions are assigned to named owners with due dates. No ownerless actions.
Retrospective skipped under deadline pressure The team is busy shipping. The retrospective feels like overhead. It is postponed and then forgotten. Six months pass without a retrospective. Schedule the retrospective as a recurring calendar event a year in advance. Treat it as a non-negotiable engineering commitment. Skipping it requires explicit justification, not silent deferral.
Only the architect speaks The architect dominates the session, presents findings, and the engineers nod. The exercise is performative; the diversity of perspective that makes retrospectives valuable is absent. Use a structured format that requires participation: each participant contributes to each section. Silent writing exercises (sticky notes on a board, or shared doc) surface ideas before any group discussion. The architect facilitates more than they speak.
