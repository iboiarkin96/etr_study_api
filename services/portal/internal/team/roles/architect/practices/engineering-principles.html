Engineering Principles — shared design standards

Short opinionated statements that replace preference battles in code review with documented intent.

TL;DR

Engineering principles are short, opinionated statements that define how the team makes design decisions. Examples: "prefer boring technology", "delete more code than you add", "optimise for developer experience". They are living documents — reviewed annually and updated when a principle consistently fails in practice. Stripe, Spotify, and Airbnb all publish their engineering principles. Without shared principles each engineer defaults to their last team's habits; code reviews become battles of preference. Principles replace preference battles with documented intent.

What it is

Engineering principles are short, declarative statements that codify how the team makes design decisions. A principle is not a rule (rules are mechanical and enforced by linters); it is a heuristic that engineers apply judgement to. A good principle has three properties:

  • Opinionated — it takes a clear position. "Use the right tool for the job" is not a principle; it is a tautology. "Prefer boring technology" is a principle because it expresses a preference.
  • Falsifiable — it could be wrong, and the team could disagree with it. Principles that nobody would disagree with provide no signal.
  • Citable — short enough to reference in code review. "Per principle 4, can we simplify this?" is a normal review comment.

A typical set is 8–12 principles. Each principle is an imperative sentence followed by a 2-sentence rationale and (often) an example. The set is published publicly or internally and reviewed annually. Principles that the team never cites are retired; new principles emerge from recurring code review conversations.

Why we need it

Without shared principles, each engineer brings the defaults from their previous team. The ex-Google engineer favours one approach; the ex-Amazon engineer favours another. Code reviews become preference battles — "this is better because I prefer it" — that consume time and erode collegiality. The same debate recurs every time a new engineer joins.

Principles replace these recurring debates with a documented baseline. New engineers read the principles in onboarding and understand what trade-offs the team has already decided. Code review feedback grounded in a principle ("we prefer boring technology, and Redis here is unnecessary because Postgres can do this with a table") is harder to argue with than personal preference. The principles also act as a forcing function during principle review — when the team writes them down, hidden disagreements surface and get resolved.

How others do it

Stripe

Stripe publishes its engineering principles publicly. Key examples: "Be a builder", "Always raise the bar", "Move with urgency". The principles are short, action-oriented, and cited in onboarding documentation and internal design reviews. Stripe also has more specific technical principles ("Use commodity tools for commodity problems", "Build what you need to be the best in the world at") that guide build-vs-buy decisions.

What we take: Publish principles publicly when possible. Public principles serve as a recruiting signal, a customer-facing trust signal, and accountability for the team to actually live by them.

Source: Stripe Engineering Principles

Dan McKinley — "Choose Boring Technology"

Dan McKinley (former Etsy principal engineer) published "Choose Boring Technology" in 2015, which became one of the most cited engineering principles in the industry. The post introduces the "innovation tokens" concept: each new technology costs an innovation token, you have a limited budget per company, and the budget should be spent on technologies that differentiate the business — not on commodity infrastructure. The post has been a foundational reference for engineering principles at hundreds of companies.

What we take: Steal proven principles from established essays rather than inventing new ones. "Choose Boring Technology" has internalised over a decade of industry learning; reinventing the equivalent principle from scratch is wasted work.

Source: McKinley — Choose Boring Technology

Spotify

Spotify's engineering culture (documented in their "Engineering Culture" videos and blog posts) is built around principles like "Autonomy enabled by alignment", "Squads own their part end-to-end", and "Fail fast, learn fast." These principles are reinforced through onboarding, internal talks, and the structural design of the Squad-Tribe-Chapter-Guild model. Spotify treats principles as the connecting tissue between technical architecture and organisational structure — the same principles apply to both.

What we take: Principles work best when they apply to both technical and organisational decisions. "Prefer reversible decisions" applies equally to architecture and to team structure.

Source: Spotify Engineering blog

Anatomy

Imperative Statement

A 5–10 word imperative sentence. "Prefer boring technology." "Delete more code than you add." "Optimise for developer experience." The brevity is the feature — it makes the principle citable.

Rationale

2–3 sentences explaining why this principle exists. The rationale matters because it lets the team judge edge cases. A principle without a rationale becomes a rule applied dogmatically; a principle with a rationale becomes a heuristic applied judgementally.

Example or Counter-Example

A specific instance the team agreed on. For "Prefer boring technology": "Don't choose CockroachDB when PostgreSQL is sufficient. Choose CockroachDB only when you need cross-region writes — which you probably don't yet." The example anchors the principle in concrete decisions.

Annual Review

Once a year, the team reviews every principle. Three outcomes: keep (still useful), revise (the rationale has shifted), retire (the team no longer cites this in practice). The review prevents principles from becoming dogma — they evolve with the team.

Code Review Citation

Reviewers cite principles by short name or number when giving feedback. "Per 'Prefer boring technology', why Kafka here instead of SQS?" Citing the principle externalises the disagreement — it is not the reviewer's opinion vs. the author's, it is the team's documented standard vs. the proposal.

Common pitfalls

Pitfall Why it happens What to do instead
Principles that are tautologies (nobody could disagree) The team writes principles like "Write good code" and "Be a good engineer." Nobody disagrees, but nobody changes their behaviour either. The principles are aspirational without being opinionated. Pressure-test each principle: "could a reasonable engineer disagree with this?" If no, the principle is too vague. Sharpen it until the disagreement becomes visible — that is where the principle adds value.
Too many principles (30+) — none get cited The team adds every good idea they hear about. After a year there are 30 principles. Nobody remembers them. Code reviews revert to personal preference. Cap at 12 principles. New principles require retiring an existing one. The cap forces the team to prioritise — only principles that matter most survive.
Principles never updated — become dogma The principles are written in year 1 and never revisited. By year 3 the team has grown, the codebase has matured, and some principles no longer fit. The team continues to cite them anyway because retiring a principle feels like admitting failure. Annual review with explicit retirement option. Make retiring a principle a celebrated act — it means the team has learned. Updated principles are signs of a living culture, not a failed one.
