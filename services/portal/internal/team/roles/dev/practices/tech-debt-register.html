Tech-Debt Register — explicit, ranked, quarterly

Every known piece of debt is written down, scored on impact and effort, and gets a fixed slice of next quarter's capacity.

TL;DR

Tech debt is Ward Cunningham's 1992 metaphor for the cost of expedient code; Martin Fowler's 2009 quadrant (deliberate/inadvertent × prudent/reckless) is the standard way to classify it. A tech-debt register is the team's single tracked list of every known piece of debt, scored on impact × frequency and effort to fix. It is reviewed every quarter, and a fixed slice (15–20%) of next quarter's capacity is reserved against it. Debt that is not written down does not exist for planning purposes — and the team keeps adding to it.

What it is

A tech-debt register is a single, persistently maintained list of every known piece of debt the team is carrying. It is owned by the engineering manager or staff engineer for the area; it is reviewed every quarter as part of planning. Each entry contains:

  • One-line description. "Sessions table has no composite index on (user_id, created_at); every dashboard query does a full scan."
  • Cost of carrying it. Quantified: "Adds 800ms p95 to the dashboard endpoint; on-call alerts twice a week."
  • Cost of fixing it. An engineer-week estimate, plus risk surface (migration size, downtime, blast radius).
  • Owner. The team accountable. Not "engineering" — a specific squad.
  • Fowler quadrant tag. Deliberate-prudent, deliberate-reckless, inadvertent-prudent, or inadvertent-reckless. Drives how it should be prioritised.

The register can be a Linear / Jira project, a spreadsheet, or a markdown file under docs/tech-debt/ — the format matters less than the discipline of keeping it current. The acid test: can a new engineer read it on day one and understand what's on fire and why?

Why we need it

Tech debt that is not written down does not exist for planning purposes. The team keeps adding to it ("we'll fix that later") while pretending the pile is shrinking. Under deadline pressure, the unwritten debt is invisible, so it loses every prioritisation argument by default — there is no row on the spreadsheet to point at. After two years, half the team's velocity is consumed by working around debt nobody has authority to fix.

An explicit register reframes the conversation. "We are carrying $X of cost per quarter and proposing to invest $Y to remove it" is a decidable trade-off. The DORA research on team performance shows teams that explicitly budget for debt repayment outperform teams that don't on both delivery speed and quality — the two goals are not in tension when debt is visible.

How others do it

Martin Fowler — the quadrant

Fowler's 2009 essay (martinfowler.com/bliki/TechnicalDebt.html) extended Ward Cunningham's 1992 metaphor into a 2×2 of deliberate/inadvertent vs prudent/reckless. The framing matters because the four quadrants need different responses: deliberate-prudent debt is paid down on schedule; inadvertent-reckless debt needs a refactor and a learning conversation.

What we take: Every register entry needs a quadrant tag. Without one, "tech debt" becomes a single bucket and the prudent-deliberate items (cheap to repay) compete equally for capacity with the reckless-inadvertent ones (expensive, also a hiring signal).

Source: martinfowler.com — Technical Debt

Thoughtworks — Technology Radar

Thoughtworks publishes the Technology Radar twice a year, with multiple editions covering tech-debt-management techniques. Their consultants repeatedly call out two patterns: (1) maintaining a debt log per service that is reviewed in every iteration retro, and (2) a "debt budget" — a fixed slice of capacity (commonly 15–20%) that is non-negotiable.

What we take: A debt budget is a forcing function. Without it, the team always "needs the capacity for features this quarter" and debt repayment is always next quarter. A 20% block that is sacrosanct makes the conversation about which debt, not whether.

Source: thoughtworks.com/insights

Increment magazine — team practices

Stripe's Increment magazine ran a "Teams" issue (increment.com/teams) collecting practices from senior engineers across Stripe, Etsy, Shopify, and others. Common pattern: a recurring "fix-it Friday" or "platform week" where the normal product roadmap pauses and the team picks debt off the register. Etsy in particular formalised "Bug Bash Fridays" for years.

What we take: A calendared repayment window beats ad-hoc effort. "Whenever there's spare time" reliably means "never". A weekly half-day or a quarterly week creates a predictable pull on the register.

Source: increment.com/teams

The pattern across the literature: explicit register, quadrant-tagged entries, a fixed capacity budget, and a calendared repayment cadence. Teams that improvise any one of those — "we just talk about it in retros" — find the register decays within two quarters and debt resumes its silent growth.

Anatomy of a working register

Single canonical list

One Linear project / Jira board / markdown file. Not "engineering's Notion, plus the staff engineer's Google Doc, plus the on-call wiki." Duplicates rot independently.

Impact × frequency score

Numeric. Impact 1–5 (cost when it bites), frequency 1–5 (how often it bites). Top of the list is impact × frequency, descending. Avoids "loudest voice wins".

Fowler quadrant tag

Each entry tagged deliberate/inadvertent × prudent/reckless. Inadvertent-reckless items get a paired learning action (architecture review, training, hire).

Quarterly review

End of quarter: walk the top 20 entries with the team. Close repaid items, age existing ones, intake new ones from incidents/retros. 90-minute meeting; minutes posted to engineering channel.

Capacity budget

15–20% of next quarter's engineering capacity is committed to register items before any feature work is planned. Not optional; not the first thing cut under deadline pressure.

Trend published

"30 items at quarter start, 24 at quarter end, 8 new added" — published in the engineering channel. Visibility is the social mechanism that keeps the practice alive.

Common pitfalls

PitfallWhy it happensWhat to do instead
Register exists but never gets capacity"This quarter we really need to ship feature X" wins every prioritisation conversation. The register becomes a graveyard — items get added, nothing gets done — and the team stops adding because there's no point.Reserve the 15–20% debt budget before feature planning, not after. Make it a quarterly commitment, not a "we'll fit it in." Track and publish quarterly capacity actually delivered against the register.
Scoring gamed to favour pet refactorsAn engineer who wants to rewrite the auth layer scores it 5×5 = 25 ("affects everything, every request"). The actual production-paining issue gets a 2×3 because nobody's championing it. The ranking becomes a popularity contest.Require evidence for impact and frequency scores: an incident link, a metric, a customer ticket count. Calibrate scores in the quarterly review as a team. Disagreements about scores expose disagreements about what hurts.
Quarterly review skipped under deadline pressureEnd-of-quarter is exactly when the team is shipping the last feature; "let's push the debt review by two weeks" becomes "let's push by a quarter" becomes "let's just do it next year".Calendar the review at week 11 of 13, not week 13. Make it 60 minutes, not 3 hours. Skipping the review is a leading indicator that the practice is failing; treat the skip itself as the alarm and reschedule within two weeks.
