CI & green-trunk discipline — every commit deployable

Every engineer integrates with main multiple times a day, the full test suite runs on every change, and a red main is the team's top priority until it's green again.

TL;DR

Continuous Integration, as Kent Beck originally described it in Extreme Programming (1999), is not "we run CI on PRs." It is a team discipline: every engineer pushes to a shared trunk many times a day, every push runs the full build + tests, and if trunk is red, fixing it preempts feature work. The DORA Accelerate research identifies trunk-based development and a green main as two of the strongest predictors of elite delivery performance. Sub-10-minute CI on every PR, a required green-main gate, and a paging policy on prolonged red trunk are the minimum machinery.

What it is

Continuous Integration is a working agreement and a piece of machinery wrapped around a shared trunk. Engineers integrate small changes to main multiple times per day. On every push, a single short pipeline runs: format check, lint, type-check, unit tests, integration tests, and a build of the deployable artifact. The pipeline is the merge gate; nothing reaches trunk without it going green.

Green-trunk discipline is the social contract layered on top. When trunk goes red — a flake, a bad merge, an environmental failure — fixing it is the highest-priority task for the team that broke it. No new features are merged on top. A red main older than 15 minutes pages an on-call developer, the same way a red production graph pages an SRE.

The "every commit deployable" target means main is never knowingly in a state that could not safely ship. Half-built features hide behind feature flags (see practice 07), not behind a long-running branch. Trunk-based development with short-lived (<24h) feature branches is the working pattern; long-running feature branches are explicitly avoided.

Why we need it

A long-running red main is a multiplicative tax on the team. Every engineer who tries to rebase, merge, or deploy on top of a broken trunk loses minutes to confusion ("is this my failure?"), and the first response is to ignore CI signals — which is the failure mode that turns elite teams into medium-performing teams.

The DORA Accelerate research, repeated yearly since 2014, shows that trunk-based development with green-main discipline correlates with elite outcomes on all four DORA metrics: deploy frequency, lead time, change-failure rate, and time to restore. The mechanism is simple — small, frequent integrations expose conflicts at the cheapest possible moment, before they compound. CI is the only sustainable way to ship fast and ship safely; everything else degrades into either slow or unsafe.

How others do it

Google — TAP + Bazel

Google runs a single monorepo where every change is tested against the entire affected dependency graph by TAP (Test Automation Platform). Bazel's hermetic, incremental builds make a full-graph rerun feasible on every submission. Trunk is green by construction: a CL that would break the build is not even allowed to land.

What we take: The "every commit deployable" property is enforced upstream of merge, not policed afterwards. The build system, not human judgement, is the gate. Investing in fast, deterministic builds pays back as the team scales.

Source: Martin Fowler — Continuous Integration

GitHub — per-PR CI on Actions

GitHub runs the full test matrix on every PR via GitHub Actions, with required status checks blocking merge until green. Their own engineering blog describes a strict policy of small PRs (target < 400 lines changed), short-lived branches (hours, not days), and a "merge queue" that re-runs CI against the latest main before the actual merge to prevent semantic conflicts.

What we take: A merge queue closes the "passed CI three hours ago, main has since moved" gap. It is the simplest fix for the otherwise-common "PR was green, merge broke main" failure mode.

Source: github.blog — engineering posts

DORA Accelerate research

The DORA "State of DevOps" reports (now annual under Google Cloud) consistently find that elite performers do trunk-based development with short branches and have a CI pipeline that runs in < 10 minutes. Low performers either don't have CI or run integration only nightly. The gap on change-failure rate between the two groups is 5x or more.

What we take: CI duration is a leading indicator. When CI passes the 10-minute mark, engineers context-switch away while waiting and then context-switch back to a stale problem; the cost compounds. Keep the pipeline sub-10-minute even if it requires test sharding or selective test execution.

Source: dora.dev/research

The shared pattern: a single short pipeline, automated merge gates, and short-lived branches. Teams that diverge from this on any one axis — manual gates, multi-hour pipelines, week-long feature branches — see change-failure rates climb until they revert.

Anatomy of green-trunk CI

Sub-10-minute pipeline

Wall-clock from push to mergeable signal < 10 minutes p95. Parallelise test shards; cache dependencies; skip unaffected paths.

Required green-main check

Branch protection rule: merge blocked unless all required checks pass. No "merge anyway" button.

Merge queue

PRs re-run against the latest main before the actual merge. Eliminates the "passed CI hours ago, semantic conflict landed" class of break.

Red-main paging

If main is red for > 15 minutes, the team's #ci-alerts channel pages whoever pushed the breaking change. Fix or revert; no third option.

Flake budget

Track flake rate per test. Any test that flakes more than 1% over a week is quarantined and owned by someone — not silently retried.

DORA telemetry

Publish deploy frequency, lead time, change-failure rate, and time-to-restore monthly. Trend, not point value, is what matters.
