Trunk-Based Development — short-lived branches, daily merges

Every engineer integrates with main at least once a day; nothing lives in a branch longer than 24 hours.

TL;DR

Trunk-Based Development, codified by Paul Hammant at trunkbaseddevelopment.com and adopted as a DORA capability, is the practice of committing every change to main at least once a day. Long-lived feature branches are forbidden; anything not finished hides behind a feature flag. DORA's State of DevOps research shows TBD is one of the strongest single predictors of elite delivery performance — teams that practise it deploy multiple times a day with low change-failure rates.

What it is

Trunk-Based Development (TBD) is a source-control workflow where every engineer commits to a single shared branch — main, trunk, master — at least once a day. The practice was articulated by Paul Hammant on trunkbaseddevelopment.com after observing the workflows used at Google, Facebook, and other companies that deploy hundreds of times a day.

TBD has three named components:

  • Short-lived feature branches — branches that exist for hours, not weeks. A branch older than 24 hours is a smell; older than 48 hours is a defect in the workflow.
  • Feature flags — the mechanism that lets unfinished work merge to main safely. A feature flag wraps the new behaviour and defaults to off; the merged code is dormant in production until the flag is flipped.
  • Release branches (optional) — short branches cut from main only when an in-flight release needs hotfixes that the team does not want polluted by ongoing trunk work. Cherry-picks flow from main to the release branch, never the other way.

The discipline is simple to describe and hard to live: every change must be small enough, behind a flag if needed, that it can be merged today rather than tomorrow.

Why we need it

The default failure mode without TBD is branch debt: a feature branch that diverges from main for two weeks accumulates conflicts the author cannot see. When the merge eventually happens, the conflicts surface all at once, often on a Friday, often during a release window. The cost is invisible in standup ("just finishing the merge") but enormous in calendar time.

TBD makes that cost visible at the moment a divergence appears, when it is one file and five minutes rather than forty files and three days. It also forces design conversations earlier — you cannot merge half a feature without a flag, so the team must decide upfront how the feature toggles on, who owns the flag, and how it gets cleaned up.

Good looks like: 100% of PRs merged within 24 hours of branch creation; zero merge-conflict incidents per quarter; deploy frequency limited only by business choice, not by integration risk.

How others do it

Paul Hammant / trunkbaseddevelopment.com (originator)

Hammant codified TBD after observing the workflows at Google and Facebook, where thousands of engineers share one trunk. His site documents two TBD variants — "scaled" (with release branches for large products) and "smaller team" (no release branches at all). The site is the canonical reference and is regularly updated with new patterns. The core invariant across both variants: developers branch from trunk, integrate back within hours, and never let a branch outlive the working day.

What we take: Pick the smaller-team variant first. Release branches are a coping mechanism for very large teams; for a team of fewer than 30 engineers they create more problems than they solve.

Source: trunkbaseddevelopment.com

DORA (Google) — research evidence

The annual State of DevOps Report has tracked TBD adoption since 2016. Across multiple years of survey data covering tens of thousands of engineers, trunk-based development is consistently one of the strongest single predictors of elite delivery performance — high deploy frequency, low lead time, low change-failure rate. Teams with fewer than three active branches at any moment, integrating to trunk multiple times a day, score in the elite cohort.

What we take: Measure active-branch count and merge frequency as leading indicators. Both are visible in any Git host's API and can be charted weekly with zero new tooling.

Source: DORA capability — Trunk-Based Development

GitHub Engineering

GitHub's own deployment workflow ("Deploy first, merge later" via ChatOps) is a strong-form TBD: a branch deploys to production via /deploy command from chat, and only merges to main after it is confirmed healthy. Branches are typically alive for under an hour. The merge to main is the celebration, not the start of the deploy. GitHub's engineering blog has documented this rhythm in multiple posts about their internal deployment system.

What we take: Coupling the merge moment to a successful production health check makes "broken main" almost impossible. Worth replicating once deploy automation is in place.

Source: GitHub Engineering blog

The pattern is consistent: trunk-based teams move fast because integration is continuous, not because they are smarter. The competitive advantage is structural, not heroic.

Anatomy

24-hour branch-age ceiling

CI job runs nightly and reports any open branch older than 24 hours. Branches older than 48 hours auto-comment a reminder on the associated PR. The cap is enforced socially first, mechanically second.

Feature flag system

A boolean toggle (LaunchDarkly, Unleash, ConfigCat, or homemade) wraps every in-progress feature. Default state is off. Flag lifecycle owner is named in the PR description; cleanup ticket is filed at merge time, max age 30 days.

Always-green main

Required CI checks must pass before merge to main: unit, lint, type-check, smoke. Median time-to-green is under 10 minutes; broken main is treated as a P1 — fix or revert within 15 minutes.

Branch-from-trunk-only rule

Every new branch is cut from the current head of main. Branching from another feature branch creates a dependency graph that defeats the model. Enforced by PR template checkbox and by reviewer reminder.

PR-size discipline

TBD only works if changes are small enough to merge in a day. PRs target < 400 changed LOC (see Practice 03). Anything larger gets sliced into a stack of small PRs, each independently mergeable behind a flag.

Weekly branch-age dashboard

A single chart in the engineering channel: median PR age, oldest open PR, number of branches older than 24h. Posted every Monday. Visible numbers drift toward improvement; hidden numbers drift toward decay.

Common pitfalls

PitfallWhy it happensWhat to do instead
Long branches survive because their age is invisibleStandup reports work as "in progress" without surfacing branch age. Run a quarterly flag audit; any flag older than 90 days is either promoted to config or removed.
"Just one more day" syndrome on a near-done branchSunk-cost fallacy. The author has invested four days; one more day feels cheaper than the conceptual cost of splitting the work and merging half.Make the split the default. If a PR is approaching 24h, the team-wide expectation is "ship what works behind a flag, continue tomorrow on a new branch." The author is not allowed to negotiate their own deadline.
