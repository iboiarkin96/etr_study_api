Exploratory Test Charters — structured unscripted testing

Gives unscripted testing a written goal and scope so tester creativity is directed toward the highest-risk areas rather than scattered randomly.

TL;DR

An exploratory test charter (James Bach, 1996) is a short written guide: "Explore [target] with [resources], looking for [risk area], in [duration]." It does not prescribe specific test cases — the tester designs and executes tests simultaneously, guided by the charter's scope. Charters find the bugs nobody thought to write a script for. Every sprint includes at least one 60-minute chartered exploratory session; session notes are filed in the defect tracker.

What it is

Exploratory testing is an approach in which the tester simultaneously designs and executes tests, using what they learn during the session to guide subsequent test choices. Unlike scripted testing, there are no pre-defined test cases — the tester's knowledge, heuristics, and intuition drive the session.

An exploratory test charter (introduced by James Bach in 1996, refined into a formal structure in his "Session-Based Test Management" methodology) provides a written scope for an exploratory session without prescribing specific test cases. The canonical charter format is:

Explore [target area] with [resources/tools], looking for [risk area/information goal], in [time limit].

Example: "Explore the user registration flow with a mobile browser, looking for form validation failures and state persistence issues, in 60 minutes."

The charter provides direction without scripts. The tester is free to investigate anything they discover within the charter's scope. Session notes are kept in real time (typically in a simple text template) and include: observations, bugs found, questions raised, and coverage achieved. Notes are filed in the defect tracker at the end of the session.

Exploratory testing is most effective when practised by skilled testers who understand the application domain, have strong heuristics for defect detection, and can reason about risk in real time. It complements scripted testing rather than replacing it.

Why we need it

Scripted tests can only find bugs that the script author anticipated. The script author is typically a QA engineer who read the requirement and wrote test cases. But requirements don't describe every possible failure — they describe the happy path and some error cases. The bugs that reach production are often the ones nobody thought to write a test for.

Exploratory testing with charters systematically fills the gap between scripted coverage and the complete risk surface. By directing tester attention at specific risk areas (the charter's "looking for" clause), it is both more structured than random testing and more discovery-oriented than scripted testing.

How others do it

James Bach (charter originator)

Bach introduced the charter format in 1996 and later formalised it as the foundation of Session-Based Test Management (SBTM). SBTM adds a debrief structure: the test manager reviews the session notes with the tester after each session and asks three questions — what was tested, what was found, and what issues blocked testing? Bach's approach treats exploratory testing as a first-class methodology, not a substitute for "real" (scripted) testing.

What we take: The charter format and the debrief. Sessions without debriefs lose institutional learning — the bugs found are filed but the "near misses" and the knowledge gained are lost.

Source: Satisfice — Exploratory Testing

Elisabeth Hendrickson (Explore It!)

Hendrickson's 2013 book Explore It! provides a comprehensive set of touring heuristics for exploratory testing: the Feature Tour (test every feature), the Complexity Tour (focus on the most complex areas), the Variability Tour (look for things that can change), the Interruptions Tour (interrupt normal flows mid-operation). These tours are structured approaches to the "looking for" clause of a charter — they give testers concrete strategies for directing attention.

What we take: Use Hendrickson's tours to populate the "looking for" clause of charters. Different tours for different risk profiles: Variability Tour for data integrity concerns, Complexity Tour for business logic concerns.

Source: Explore It! — Elisabeth Hendrickson

Spotify

Spotify engineering posts describe exploratory testing as a mandatory complement to their automated test suite. Each squad runs at least one chartered exploratory session before each major release. The sessions are time-boxed to 60–90 minutes and charters are written in a standard format that includes the target (a specific user flow or feature), the risk focus (integration points, edge cases, performance), and the tools (specific browser/device, specific user account type). Session notes are stored in Confluence and linked from the release ticket.

What we take: Make exploratory sessions mandatory before major releases, not optional. Link session notes to the release ticket so the sign-off decision includes evidence of exploratory coverage.

Source: Spotify Engineering Blog

Anatomy

Charter template

"Explore [target] with [resources], looking for [risk], in [duration]." Written before the session starts — takes 5 minutes.

Session notes

Real-time notes taken during the session: what was explored, what was observed, bugs found, questions raised. Filed in the defect tracker at session end.

Time box

Typically 60–90 minutes. The time box prevents sessions from expanding indefinitely and makes scheduling predictable.

Debrief

A brief (10-minute) conversation after the session: what was found, what was missed, what follow-up is needed. Preserves institutional learning.

Common pitfalls

PitfallWhy it happensWhat to do instead
Exploration without a charter The tester is told to "just explore" without a written charter. The session drifts to familiar areas and ignores high-risk areas. Always write the charter before starting. The five minutes spent writing the charter is the highest-value five minutes of the session.
Session notes not filed The tester explores and finds bugs but doesn't file session notes. The knowledge of what was covered is lost. Require session notes as a non-negotiable deliverable. A session without notes has no evidence of coverage and cannot be cited in a release sign-off.
Exploratory testing as a substitute for scripted testing The team treats exploratory sessions as a substitute for systematic test cases, skipping BVA, EP, and scenario testing. Exploratory and scripted testing are complements, not substitutes. Scripted tests cover known risks; exploratory tests cover unknown risks. Both are required.
