Quality Advocacy — make quality the team's culture, not only QA's job

Actively builds quality culture by teaching test techniques to developers, sharing quality metrics in sprint reviews, and making the cost of defects visible.

TL;DR

Quality advocacy is the practice of making quality everybody's responsibility, not a gate at the end of the sprint. Concrete actions: QA shares the quality dashboard in sprint reviews (metrics visible to all); QA teaches developers one test technique per quarter (BVA, equivalence partitioning, exploratory charters) so they write better unit tests; QA makes the cost of escaped defects visible in retrospectives ("this escape cost 3 days of engineering time plus 2 hours of user support"). When quality is seen only as a QA-lane activity, developers write tests to meet a coverage number, not to catch defects. Advocacy changes that dynamic.

What it is

Quality advocacy is the practice of actively building quality culture within the engineering team — not waiting for developers to ask QA how to write better tests, but proactively teaching, sharing, and making quality visible. It is the recognition that a QA engineer who improves the quality thinking of the entire team is more effective than one who only finds defects themselves.

The three core advocacy activities are:

  1. Share the quality dashboard in sprint review: Five minutes in the sprint review to walk through the quality dashboard. Escape rate, open defects, coverage. Not as a performance report, but as a shared team metric. "We had two escapes this sprint; here is what we missed and what we are changing." This makes quality outcomes visible to everyone and reinforces that quality is the team's output, not QA's output.
  2. Teach one test technique per quarter: Run a 30-minute "QA knowledge share" session for the development team once per quarter. Topics cycle through the practices in groups A and B of this radar: risk-based testing, boundary value analysis, equivalence partitioning, exploratory charters, decision table testing. The goal is not to make developers into QA engineers — it is to improve the quality of the unit and integration tests they write by giving them the same techniques QA uses.
  3. Make the cost of escaped defects visible: In retrospectives, when an escaped defect is reviewed, state the cost explicitly: "this escape consumed 3 days of engineering investigation and fix time, 2 hours of user support, and caused the feature to be unavailable for 4 hours in production." Cost makes the impact concrete. Developers who see the cost of escaped defects are more motivated to prevent them.

Why we need it

A QA engineer working alone can only test so many scenarios per sprint. A development team of five engineers, each writing better tests because they understand BVA and equivalence partitioning, multiplies QA coverage by five without any additional QA headcount. The highest-leverage activity a QA engineer can perform is to improve the quality thinking of the people who write the most code.

Quality advocacy also prevents the adversarial dynamic that emerges when quality is seen as QA's job alone. In that dynamic, developers write code; QA finds problems; developers fix problems; developers resent QA for slowing them down. When quality is the team's shared responsibility — enforced by metrics visible to everyone — the dynamic shifts to "how do we collectively improve our quality practices?" — a collaborative, not adversarial, question.

How others do it

Google (quality culture at scale)

Google's approach to quality, as documented in Software Engineering at Google, is explicitly culture-first: testing is not a specialised discipline that one team does while others do not — it is a practice expected of every engineer at every level. Google's quality culture is built through: shared testing education in the engineering onboarding programme, senior engineers who model testing practices explicitly, and a promotion framework that rewards engineers who improve the quality culture of their team. Google's quality advocacy is structural — it is embedded in career progression, not dependent on individual QA engineers' initiative.

What we take: Quality advocacy is most effective when it is structural: embedded in onboarding, sprint review, and retrospective, not dependent on one QA engineer's personality. Build advocacy into the team's existing ceremonies.

Source: Software Engineering at Google

Microsoft (the whole team approach)

Microsoft's "whole team quality" model, described in their engineering excellence materials, assigns quality ownership to the entire feature team rather than to a separate QA team. Developers on Microsoft's Azure teams are expected to write test plans, run accessibility scans on their features, and participate in the release sign-off checklist — not leave these activities to QA. QA's role shifts from "tester of the developer's output" to "quality coach who sets standards and improves the team's capability." This shift is enabled by QA advocacy: teaching developers what they need to know to own quality in their own work.

What we take: The QA engineer's goal is to make themselves needed less as a tester and more as a coach. The measure of quality advocacy success is when developers are catching defects in unit tests that would previously have reached QA.

Source: Microsoft — DevOps at Microsoft

Angie Jones (quality advocacy)

Angie Jones, Test Automation University founder and former principal engineer at Twitter and Applitools, is the most prominent advocate for QA as culture-building rather than defect-finding. Her talks and writing describe the role of QA as "quality advocate" — someone who builds testing capability across the team, champions quality metrics in leadership discussions, and makes the business case for quality investment. Jones' key argument: QA engineers who position themselves as the testing police are replaced; QA engineers who position themselves as quality coaches are indispensable.

What we take: The QA engineer's most valuable asset is quality knowledge that can be shared, not testing time that cannot be scaled. Teaching five developers one technique each quarter is more impactful than running more tests yourself.

Source: Test Automation University — Angie Jones

Anatomy

Sprint review dashboard walk-through

5 minutes in every sprint review: current dashboard state, sprint trend, one notable quality event. Keeps quality visible to the whole team at a regular cadence.

Quarterly technique session

30-minute knowledge share for the development team. One test technique per quarter from groups A and B of this radar. Practical examples, not theory.

Defect cost visibility

In retrospectives, state the engineering cost of escaped defects explicitly. Hours of investigation + hours of fix + hours of user impact. Cost makes risk concrete.

PR review participation

QA participates in PR reviews for high-risk features — not as a blocker but as a reviewer who asks: "what is the test coverage for this edge case?" This models quality thinking in the development workflow.

Common pitfalls

PitfallWhy it happensWhat to do instead
Advocacy as lecturing The quarterly knowledge share becomes a QA lecture that developers attend out of obligation. The content is theoretical; developers don't see how it applies to their work. Use concrete examples from the team's own codebase. "Here is a function we shipped last sprint that had a defect we could have caught with BVA — let me show you how." Real examples are more persuasive than theory.
Dashboard as report card The sprint review dashboard walk-through is framed as "QA reporting on developer quality." Developers feel measured and react defensively. Frame the dashboard as a team metric: "our escape rate went up this sprint — here is what happened and what we are changing together." The "we" is important. Quality metrics are team metrics, not QA scorecards for developer performance.
QA the testing police QA focuses on blocking releases and finding defects, not on helping developers write better tests. The team sees QA as an obstacle to delivery. Reframe the QA role from "gatekeeper who finds defects" to "quality coach who prevents them." This means helping developers write testable code, reviewing test plans proactively, and celebrating when developers catch defects in their own unit tests.
