QA Retrospective — learn from escaped defects each sprint

A focused, quarterly QA-specific retrospective that reviews escaped defects, test process gaps, and suite health — separate from the team's general retrospective.

TL;DR

The QA retrospective is a quarterly, QA-focused session (60 minutes) that reviews three things: the escaped defects from the last quarter (which test layer missed them?), the test process metrics (escape rate, coverage, mean triage time), and the suite health (flakiness, speed, staleness). The general team retrospective does not go into this level of QA-specific depth — the QA retro is the forum for improving the test process itself. Each retro produces two to three action items with owners and due dates, tracked in the following retro.

What it is

The QA retrospective is a quarterly, QA-specific retrospective session (60 minutes) that reviews the test process in depth. It is separate from the team's general sprint retrospective, which focuses on team dynamics, process, and delivery. The general retrospective does not go into the level of QA-specific depth required to improve the test process.

The session has three phases:

  1. Escape review (20 minutes): List every defect that escaped QA testing in the last quarter. For each escaped Blocker or Critical, review the RCA from practice 18. For escaped Minors and Majors without a full RCA, identify in 2 minutes which test layer should have caught it. The output is a list of process gaps sorted by frequency: "five escapes were missed because we had no scenario test for user journeys involving authentication and content creation."
  2. Metrics review (20 minutes): Review the five quality dashboard metrics for trend over the last quarter. Which metrics improved? Which degraded? What drove the changes? The metrics review is quantitative — it uses the quality dashboard data from practice 19.
  3. Suite health review (20 minutes): Review the regression suite health from practice 23. Flakiness rate trend, smoke execution time trend, stale test count. Is the suite getting better or worse? What drove the changes?

The session produces two to three action items with owners and due dates. Two to three, not ten — focus is more valuable than comprehensiveness. At the start of the next QA retro, the first agenda item is: what happened with last retro's action items?

Why we need it

The general team retrospective focuses on team dynamics, process efficiency, and delivery velocity. It rarely has time or focus for QA-specific process review. Without a dedicated QA retrospective, the test process improves only in response to crises (a major defect escapes, the CI pipeline breaks) rather than through systematic reflection.

Systematic reflection on the test process is what separates a QA practice that improves over time from one that stays at the same level indefinitely. Each QA retro produces process improvements that prevent the next class of escapes before they happen.

How others do it

Atlassian (retrospective practices)

Atlassian's agile coaching materials describe the "team retrospective" as insufficient for deep process review in specialised disciplines. Their recommendation for QA, security, and architecture is "guild retrospectives" — quarterly sessions within the discipline where practitioners review discipline-specific metrics and practices without the constraint of the team retrospective format. Atlassian's internal QA guild runs quarterly retrospectives reviewing escape rate, coverage trends, and tool effectiveness across all product squads.

What we take: Discipline-specific retrospectives fill the gap that team retrospectives cannot. The QA retro is the QA guild's instrument for process improvement, not a substitute for the team retro.

Source: Atlassian — Retrospectives

Esther Derby and Diana Larsen (Agile Retrospectives)

Derby and Larsen's "Agile Retrospectives" (2006) is the canonical reference for retrospective facilitation. Their five-phase model (Set the Stage, Gather Data, Generate Insights, Decide What to Do, Close the Retrospective) is applicable to any retrospective, including QA-specific ones. Their key insight for action item generation: retrospectives that produce more than three action items rarely complete any of them. Teams that commit to two action items and complete both are more effective than teams that commit to ten and complete two. Focus over completeness.

What we take: Two to three action items per retro, maximum. Each action item has an owner, a due date, and is tracked at the next retro. Incomplete action items from the previous retro take priority over generating new ones.

Source: Derby & Larsen — Agile Retrospectives

Spotify (squad health checks)

Spotify's quarterly squad health checks include a "testing" dimension that functions as a lightweight QA retrospective. Each squad rates their testing health as green, amber, or red across five dimensions: test suite reliability, coverage, CI speed, defect escape rate, and collaboration with QA. The rating process surfaces the dimension most in need of improvement. Spotify's approach demonstrates that even a 15-minute structured rating exercise produces more actionable insight than an unstructured "how is testing going?" discussion.

What we take: Structure drives depth. A QA retro with a defined three-phase agenda produces more actionable output than an open-ended "lessons learned" discussion.

Source: Spotify — Squad Health Check

Anatomy

Escape review (20 min)

List and review escaped defects. Identify the missed test layer for each. Produce a frequency table of process gaps.

Metrics review (20 min)

Quality dashboard metrics trend over the quarter: escape rate, coverage, mean triage time, CI pass rate, open defect count.

Suite health review (20 min)

Flakiness rate, smoke run time, stale test count — trend and current state.

Action items (2–3 max)

Each with an owner and a due date. Tracked at the next QA retro before new actions are generated.

Common pitfalls

PitfallWhy it happensWhat to do instead
QA retro replaced by general retro The team is busy and the QA retro is cancelled in favour of the general retro. The QA-specific process review never happens. Schedule the QA retro as a standing quarterly calendar event. It does not replace the team retro — it is a separate session. 60 minutes, quarterly — a small investment for systematic improvement.
No follow-through on action items Action items are generated but not tracked. The next retro starts with a clean slate and the same issues are identified again. The first agenda item of every QA retro is: review action items from last retro. If action items were not completed, understand why before generating new ones. No retro may produce new action items until previous ones are reviewed.
Escape review becomes blame review The escape review focuses on "who missed this" rather than "what process gap allowed this to escape." Participants become defensive; the real cause is not surfaced. Apply blameless framing explicitly at the start of the escape review. Escapes are process failures, not individual failures. The question is always "what was missing from our process?" not "who should have caught this?"
