Risk-based Testing — allocate effort by risk

Prioritises test effort according to the likelihood and impact of failure, so the highest-risk areas always get the deepest coverage.

TL;DR

Risk-based testing (RBT) replaces random or time-ordered test selection with a deliberate risk matrix: risk = likelihood × impact. Formalised by Dorothy Graham and Mark Fewster in the late 1990s, it tells the team exactly where to spend scarce test cycles. High-risk features get deep coverage; low-risk features get a smoke test. The outcome is a test suite that finds the bugs that matter most before they reach users.

What it is

Risk-based testing (RBT) is a systematic approach to test planning in which the allocation of test effort is proportional to the risk associated with each feature, function, or component. Risk is calculated as the product of two dimensions: the likelihood that a defect exists, and the impact that a defect would have on users or the business if it reached production.

The practice was formally described by Dorothy Graham and Mark Fewster in their 1999 book Software Test Automation, and later developed into a full methodology by James Bach and Jon Bach. It has since been adopted as a core technique in the ISTQB Advanced Test Analyst syllabus.

In practice, RBT begins with a risk workshop at the start of each testing cycle. The team — ideally including the developer, QA engineer, and product manager — scores each work item on likelihood (1–5) and impact (1–5). Features scoring high on both axes receive the deepest coverage: multi-path testing, edge cases, security and performance scenarios. Features scoring low on both axes receive a single happy-path smoke test.

The risk scores are not fixed. They are reassessed when requirements change, when new information emerges, or when a previous testing cycle reveals unexpected defects in a formerly low-risk area.

Why we need it

Without risk weighting, test effort distributes itself randomly or chronologically — the last feature built gets the least testing because time has run out. The result is systematic under-testing of the features that matter most and over-testing of features that rarely fail.

Risk-based testing solves this by making the trade-offs explicit before test execution begins. The team knows, in writing, that the payment confirmation flow has a risk score of 25 and the read-only profile tooltip has a risk score of 2. When the sprint runs out of time, the decision about what to cut is informed rather than accidental.

It also provides an audit trail: the risk assessment document is evidence that the QA team applied professional judgement about where to focus. This matters for release sign-offs, compliance reviews, and post-incident investigations.

How others do it

Short tour of how teams known for the practice apply it.

ISTQB (International Software Testing Qualifications Board)

ISTQB formalised RBT as a core technique in the Advanced Test Analyst (CTAL-TA) curriculum. Their framework distinguishes product risk (likelihood × impact of failure) from project risk (schedule, budget, team). The ISTQB risk-based approach requires a formal risk identification session, a risk assessment matrix, and a documented risk response for each item. Risk items are tracked through the test cycle and re-assessed at defined milestones.

What we take: The two-dimensional matrix (likelihood × impact) and the formal re-assessment cadence. Risk scores are a living document, not a one-time snapshot.

Source: ISTQB — Advanced Test Analyst

Spotify

Spotify engineering blogs describe a quality approach where each squad maintains a risk register for its features. High-risk areas (payment flows, playlist sync, social sharing) receive dedicated test squads and automated canary analysis. Lower-risk areas (settings pages, notification preferences) rely on developer-authored unit tests and occasional exploratory sessions. The risk classification is reviewed in each squad's quality retrospective.

What we take: Embedding the risk review into the retrospective so it stays current, rather than becoming a quarterly artefact that nobody reads.

Source: Spotify Engineering Blog

Google (Testing at Google)

The book Software Engineering at Google describes how Google's test engineering teams use risk classification to decide which features require dedicated test engineers versus developer-only testing. High-criticality features (search ranking, ads serving, authentication) are assigned a test engineer; commodity features rely on unit tests and code review. Risk is assessed along two axes: user impact and system complexity.

What we take: The distinction between user impact and system complexity as independent risk dimensions — a feature can be simple to build but catastrophic if it fails.

Source: Software Engineering at Google (Winters, Manshreck, Wright)

Across organisations the pattern is consistent: risk assessment before test planning, explicit scores for likelihood and impact, deeper coverage for higher scores, and periodic re-assessment. The scoring scale and the cadence vary; the structure does not.

Anatomy

The parts a complete risk-based testing practice has. None of these are optional.

Risk identification session

A time-boxed meeting (10–20 minutes) at the start of each sprint where the team lists the features under test and assigns preliminary risk scores.

Risk matrix

A table or 2×2 grid showing each feature's likelihood score, impact score, and combined risk rating. Stored in the test plan or a shared document.

Coverage rules by risk tier

Explicit rules: critical-risk features require full path coverage + negative tests; low-risk features require a single happy-path smoke.

Risk response actions

For each high-risk area: what mitigation is in place (automation, monitoring, feature flags) and what additional testing will be added.

Re-assessment trigger

Documented conditions that trigger a risk re-assessment: requirement change, discovered defect in a previously low-risk area, architecture change.

Common pitfalls

PitfallWhy it happensWhat to do instead
Scoring everything as high risk Teams are reluctant to commit to "low risk" because it feels like accepting that an area won't be tested thoroughly. Enforce a forced-ranking rule: at least 30% of features in each sprint must be classified as low or medium risk. The goal is differentiation, not coverage maximisation.
Static risk scores The risk matrix is filled in at the start of the project and never updated, even when requirements change significantly. Add a risk re-assessment to the sprint planning checklist. Treat the risk score as a living attribute of the feature, not a historical fact.
Risk assessment without coverage impact The team scores risks but doesn't change what they test — every feature still gets the same test depth. Define explicit coverage rules per risk tier before the first risk assessment, so the scores have a concrete impact on what gets tested and what gets skipped.
