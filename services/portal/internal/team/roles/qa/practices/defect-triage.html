Defect Triage — severity × priority classification on every bug

Separates how bad a defect is (severity) from how urgently it must be fixed (priority), preventing misallocation of engineering effort.

TL;DR

Defect triage assigns two independent dimensions to every bug: severity (how bad is the failure — from blocker to cosmetic) and priority (how urgently must it be fixed — from immediate to backlog). High severity does not imply high priority and vice versa: a blocker in a deprecated feature may be low priority; a cosmetic defect on the login page may be high priority for brand reasons. Mixing the two dimensions is the most common triage failure mode. Every defect is triaged within 24 hours of filing; blockers are assigned immediately.

What it is

Defect triage is the process of classifying every reported defect on two independent dimensions before it enters the backlog:

  • Severity measures how bad the failure is from a technical standpoint — what is the impact on the system if this defect is present? Severity classifications: Blocker (system cannot be used), Critical (major functionality lost), Major (significant degradation), Minor (minor degradation), Cosmetic (appearance only).
  • Priority measures how urgently the defect must be fixed from a business standpoint — how quickly must this be resolved? Priority classifications: Immediate (fix in current sprint, hotfix if in production), High (fix in next sprint), Medium (fix within the quarter), Low (backlog — fix in scheduled cleanup).

The critical insight is that severity and priority are independent. A high-severity defect (blocker) in a feature used by 2 internal users may be lower priority than a low-severity defect (cosmetic) on the login page that affects every user's first impression. Conflating the two dimensions leads to systematic misallocation of engineering effort.

Triage is a team activity, not a QA-only one. Every defect is triaged within 24 hours of filing in a brief triage session with QA, the relevant developer, and optionally the PM. Blockers are assigned immediately without waiting for a scheduled triage.

Why we need it

Without explicit triage, defect classification defaults to whoever filed the bug using whatever scale they felt appropriate. The result is an inconsistent backlog where "critical" may mean "annoyed me during testing" for one QA engineer and "system data loss" for another. Engineers sorting the backlog cannot prioritise effectively because the classifications are not comparable.

Triage also catches duplicates, invalid reports (not a bug — working as designed), and defects that are actually missing requirements rather than implementation failures. A defect resolved at triage as "working as designed" does not waste developer investigation time.

How others do it

ISTQB (standard classification)

The ISTQB Foundation Level Syllabus defines the severity/priority split as a core concept in defect management. ISTQB explicitly lists the common failure mode: teams conflate severity with priority, causing high-severity low-priority defects to be treated as emergencies and low-severity high-priority defects to be deprioritised. The ISTQB defect report template includes both fields as mandatory, with independent scales for each. This is the canonical reference for QA practitioners training to the Foundation Level.

What we take: Both severity and priority are mandatory fields on every defect report. Neither can be inferred from the other. Triage must set both explicitly.

Source: ISTQB — Foundation Level Syllabus

Jira / Atlassian (defect workflow)

Jira's default bug issue type includes both a Priority field (Blocker/Critical/Major/Minor/Trivial) and a Severity custom field, recognising the two-dimension model. Atlassian's QA team guidance recommends daily triage meetings for bug-heavy projects and weekly triage for stable products. They advocate a "triage queue" as a distinct Jira status — bugs that have been reported but not yet triaged. Bugs should not move into "To Do" until they have been triaged and classified.

What we take: A "Triage" queue state separate from "To Do." Bugs in triage are not yet prioritised; bugs in "To Do" have been triaged and have assigned severity and priority.

Source: Atlassian — Defect management

Microsoft (bug bar)

Microsoft's internal quality process uses a "bug bar" — a documented, project-wide agreement on which severity of defects must be fixed before release. The Windows and Azure teams publish their bug bars internally, specifying that no Sev1 (blocker) bugs may ship, Sev2 (critical) bugs require VP approval to ship, and Sev3+ may ship with documentation. The bug bar converts triage classification into a release decision rule, removing the ad-hoc judgement call at release time.

What we take: Document a bug bar: the maximum severity of defects that may be present at release for each severity level. The bar makes release decisions objective rather than negotiated at each release.

Source: Microsoft — DevOps bug management

Anatomy

Severity scale (technical impact)

Blocker / Critical / Major / Minor / Cosmetic. Determined by the technical impact on the system. Set by QA; may be overridden by engineering lead with justification.

Priority scale (business urgency)

Immediate / High / Medium / Low. Determined by business impact and user exposure. Set in triage with PM input; may differ from severity.

Triage SLA

Every bug is triaged within 24 hours of filing. Blockers skip the queue — they are assigned immediately to an engineer on filing.

Bug bar (release gate)

Documented policy: no Blockers or Criticals may ship. Majors require QA lead sign-off. Minor and Cosmetic may ship with documented tracking.

Common pitfalls

PitfallWhy it happensWhat to do instead
Conflating severity and priority The team uses a single "priority" field for both how bad the bug is and how urgently it must be fixed. The two meanings get mixed and the backlog becomes unsortable. Use two explicit fields. Train the team on the difference with examples: "blocker severity, low priority" is a valid classification — it means the bug is technically severe but in a low-traffic area that can wait.
Severity inflation Reporters mark every bug as Critical to get attention. The Critical bucket fills with minor issues; actual Critical bugs are not distinguishable. QA owns severity classification, not the reporter. The reporter's severity is advisory; QA validates it at triage. Calibrate severity with documented examples for each level.
No triage SLA Bugs accumulate in the triage queue for weeks. The backlog grows stale; bugs that were Blockers when filed are now irrelevant because the feature was redesigned. Enforce a 24-hour triage SLA. Track the triage queue age as a metric in the quality dashboard. Escalate if the queue age exceeds the SLA.
