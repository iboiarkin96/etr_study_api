Product Postmortem — learn from every miss and every win

A structured retrospective on a completed initiative — not to assign blame, but to surface systemic improvements that prevent the same failure from recurring.

TL;DR

A Product Postmortem is a written retrospective conducted within 2 weeks of completing a significant initiative (whether it succeeded or failed). It covers: what was the intended outcome, what actually happened, what went well and why, what went wrong and why (root cause analysis), and what specific process changes will prevent the failure from recurring. Postmortems are blameless — focused on systemic causes, not individual errors. They are shared across the product organisation, not filed in a drawer.

What it is

A Product Postmortem is a structured written retrospective conducted after the completion of a significant initiative — whether it succeeded, failed, or was cancelled. It is written by the PM within two weeks of completion (while memory is fresh) and reviewed by the team in a 60-minute meeting.

The postmortem covers five sections:

  1. Timeline: A chronological factual account of the initiative — when key decisions were made, when significant problems were discovered, when it shipped. No interpretation yet.
  2. Impact: Quantified outcomes — what metrics moved, by how much, compared to what was expected.
  3. What worked: Specific decisions and practices that contributed to positive outcomes, with analysis of why they worked. Not a list of everything that went smoothly — specifically the decisions that made the difference.
  4. Root causes: For each significant failure, a 5 Whys analysis that identifies the systemic cause. Not "the launch was delayed because the engineer was late" but "the launch was delayed because the DoR did not require deployment readiness validation before sprint entry."
  5. Actions: One named action item per root cause, with a DRI and a deadline. No action item = the root cause will produce the same failure in the next initiative.

Why we need it

Without postmortems, product organisations are doomed to repeat their mistakes. The same launch delays, the same mid-sprint scope changes, the same misaligned stakeholder surprises appear quarter after quarter because no one captured why they happened and changed the process. The postmortem is the mechanism that converts failure into institutional learning.

The blameless principle is essential. When postmortems assign blame to individuals, those individuals learn to hide problems and avoid transparent discussion. When postmortems are explicitly blameless — focused on systemic causes and process improvements — individuals contribute honestly and the organisation improves faster. This is the insight from SRE postmortem culture at Google and Netflix: the same principle applies to product failures.

How others do it

Google SRE (adapted for Product)

Google's Site Reliability Engineering postmortem culture — developed for infrastructure incidents — has been adapted for product failures at many companies. The key principles: blameless, written within 48 hours (product: 2 weeks), published to the entire relevant organisation (not filed privately), and actions tracked to completion in a public register. Google treats a postmortem published without action items as an incomplete postmortem.

What we take: Make postmortems public within the product organisation. A postmortem that only the PM and their manager read does not spread learning. When every PM can read every other PM's postmortems, the organisation learns from all failures, not just the ones they directly experienced.

Source: Google SRE Book — Postmortem Culture

Etsy

Etsy runs "blameless post-mortems" on both technical incidents and product launch failures. Their PM team added a "what would we have needed to know on day 1 to have avoided this?" question to the standard postmortem template — this specifically targets the information gaps that caused the failure, which often reveals process improvements in discovery or brief quality rather than execution.

What we take: The "what would we have needed to know earlier?" question is more productive than "what went wrong?" It focuses energy on preventive process changes (earlier discovery, better brief quality) rather than retrospective blame assignment.

Source: Etsy Code as Craft Blog

Anatomy

Blameless principle

The document never names individuals as causes of failure. "The deploy failed because an engineer didn't test" → "The deploy failed because the DoD did not require staging validation." Systemic, not personal.

5 Whys

For each significant failure, ask "why did this happen?" five times. The fifth answer is usually the systemic root cause. The first answer is usually the symptom.

Action items

One action item per root cause. Named DRI, specific deadline. Tracked in the product review cadence until closed. No action item = no learning.

Publication

Published to the full product organisation within 2 weeks of the initiative completing. Linked from the quarterly learnings summary. Not filed privately.

What worked section

Required, not optional. Identifying what worked and why preserves the practices that should be repeated. Postmortems that only cover failures erode confidence without building institutional knowledge.

Common pitfalls

PitfallWhy it happensWhat to do instead
Postmortem only for failures, never for successes"We don't need to analyse what went right."Run postmortems on successful launches too. Understanding why something worked is as valuable as understanding why something failed — it identifies practices worth repeating deliberately.
Root causes stop at the first "why"The first answer feels sufficient.Force five levels of why for every significant failure. "Why did we miss the launch date?" → "Why did engineering estimate wrong?" → "Why was the estimate made without a spec?" → "Why was there no spec?" → "Why is spec writing not a DoR criterion?"
Action items are never followed upThe postmortem review is treated as closure, not a starting point.Add all postmortem action items to the product review cadence tracking list. Review status at each monthly product review until all items are closed.
