Impact Mapping — connect deliverables to business goals

A four-level mind map that makes the business goal visible at every step — so the team can drop scope without dropping value.

TL;DR

Impact Mapping (Gojko Adzic, 2012) is a visual planning technique structured as a four-level mind map: Why (the business goal) → Who (actors who can cause or prevent it) → How (behaviours those actors must change) → What (deliverables that enable those behaviour changes). Every feature on the map can be traced directly to a business goal. Features with no traceable goal are not built.

What it is

Impact Mapping was developed by Gojko Adzic and published in his 2012 book of the same name. It is a visual planning technique that structures scope decisions as a four-level mind map:

  1. Why — the single measurable business goal. Not "launch feature X" but "increase trial-to-paid conversion by 15% in Q3."
  2. Who — the actors who can help achieve or hinder that goal. This includes customers, internal users, partner systems, and sometimes adversaries (churned users, competitors' customers).
  3. How — the behaviour changes those actors need to make. Not "use the new dashboard" but "check their usage data weekly instead of monthly."
  4. What — the deliverables (features, content, integrations) that enable those behaviour changes.

The map is built collaboratively in a workshop with PM, engineering, design, and business stakeholders. Every item on the What level must trace back through How → Who → Why. Items that cannot be traced are not in scope.

Why we need it

Most feature roadmaps are feature lists with no connection to business outcomes. When priorities shift or resources are cut, there is no principled way to decide what to drop — the decision becomes political. Impact Mapping solves this by making the connection between every feature and a specific business goal explicit and visual. When budget is cut, the team can look at the map and ask: which How branches are highest-impact? Which What items can we drop without eliminating an entire How branch?

The technique also surfaces alignment failures early. When the PM says the goal is "double signups" and the engineering lead lists "improve admin reporting" as a What, the disconnect is immediately visible in the map. In a flat feature list, that misalignment would only surface during sprint review.

How others do it

Gojko Adzic (originator)

Adzic developed impact mapping while consulting on large government and financial services projects where scope kept expanding without clear connection to outcomes. His key finding: teams that built the Why level first — with a measurable, time-bound goal — were able to cut 40–60% of planned scope without reducing business impact, because most features were not connected to the primary goal.

What we take: The Why level must be a single, measurable goal with a number and a deadline. "Improve the product" is not a Why. "Reduce enterprise churn from 12% to 8% by Q4" is a Why. Everything else depends on this precision.

Source: ImpactMapping.org — Gojko Adzic

BBC

BBC's digital product teams use impact mapping in quarterly planning to align product squads around shared audience goals. The BBC's primary Why in most maps is expressed as "minutes of content consumed per week per active user" — a metric that ties directly to their editorial and commercial objectives. Feature squads build impact maps against this shared goal before sprint planning.

What we take: A shared company-level Why (like the BBC's engagement metric) means different product squads can build independent impact maps that are still aligned. The North Star Metric is the Why that every squad's map shares.

Source: BBC Internet Blog

Spotify

Spotify's squads run impact mapping workshops at the start of each OKR cycle. The squad's Key Result becomes the Why. The squad then identifies which listener segments (Who) need to change behaviour (How) to produce that Key Result, and only then lists the product changes (What) that enable those behaviour changes. The map is posted alongside the OKR card in the squad room.

What we take: Pairing impact mapping with OKRs is the most effective combination: the OKR provides the Why and the metric; the impact map provides the causal chain from feature to outcome. Together they close the accountability loop.

Source: Spotify Engineering Blog

Anatomy

Why — the goal

One measurable business goal with a number and a deadline. The map has exactly one Why node. If there are two goals, build two maps.

Who — the actors

All stakeholders who can influence the Why: customers, internal users, partner systems, support agents. Include negative actors (churned users, users who resist change).

How — the behaviour changes

What must each actor do differently for the Why to be achieved? Expressed as observable behaviour: "checks usage data weekly" not "uses dashboard."

What — the deliverables

Features, content, integrations, or process changes that enable the How. Only What items with a traceable path to Why are in scope.

Priority slices

Horizontal slices across the map define release scopes — similar to story map release cuts. The minimum viable release is the thinnest horizontal slice that produces measurable movement on Why.

Common pitfalls

PitfallWhy it happensWhat to do instead
Multiple Why goals on one mapStakeholders each want their goal represented.Build a separate map for each goal. A map with two Why nodes has no causal discipline — features cannot be traced to a single outcome.
How level describes features, not behavioursTeams default to thinking in deliverables.Rewrite every How as an observable actor behaviour: "User checks X weekly." If it doesn't describe a behaviour change, it belongs in What.
Map built by PM alone before the workshopPM wants to come prepared.The map must be built collaboratively. The discovery of Who and How nodes requires engineering and business stakeholders — they know things the PM doesn't.
