Working Backwards — PR/FAQ before you build

Write the press release and the FAQ first. If you can't write them, the product idea isn't ready to build.

TL;DR

Working Backwards is Amazon's product development method: before any design or engineering begins, the PM writes a mock press release (one page) and a FAQ (one to three pages). The press release announces the finished product as if it has shipped. The FAQ answers every hard question a customer, engineer, or executive might ask. Clarity of writing reveals clarity of thinking — and uncovers fatal flaws before they become expensive.

What it is

Working Backwards is Amazon's mechanism for defining products before building them. Before any design or engineering work begins on a significant initiative, the PM writes two documents:

  1. Press Release (one page): Written as if the product has already shipped and a journalist is covering the announcement. It includes: the headline (what is it?), the customer problem it solves, how the product solves it, a customer quote, and a call to action. No internal metrics — only customer-facing impact.
  2. FAQ (one to three pages): Anticipates every hard question the leadership, engineering team, or customers might ask. Examples: How does this work for customers without an internet connection? How do we handle data privacy? How does this change the pricing model? What happens if the experiment fails? The FAQ is where the difficult trade-offs are documented before they become surprises.

Together, the PR and FAQ serve as the product's source of truth from day one of the initiative. All subsequent design and engineering decisions are checked against the PR/FAQ. If a design decision contradicts the PR, one of them is wrong.

Why we need it

Most product failures are clarity failures, not engineering failures. The team builds for months, then discovers that different people had different mental models of what was being built, for whom, and why. Working Backwards surfaces those conflicts in a document review at the start, when fixing them costs hours rather than months.

Writing a press release is also a proxy test for whether the product idea is any good. If the PM cannot write a coherent headline and a customer quote that feels authentic, the product vision is not clear enough to build towards. Amazon teams know the initiative is ready to build when the PR makes a senior leader want to use the product before it exists.

How others do it

Amazon

Every significant Amazon product — from Echo to AWS Lambda to Amazon Go — started with a PR/FAQ. The document is read silently at the beginning of leadership review meetings (no presentations). Leaders annotate questions in the margin. The PM updates the document and re-presents. Sometimes this cycle repeats five or six times before build begins.

What we take: Silent reading at the meeting start, not a presentation, is the key ritual. It removes the PM's ability to paper over weak ideas with confident delivery. The document must stand alone.

Source: Amazon Working Backwards

Microsoft (Azure)

Azure product teams adopted a variant of Working Backwards for new service development: a one-page "Customer Promise" document that replaces the press release but serves the same purpose — describing the service from the customer's perspective before any architecture is defined. Engineering uses the Customer Promise to evaluate scope and interface design decisions.

What we take: The Customer Promise (or PR) should be a living gate in the design process, not a one-time artifact. When engineering proposes a simplification that would break a customer promise, that trade-off should be explicit and decided consciously.

Source: Azure — Microsoft

Stripe

Stripe's PM team uses a "product brief" that incorporates the core elements of Working Backwards: a customer-facing description of the value delivered, a list of explicitly out-of-scope items, and a set of FAQs that capture the riskiest assumptions. The brief is shared with the entire product team before any sprint begins.

What we take: The out-of-scope section of the brief is as valuable as the in-scope description. Making explicit what you are NOT building prevents scope creep and aligns the team on what is acceptable to cut under pressure.

Source: Stripe Product Team Blog

Anatomy of a PR/FAQ

Headline

One sentence, written for a customer, not an engineer. If it requires explanation, it's not ready.

Customer problem

What specific struggle does this eliminate? Written in customer language.

Solution description

How does the product solve the problem? Two to three paragraphs, no internal jargon.

Customer quote

A fictional but believable quote from a real customer persona. If you can't write a believable quote, you don't know the customer well enough.

FAQ — customer questions

Five to ten questions a customer would ask. Includes "what does it cost?" and "what happens if X fails?"

FAQ — internal questions

Five to ten questions engineering, legal, or sales would ask. Captures the hard trade-offs before they're discovered mid-build.

Common pitfalls

PitfallWhy it happensWhat to do instead
PR written in internal languagePM defaults to their own vocabulary.Give the PR to a new hire or a customer and ask them to explain it back. If they can't, rewrite it.
FAQ contains only easy questionsPM writes questions they already know how to answer.Explicitly ask engineering, sales, and design for the three hardest questions they have. Include every one.
PR/FAQ becomes a bureaucratic checklistTeams go through the motions without real leadership engagement.Leadership must actively challenge the document. If it's never returned for revision, the process is theatrical.

Quality bar — what good looks like

  • ○

    PR/FAQ required for any initiative >3 weeks effort

    No build starts without it — enforced in sprint planning rules.

  • ○

    PR reviewed by leadership before design begins

    Silent read format, with written questions returned to PM.

  • ○

    PR/FAQ published alongside the product brief

    Linked from the feature Jira epic and referenced in sprint review.
