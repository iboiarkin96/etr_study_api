Product Health Dashboard — always know if the product is working

A curated, always-on view of the metrics that matter — designed so a 5-minute daily review answers "is the product healthy right now?"

TL;DR

A Product Health Dashboard is a single screen (physical or digital) showing 8–12 curated metrics that together answer "is the product healthy?" It includes the North Star Metric, 3–5 input metrics, one activation metric, one retention metric, and 1–2 business metrics. Each metric has a target and an alert threshold. The dashboard is reviewed in 5 minutes daily and acts as an early warning system — not a comprehensive analytics suite.

What it is

A Product Health Dashboard is a deliberately curated, always-visible display of the 8–12 metrics that together answer the question "is the product healthy right now?" It is not a comprehensive analytics environment — it is an early warning system. The distinction matters: a comprehensive analytics environment has hundreds of metrics and requires hours to interpret; a health dashboard has a dozen metrics and requires 5 minutes.

The dashboard is structured in a hierarchy: the North Star Metric at the top, supported by three to five input metrics that directly predict it. Below that: one activation metric (are new users reaching value?), one retention metric (are existing users returning?), and one or two business metrics (MRR, churn rate). Each metric has a target value and an alert threshold — when a metric crosses the alert threshold, it turns red and triggers investigation that day, not at the next weekly review.

Why we need it

Without a health dashboard, product problems are discovered late — often at a quarterly business review when the data spans three months of undiscovered decline. A health dashboard shortens the detection window from months to days. When retention drops 5 points in a week, the PM sees it Monday morning and can investigate before it compounds.

The curating exercise is itself valuable: deciding which 10 metrics belong on the dashboard forces a conversation about which metrics actually matter. Teams that cannot agree on 10 metrics do not have agreement on what product success means. The dashboard makes that disagreement visible and resolvable.

How others do it

Duolingo

Duolingo's product team uses a "streak health" dashboard centred on their NSM (daily active users completing at least one lesson). The dashboard has four alert levels: green (on-target), yellow (5% below 30-day average), orange (10% below), and red (15% below or unexpected weekend/holiday patterns). Orange and red levels trigger automatic Slack alerts to the product leadership team within 30 minutes of detection.

What we take: Automated alerts based on threshold breaches are more reliable than human monitoring. Build the alert logic into the dashboard rather than relying on a PM to notice a trend. A dashboard without alerts is a passive display; a dashboard with alerts is an active early warning system.

Source: Duolingo Engineering Blog

Figma

Figma's PM team uses a weekly "product pulse" dashboard reviewed in a 15-minute Monday standup. The dashboard has exactly 10 metrics: 1 NSM, 3 input metrics, 2 activation metrics (D1 and D7 activation rates), 2 retention metrics (W4 and M3), and 2 business metrics (seats added, enterprise expansion rate). Metrics are compared to the same period in the prior quarter, not just last week — to surface seasonal patterns.

What we take: Comparing to the same period in the prior quarter is more informative than week-over-week comparison for products with usage seasonality. A 10% drop on the Monday after a major US holiday is not a product problem; a 10% drop in the same week of the prior year is.

Source: Figma Product Blog

Anatomy

North Star Metric

One metric at the top. Current value, vs. 7-day ago, vs. target. Alert threshold defined. This is the most important number on the dashboard.

Input metrics (3–5)

The leading indicators that predict the NSM. Each owned by a specific squad. If the NSM drops, one of these will explain why.

Activation metric

D1 or D7 activation rate — the percentage of new users who reach the "first value moment" within day 1 or day 7 of sign-up.

Retention metric

W4 or M3 retention rate. Compared to the same cohort in the prior quarter, not just the previous week.

Alert thresholds

Each metric has yellow (watch) and red (investigate today) thresholds. Red threshold triggers a Slack alert within 30 minutes — not discovered at the next review meeting.

Common pitfalls

PitfallWhy it happensWhat to do instead
Dashboard has 50 metricsEvery stakeholder added "their" metric.A dashboard with 50 metrics has no dashboard. Limit to 12. Any metric not in the top 12 belongs in a drill-down report, not the health dashboard.
No alert thresholds definedTeam agrees metrics matter but doesn't commit to what "bad" looks like.Define yellow and red thresholds for each metric before launching the dashboard. If the team cannot agree on thresholds, they don't agree on health.
Dashboard reviewed monthly, not dailyIt takes too long to interpret.A dashboard that takes more than 5 minutes to review is too complex. Cut metrics until the 5-minute review is achievable.
