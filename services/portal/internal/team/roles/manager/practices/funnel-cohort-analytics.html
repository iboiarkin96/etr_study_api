Funnel & Cohort Analytics — where users leave and why

Two complementary lenses: funnels show where users drop off in a flow; cohorts show how retention changes across groups over time.

TL;DR

Funnel analysis tracks sequential step completion rates to identify where users abandon a critical flow (sign-up, checkout, onboarding). Cohort analysis groups users by a shared attribute (sign-up week, acquisition channel, plan type) and tracks their behaviour over time to identify retention patterns and segments that churn earlier or later. Used together, they answer "where do users drop off?" (funnel) and "which users come back?" (cohort).

What it is

Funnel analysis tracks the sequential step-by-step completion rate of a critical user flow. For each step in the funnel, the analysis shows what percentage of users who completed the previous step also completed this step. The drop-off rate at each step reveals where user intent breaks down — where friction exceeds motivation.

Cohort analysis groups users by a shared attribute at a point in time (the cohort definition) and tracks their behaviour over subsequent time periods. The most common cohort definition is "week of first use" — this creates weekly cohorts that can be compared for retention at 1, 4, 8, 12, and 24 weeks. Cohorts with better retention than average are candidates for understanding what drove that improvement.

The two techniques are complementary: funnel analysis identifies where users leave a specific flow; cohort analysis identifies which users come back over time and whether product changes are improving baseline retention.

Why we need it

Without funnel analysis, product teams invest in improving features that are not the bottleneck. A team might spend a sprint redesigning the sign-up form when 70% of users are dropping off at the email verification step — a step that was never analysed. Funnel analysis directs improvement effort to the highest-impact point in the user journey.

Without cohort analysis, product teams cannot distinguish between a product that is growing (acquiring new users) and a product that is healthy (retaining existing users). A flat DAU line could mean healthy retention with no acquisition, or rapid acquisition masking catastrophic churn. Cohort analysis separates the two signals.

How others do it

Amplitude

Amplitude's own product is structured around funnel and cohort analysis as primary analysis primitives. Their standard onboarding analysis for new enterprise customers begins with a sign-up-to-activation funnel and a week-0 to week-8 cohort retention chart. These two charts together reveal whether the activation problem is "users don't complete sign-up" (funnel) or "users who activate don't come back" (cohort) — requiring different solutions.

What we take: The funnel and cohort diagnosis must precede solution design. A sign-up redesign solves a funnel problem; an onboarding sequence redesign solves a cohort problem. Conflating the two wastes sprint capacity on the wrong solution.

Source: Amplitude Product Analytics Blog

Intercom

Intercom runs activation funnel reviews weekly for each product area. The funnel is divided into three phases: awareness (landing page → trial start), activation (trial start → first value moment), and habit formation (first value moment → return in week 2). Each phase is owned by a different squad. If the activation phase drops below 35%, the activation squad pauses other work to diagnose the drop.

What we take: Dividing the funnel into phases with squad ownership creates accountability without requiring all squads to optimise the same metric. Each phase has a minimum acceptable conversion rate that triggers automatic investigation — rather than waiting for quarterly review.

Source: Intercom Product Analytics

Anatomy

Funnel steps

3–7 sequential steps in a critical user flow. Each step is a distinct user action. Non-sequential "steps" are not a funnel — they are a list.

Drop-off rate per step

The percentage of users who completed step N but did not complete step N+1. The highest drop-off step is the priority for investigation.

Cohort definition

The shared attribute that defines cohort membership: sign-up week, acquisition channel, plan type, or feature adoption. One attribute per cohort analysis.

Retention curve

Percentage of cohort members active at week N after the cohort start date. A healthy product has a flattening curve — retention stabilises rather than trending to zero.

Cohort comparison

Side-by-side retention curves for multiple cohorts. A cohort with materially better retention than previous cohorts identifies a period when something improved — worthy of investigation.

Common pitfalls

PitfallWhy it happensWhat to do instead
Optimising a middle-funnel step when top-of-funnel is brokenThe middle step feels fixable; top-of-funnel requires repositioning.Always start analysis at the top of the funnel. A 50% improvement at step 3 is worth less than a 20% improvement at step 1 because step 1 affects all downstream steps.
Mixing cohorts in a single retention chartAll users are analysed together.A mixed-cohort retention chart is almost always misleading. Break retention by cohort definition before drawing conclusions — what looks like stable retention may be new users masking churn of older users.
Funnel defined after the fact to match a hypothesisPM sees a bad metric and adjusts the funnel definition until it looks better.Define the funnel steps before looking at the data. A post-hoc funnel definition is data mining, not analysis.
