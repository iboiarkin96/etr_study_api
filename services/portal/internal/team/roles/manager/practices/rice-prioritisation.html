RICE Prioritisation — scoring over gut feel

Score every candidate feature on Reach, Impact, Confidence, and Effort — then rank by the score.

TL;DR

RICE is a prioritisation scoring framework created by Intercom. Each feature is scored on four dimensions: Reach (customers per quarter), Impact (0.25–3 scale), Confidence (0–100%), and Effort (person-weeks). Score = (Reach × Impact × Confidence) / Effort. The list is ranked from highest to lowest score. Discussions are about the inputs, not the conclusions.

What it is

RICE is a prioritisation scoring model developed by Intercom PM Sean McBride and published in 2015. It reduces prioritisation to four scored dimensions:

  • Reach: How many customers will this impact in a quarter? Use real data where possible — active users per month, segment size, etc.
  • Impact: How much will this affect each customer who encounters it? Scored: 3 = massive, 2 = high, 1 = medium, 0.5 = low, 0.25 = minimal.
  • Confidence: How confident are we in the Reach and Impact estimates? Scored 0–100%. Low confidence → low score.
  • Effort: How many person-weeks will this take across all functions (PM + design + engineering)? Minimum: 0.5 weeks.

The resulting score ranks features by expected value per unit of investment. The ranking is the starting point for discussion — teams debate the inputs (is the Reach estimate realistic? Is the Confidence too high?) rather than debating which feature feels more important.

Why we need it

Without a scoring model, prioritisation is determined by whoever makes the most compelling argument in a meeting. That person is usually the most senior stakeholder or the loudest voice, not the person with the best evidence. RICE externalises the reasoning behind every prioritisation decision and makes it debatable at the input level rather than the conclusion level.

The Confidence dimension is particularly valuable: it forces the PM to explicitly acknowledge how much they don't know before committing resources. A feature with a high RICE score but a 40% Confidence rating is a candidate for a discovery experiment before full commitment — and that insight is invisible in gut-feel prioritisation.

How others do it

Intercom (inventors)

Intercom developed RICE to replace informal sprint planning debates that were dominated by the most senior voice in the room. They RICE-score every feature before it enters sprint planning. The score is not binding — the team can override it — but overriding requires an explicit written justification that is visible to everyone.

What we take: The override mechanism is as important as the scoring. RICE is not an algorithm — it is a structured conversation starter. The value is in making overrides explicit and documented, not in following the ranked list blindly.

Source: Intercom — RICE scoring

Atlassian

Atlassian uses a variant of RICE where Confidence is replaced with "Customer Evidence" — a 0–3 score based on the number of customers who have specifically requested or described the need. This operationalises the Continuous Discovery habit: features requested by more customers get a higher evidence score.

What we take: Tying the Confidence dimension to concrete customer evidence (interview count, support ticket volume) makes it measurable rather than a PM's subjective feeling. This rewards the teams that do the most customer discovery.

Source: Atlassian — Prioritization

HubSpot

HubSpot's product teams score features monthly in a shared spreadsheet visible to all PMs. Each PM scores their own items; a product leadership committee reviews the cross-team ranked list quarterly and allocates engineering capacity based on combined scores. The transparency prevents any single team from gaming the process.

What we take: Cross-team RICE visibility surfaces resource conflicts before sprint planning rather than during it. When every team's prioritised list is visible, engineering allocation becomes a strategic conversation rather than a political one.

Source: HubSpot Product Blog

Anatomy — RICE scoring rubric

Reach

Customers affected per quarter. Source: product analytics, not guesses. If unknown, mark Confidence at 50% or lower.

Impact scale

3 = massive (transforms the experience), 2 = high, 1 = medium, 0.5 = low, 0.25 = minimal. Use consistently across all items.

Confidence %

100% = multiple data points confirm Reach and Impact. 50% = rough estimate. 20% = gut feeling. Be honest.

Effort (person-weeks)

Includes PM discovery time, design, and engineering. Minimum 0.5 weeks. Get an engineering estimate before scoring.

Score

(Reach × Impact × Confidence%) / Effort. Rank descending. Items with the same score are equally prioritised — discuss other factors.
