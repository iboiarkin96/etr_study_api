A/B Testing — let users decide, not opinions

Controlled experiments that replace "I think" with "the data shows" — and prevent shipping changes that harm the metrics that matter.

TL;DR

A/B testing (controlled online experiment) splits users randomly into a control group (A, current experience) and a treatment group (B, new variant). Both groups are measured on a pre-defined primary metric and counter-metrics. If B outperforms A at statistical significance (p < 0.05) and sample size meets the required minimum, B is shipped. If not, B is killed — regardless of how much effort was invested in building it.

What it is

A/B testing (also called controlled online experimentation or split testing) is a method for measuring the causal impact of a product change. Users are randomly assigned to a control group (A, the current experience) or a treatment group (B, the new variant). Both groups are exposed simultaneously. After a pre-determined minimum sample size is reached, the primary metric is compared between groups using statistical hypothesis testing.

A valid A/B test requires: a pre-registered hypothesis ("changing X will increase Y because Z"), a pre-defined primary metric, a pre-defined minimum detectable effect, a calculated required sample size (from the MDE and baseline conversion rate), and no peeking at results before the sample size is reached. Tests that violate these conditions produce unreliable results regardless of p-value.

Why we need it

Human intuition about what will improve metrics is correct roughly 30% of the time in controlled experiments — a finding consistent across Google, Microsoft, and Booking.com's large-scale experimentation programmes. Two-thirds of confidently proposed improvements either have no effect or make the metric worse. A/B testing is the mechanism that catches the two-thirds before they ship.

A/B testing also enables a cultural shift: from "we shipped it, we're done" to "we shipped it, let's see if it worked." This closes the feedback loop between product decisions and customer outcomes, which is the foundation of evidence-based product management.

How others do it

Booking.com

Booking.com runs over 1,000 simultaneous A/B tests at any time. Every significant product change is tested. Their culture is "no feature ships without a test" — enforced at the engineering level, not just the PM level. They maintain a centralised experimentation platform (the XP platform) that handles randomisation, sample size calculation, and result visualisation for every team.

What we take: Experimentation at scale requires infrastructure investment before cultural investment. Without a shared experimentation platform, A/B testing becomes a per-team craft skill with inconsistent methodology. Build the platform first; the culture follows the tooling.

Source: Booking.com AI & Research

Microsoft (ExP)

Microsoft's Experimentation Platform (ExP) runs tens of thousands of experiments per year across Bing, Azure, Office, and Teams. Microsoft's research found that only 1 in 3 internal feature ideas pass their success metrics in controlled experiments. Their "guardrail metrics" system ensures that no experiment that degrades core business metrics (revenue, retention, page load time) can ship — even if the primary hypothesis metric is positive.

What we take: Guardrail metrics are non-negotiable. A feature that increases sign-ups but degrades page load time by 20% fails the guardrail check and is not shipped. Define guardrails before the experiment runs — after you see results, objectivity is compromised.

Source: Microsoft ExP Platform

Netflix

Netflix tests virtually every product decision — thumbnail images, recommendation algorithms, UI layouts, and email subject lines. Their experimentation culture is supported by a statistical methodology team that reviews all experiments for design validity before they run. A test that is not designed correctly is killed before it starts, not after it produces misleading results.

What we take: Experiment design review (before launch) is as important as result analysis (after launch). Bad experiment design — inadequate sample size, confounded assignment, peeking — produces results that mislead rather than inform. Add a design review gate to the experiment workflow.

Source: Netflix Tech Blog

Anatomy

Hypothesis

"If we change X, then Y metric will increase by at least Z%, because we believe [mechanism]." Must be written before the experiment starts.

Primary metric

One metric the experiment is designed to move. The decision to ship or kill B is based solely on this metric meeting or missing the minimum detectable effect.

Guardrail metrics

Metrics that must not degrade. Revenue, retention, core task success rate. If any guardrail degrades, B is killed regardless of primary metric performance.

Sample size calculation

Required sample size based on baseline conversion rate, minimum detectable effect (MDE), and desired statistical power (80%). Run the calculation before starting, not after seeing results.

No-peek rule

Results are not reviewed until the required sample size is reached. Early peeking inflates false positive rates — a significant result on day 2 is not a significant result.
