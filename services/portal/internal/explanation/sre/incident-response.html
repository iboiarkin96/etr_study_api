Incident response

What an incident is

An incident is any unplanned event that hurts users or breaks an important promise (an SLO). The point of incident response is to stop the bleeding fast, communicate clearly, and learn afterwards. This page is the playbook; once an incident is over, the story is captured in a postmortem.

The framework below mirrors the SRE Book — Managing Incidents and Emergency Response chapters, scaled down to a small team.

The four-step loop

  1. Declare. Name the incident, give it a severity, open a chat channel, start a timeline.
  2. Mitigate. Make the user-visible problem stop. Roll back, switch traffic, scale up, disable a feature flag — anything that restores service. Root cause can wait.
  3. Communicate. Tell the affected users what is happening and what to expect next. Update on the cadence the severity requires.
  4. Resolve. Verify the system is healthy with metrics and logs, not with a feeling. Mark the incident closed only after confirmation.

After resolve comes the postmortem. That is not part of the live loop, but it is mandatory for SEV-1 and SEV-2.

Severity matrix

SeverityDescriptionExamplesPostmortem required
SEV-1Major outage. The service is unusable for most users, or data is at risk.API returns 5xx for > 50% of requests; database is corrupted; auth is broken for everyone.Yes
SEV-2Partial outage. A core feature is down or seriously slow for many users.One major endpoint returns 5xx; latency p99 above 5 s; background jobs stuck for an hour.Yes
SEV-3Degraded service. Noticeable but not blocking.One non-critical endpoint slow; UI glitch on a non-critical page.Recommended
SEV-4Minor. Annoying, can wait until next working day.One typo in an error message; a single edge-case 500.No

Pick a severity quickly and round up if unsure — it is easier to downgrade than to explain why you treated SEV-1 as SEV-3.

Roles during a live incident

Incident commander (IC)
Owns the response. Decides what to do next, keeps the timeline, decides when the incident is resolved. Does not fix bugs personally — they coordinate.
Operations lead (Ops)
Hands on the system. Runs commands, ships rollbacks, edits configs. Reports facts back to the IC.
Communications lead (Comms)
Writes updates for users (status page, email, in-app banner). Talks to anyone outside the response team so the IC and Ops can focus.

For a single-person team, one human plays all three roles — but write down which hat you are wearing for each action. It keeps the timeline honest.

Step-by-step: the first 15 minutes

  1. 0:00 — Acknowledge the page. Mute the alert channel only after acknowledging.
  2. 0:01 — Confirm the symptom. Open the dashboard. Is it real? Is it global or one endpoint?
  3. 0:03 — Declare. Post: "Declaring SEV-X. Symptom: <short>. IC: <name>." Open the timeline doc.
  4. 0:05 — First user message. If SEV-1 or SEV-2, post a status update: "We are aware of an issue affecting X. Investigating. Next update in 30 min."
  5. 0:10 — Identify the latest change. Last deploy, config change, dependency update. Roll it back if the timing matches; do not wait for a perfect causal proof.
  6. 0:15 — First mitigation attempt complete. Confirm with metrics that the symptom is gone. If not, try the next thing.

Communication templates

Initial status update

[INVESTIGATING] We are seeing increased errors on <feature> since <HH:MM UTC>.
Impact: <who and what is affected>.
We are looking into it now. Next update by <HH:MM UTC>.

Mitigation in progress

[IDENTIFIED] We have identified the cause: <short, plain words>.
Mitigation in progress: <what we are doing>.
Next update by <HH:MM UTC>.

Resolved

[RESOLVED] <feature> is back to normal as of <HH:MM UTC>.
Cause: <one sentence>.
A postmortem will be published within 5 working days.

Timeline — what to write down (and when)

The timeline is the raw material for the postmortem. Capture facts as they happen — not "what we now think happened". Useful entries:

  • Every alert that fires, with timestamp.
  • Every action taken: command, deploy, rollback, config change.
  • Every external message sent, with link.
  • Every hypothesis you tried and disproved.

Use UTC timestamps. Local times will betray you in a postmortem written a week later.

When to call the incident over

Resolve only when all of the following are true:

  • The user-visible symptom is gone (verified on the dashboard, not assumed).
  • You understand at least the immediate cause; you do not have to know the root cause yet.
  • No fragile workaround is silently still in place. If a workaround stays, file a ticket and link it from the resolved post.
  • A postmortem owner is named (for SEV-1 and SEV-2).
