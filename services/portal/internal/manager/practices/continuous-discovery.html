Continuous Discovery — weekly customer touchpoints

At least one customer interview per week, every week, feeding directly into product decisions.

TL;DR

Continuous Discovery (Teresa Torres, 2021) replaces sporadic research phases with a weekly habit: the PM and designer interview at least one customer every week, extract one opportunity, and map it onto an Opportunity Solution Tree. The rhythm creates a direct feedback loop between real customer problems and every product decision the team makes.

What it is

Continuous Discovery is the practice of conducting at least one customer interview every week as a non-negotiable team habit. Popularised by Teresa Torres in her 2021 book, it reframes discovery not as a project phase but as an ongoing operational rhythm — as regular as sprint planning or retrospectives.

Each weekly session has three deliverables: a recording (with consent), a three-bullet insight summary shared with the team, and at least one opportunity card added to the Opportunity Solution Tree. The OST is the living artifact that connects weekly interviews to active roadmap decisions.

Continuous Discovery is deliberately narrow in scope per session — 30 to 45 minutes per interview — but broad over time. After 12 weeks, a team has spoken to 12 or more customers and has a pattern-rich OST that no quarterly research sprint could match.

Why we need it

The default mode for most product teams is episodic discovery: research happens at the start of a project, findings inform the roadmap, and nobody talks to a customer again until the next project starts. By then, the market has shifted, the assumptions from the earlier research are stale, and the team is shipping to a customer they no longer understand.

Weekly touchpoints eliminate staleness. They also change the team's relationship to uncertainty — instead of treating unknown customer needs as a problem to be solved in a big research sprint, the team treats them as a stream of information arriving continuously, updated every week.

Teams that practice Continuous Discovery report faster decision-making (evidence is always fresh), lower rework rates (problems caught early), and stronger product-market fit (solutions are tested before full build). The habit is the competitive advantage — it is very hard to replicate an organisation that has talked to 150 customers in the past three years.

How others do it

Short tour of how top-tier teams apply this practice. Each card is one company, what they do, and what we take from it.

Intercom

Every PM maintains a "customer advisory panel" of 20 recruited customers available for 30-minute calls. PMs book these slots as recurring weekly calendar blocks. Interview summaries are posted in a shared Slack channel within four hours of each session.

What we take: Pre-recruiting a standing panel removes the scheduling friction that kills the weekly habit. The summary-in-Slack norm makes discovery a team sport, not a PM silo.

Source: Intercom Blog — Customer Research

Figma

Product and design pairs share a joint interview calendar. Every quarter, each pair must complete at least 12 recorded interviews. The OST is reviewed in the monthly design critique — not just the product review — so both disciplines own the discovery output.

What we take: Making design a co-owner of the weekly interview closes the gap between research insight and design decision. Joint scheduling doubles the capacity without doubling the PM workload.

Source: Figma Blog — User Research

Amazon

Amazon's "Customer Obsession" leadership principle is operationalised partly through weekly customer listening sessions embedded in each product team's rhythm. Product reviews open with customer data — recorded calls, verbatim quotes, NPS comments — before any internal metrics are discussed.

What we take: Leading with the customer voice in every meeting, not just research readouts, creates a norm where any decision that cannot be linked to a customer insight is visibly suspicious.

Source: Amazon Leadership Principles

Airbnb

During the early growth years, Brian Chesky personally visited hosts every week. This became institutionalised as a quarterly "host immersion" week where every PM spends five days doing the job of the most important user — hosting or booking — to generate raw material for discovery sessions.

What we take: Immersive discovery (doing the job) surfaces pain points that interview questions never surface. Weekly interviews and periodic immersion complement each other.

Source: HBR — Airbnb Customer Experience

Across companies the pattern is the same: discovery is a habit, not a project. The teams that ship the best products have the most current customer understanding, not the best research methodologies.

Anatomy

The components a healthy Continuous Discovery practice must have. None are optional.

Standing panel

15–25 pre-recruited customers available for calls. Refreshed monthly as new customers are added and inactive ones are retired.

Weekly calendar block

A recurring 60-minute slot, blocked before any sprint planning. Missing it requires explicit team acknowledgment, not just a quiet skip.

Interview guide

5–7 open-ended questions focused on the customer's experience, not feature reactions. Updated quarterly based on the OST's current open questions.

Insight summary

Three bullet points posted to the team channel within four hours: one new thing learned, one assumption confirmed or rejected, one open question surfaced.

Opportunity Solution Tree

Updated weekly with new opportunity nodes from insights. The OST is the living bridge between raw interviews and active roadmap items.

Monthly synthesis

A 90-minute session where the PM clusters the month's insight cards into themes and reprioritises the OST opportunity layer.

How it works in this project

The discovery calendar block is every Thursday 10:00–11:00. The PM and designer rotate the interviewer/note-taker roles weekly. All sessions are recorded in Loom with participant consent. The insight summary is posted in #pm-discovery by 14:00 on the same day.

The OST lives in Notion under PM / Discovery / OST. Each opportunity node links back to the interview session(s) that surfaced it. The OST is opened as the first tab in every product review meeting.

Common pitfalls

PitfallWhy it happensWhat to do instead
Skipping weeks "when busy"Discovery feels optional when delivery pressure peaks.Treat the weekly slot as immovable as sprint planning. If you skip it, you decide in the dark next sprint.
Interviewing only happy usersScheduling convenience pulls toward the most responsive customers.Recruit churned users and inactive users intentionally. Their answers are the most valuable.
Asking about featuresPMs want to validate their roadmap, not discover problems.Never mention your roadmap in an interview. Ask about recent behaviour, frustrations, and workarounds instead.
Hoarding insights in the PM's notesSynthesis feels like extra work after a long interview day.Mandate the three-bullet Slack post within four hours. Shared notes are a team resource.
