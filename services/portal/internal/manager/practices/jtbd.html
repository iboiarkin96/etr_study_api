Jobs-to-be-Done — outcome-driven requirements

Customers hire products to make progress in specific situations — understand the job, not the persona.

TL;DR

Jobs-to-be-Done (JTBD) is a theory of customer motivation: people "hire" products to make progress in a specific situation. The unit of analysis is the job — stable, enduring, situation-dependent — not the demographic persona. JTBD interviews ("switch interviews") reveal why customers change products and what progress they're actually trying to make.

What it is

Jobs-to-be-Done is a framework for understanding why customers buy and use products. Developed by Clayton Christensen and Tony Ulwick, it shifts the unit of analysis from the customer's demographics or psychographics to the job — the progress the customer is trying to make in a specific situation.

A job statement has three parts: When [situation], I want to [motivation], so I can [expected outcome]. The situation defines the context, the motivation captures the driver, and the expected outcome describes what success feels like. The job is stable over time even as the products hired to do it change radically.

The primary research method is the switch interview: a structured conversation with a customer who recently switched to or from a product, exploring the timeline of events, emotions, and social pressures that led to the switch. Switch interviews reveal what competing "hires" are (often non-obvious) and why customers fire the old solution.

Why we need it

Persona-based requirements describe who the customer is. JTBD describes what they're trying to accomplish. These are different questions with different answers. A 35-year-old marketing manager and a 55-year-old retired teacher can share the same job ("When I need to book accommodation in an unfamiliar city, I want to feel confident I'm not being ripped off, so I can enjoy the trip without financial anxiety"). Persona-based segmentation would put them in entirely different categories; JTBD would show them as the same target.

JTBD also explains the real competition. Slack's competitors are not just other messaging apps — they are email, hallway conversations, and project management tools. Understanding the full set of solutions customers hire for a job reveals the actual market the product is competing in.

How others do it

Intercom

Intercom publicly credited JTBD as the framework behind their core product decisions in their early years. They ran switch interviews with customers who moved from email to Intercom to understand exactly what job email was failing to do. Their product positioning shifted from "live chat" to "customer communication" based on the insights.

What we take: JTBD reframes competition. Run switch interviews not just when you win customers but when you lose them — the lost-sale interviews are more revealing.

Source: Intercom Blog — JTBD

Apple (iPod/iTunes)

The iPod succeeded not because Apple made a better MP3 player but because Apple identified the job more precisely: "When I want to listen to music on the go, I want my entire library with me, so I can find any song I feel like hearing right now." No existing product served that job. iTunes served the adjacent job of managing that library with zero friction.

What we take: Finding the underserved job — one that exists but no product serves well — is more valuable than finding an underserved segment. Jobs are the right level of analysis.

Source: HBR — Know Your Customers' Jobs

Basecamp

Basecamp's co-founder Jason Fried applies JTBD to their entire product philosophy: they deliberately avoid feature bloat by asking whether each feature serves a job that customers actually hire for, or whether it serves a job that customers say they want but never actually do. The result is a product that is notably simpler than competitors.

What we take: JTBD is a filter, not just a discovery tool. Before building any feature, ask: what is the job this serves, and have we seen customers hiring something else to do that job today?

Source: Rework — Basecamp

The pattern across companies: JTBD is most powerful not as a research framework but as a decision filter. Every roadmap decision should be testable against "what job does this serve, and how do we know customers are hiring something else for it today?"

Anatomy of a switch interview

First thought moment

When did you first think about changing? What was happening? Surfaces the triggering event.

Passive looking

What did you do with that thought initially? Understanding the gap between trigger and active search.

Active looking

When did you start seriously evaluating options? What were you looking at? Reveals real competition.

Decision moment

What made you decide? Who else was involved? Surfaces social and functional forces.

First use

What happened the first time you used it? Was it what you expected? Surfaces onboarding reality.

Ongoing use / fire

Are you still using it? If not, when did you stop? The firing moment is as informative as the hiring moment.

Common pitfalls

PitfallWhy it happensWhat to do instead
Writing feature-level job statements"When I want to export a report…" is a task, not a job.Jobs are high-level progress: "When I need to present results to leadership…". Keep the level high.
Confusing JTBD with user storiesThe syntax looks similar. User stories describe what to build; JTBD describes why it's needed.Write JTBD first, derive user stories from it. Never the reverse.
Skipping switch interviews for surveysSurveys are faster but capture stated preferences, not real behavior.Run at least three switch interviews before any major product decision. Surveys can follow.

Quality bar — what good looks like

  • ○

    Top 5 jobs documented with situation / motivation / outcome

    Sourced from switch interviews, not internal brainstorm.

  • ○

    Real competition identified per job

    Includes non-product competitors (email, spreadsheet, doing nothing).

  • ○

    Every roadmap item traces to a documented job

    If a feature has no job, it has no evidence basis.
