Now-Next-Later Roadmap — outcome over timeline

Three columns, no dates in Later — communicates direction and priority without committing to timelines that cannot be kept.

TL;DR

The Now-Next-Later roadmap (invented by ProdPad's Janna Bastow) replaces date-driven Gantt charts with three columns: Now (in progress), Next (ready to start once Now is done), and Later (directional, no dates). Each item describes an outcome, not a feature. The format is honest about uncertainty while still communicating clear priorities.

What it is

The Now-Next-Later roadmap is a three-column format that replaces date-driven timelines with proximity-based prioritisation. Created by ProdPad's Janna Bastow, it became widely adopted because it solves the central tension of product roadmaps: stakeholders want commitments, but engineering realities make specific date commitments almost always wrong.

Now contains work actively in progress. Items here are fully validated, scoped, and assigned. The team is accountable to completing Now items in the current sprint or quarter.

Next contains work that is validated and ready to start as soon as Now items complete. Next items have been through discovery, have a brief, and have a rough effort estimate. There are no date commitments, but the ordering within Next reflects priority.

Later is directional — it communicates the themes and outcomes the team expects to eventually address, without any timeline or scope commitments. Later items may be invalidated, re-ordered, or removed as new customer data arrives. This is honest and explicit.

Why we need it

Date-based roadmaps destroy trust systematically. The PM sets dates based on current estimates; requirements change, unknowns surface, and the dates slip. After the second slip, stakeholders stop believing the roadmap. After the third, they build their own parallel timelines. The Now-Next-Later format eliminates slips by eliminating precise date commitments where precision is impossible.

The format also forces a different kind of roadmap conversation. Instead of "when will we get X?" the discussion becomes "should X be in Next or Later, and what would need to be true for it to move to Now?" This is a far more productive question because it has a concrete answer tied to evidence and capacity.

How others do it

ProdPad (inventors)

ProdPad's own product is managed with the Now-Next-Later format. Their team posts a public roadmap using the format, which has become a case study in transparent product communication. They update it monthly and link each item to the customer problem or feature request that motivated it.

What we take: A public Now-Next-Later roadmap is a customer relationship tool. Customers see their requests acknowledged in Later, which reduces support escalations and increases product trust without requiring date commitments.

Source: ProdPad — Now-Next-Later origin

Basecamp

Basecamp uses a six-week cycle model where commitments are only made within a six-week horizon. Everything beyond six weeks is treated as directional. Their "Shape Up" methodology makes the Now column explicit (the active six-week cycle) while keeping a loose backlog of shaped ideas that is equivalent to Later.

What we take: The commitment horizon is the most important decision in roadmap format. Now should only contain work that will realistically complete in the current quarter. Anything further out belongs in Next or Later regardless of stakeholder pressure.

Source: Shape Up — Basecamp

Roman Pichler

Pichler's "GO Product Roadmap" is a goal-oriented variant of Now-Next-Later: each column contains a goal (outcome), not a feature list. The format forces the PM to define what success looks like in each time horizon rather than listing what will be built.

What we take: Describing roadmap items as outcomes ("reduce churn in enterprise segment") rather than features ("add admin dashboard") keeps the conversation on why before what. This is the most important structural choice in the format.

Source: Roman Pichler — Goal roadmap

Anatomy

Now column

Max 3 items. Every item has a brief, acceptance criteria, and a named DRI. Updated weekly.

Next column

Max 5 items. Every item has a brief and a rough effort estimate. Ordered by RICE score.

Later column

Unlimited items. Described as outcomes, not features. No dates. Reviewed and reprioritised monthly.

Item format

One sentence describing the outcome: "Reduce enterprise churn by improving the audit trail experience." No feature descriptions.

Customer link

Each item links to the OST opportunity or customer insight that motivates it.
