North Star Metric — the single number that matters

One metric that captures the core value the product delivers to customers — and predicts long-term revenue better than revenue itself.

TL;DR

The North Star Metric (NSM) is the single product metric that best captures the core value the product delivers to customers. It sits between daily activity metrics (vanity) and revenue (lagging) — it moves when customers succeed and predicts revenue before revenue moves. All product squads orient their OKRs to move the NSM. If a feature doesn't move the NSM, it needs a strong justification to ship.

What it is

The North Star Metric is a concept popularised by Sean Ellis (GrowthHackers) and Amplitude as the single product metric that best captures the core value exchange between the product and its customers. It is not revenue (which is an outcome of value delivered) and not a vanity metric like page views (which may not correlate with value at all). It is the metric that moves when customers are genuinely succeeding with the product.

Classic examples: Spotify's NSM is time spent listening per active user per week (captures the core value of music discovery and enjoyment). Airbnb's NSM is nights booked (captures the core transaction that delivers value to both host and guest). Slack's NSM is messages sent per active team (captures the collaboration value that drives retention).

The NSM is supported by a small number of input metrics — three to five leading indicators that the team directly influences and that collectively predict movement in the NSM. If the NSM goes up, at least one input metric should explain why.

Why we need it

Without a North Star, product teams optimise local metrics that don't compound into business value. One squad increases signups while another team's retention drops — and there is no shared metric that makes the trade-off visible. The NSM provides a single reference point that every squad can orient toward, making cross-squad trade-offs visible and discussable.

The NSM also disciplines product conversations with leadership. Instead of "we shipped 12 features last quarter," the PM can say "the NSM moved from 4.2 to 5.1 — here are the two features that drove that movement and the two that didn't." This shifts the conversation from activity to impact, which is the only conversation worth having.

How others do it

Amplitude

Amplitude popularised the NSM framework and uses it internally: their own NSM is "weekly learning users" — users who perform at least three queries per week. This captures the core value of analytics discovery. Their product roadmap is evaluated against whether features increase weekly learning users, not against Amplitude's own revenue (which is downstream).

What we take: The NSM test: would a feature that dramatically increased revenue but didn't move the NSM be worth shipping? If yes, the NSM is wrong. The NSM must be the metric where you would sacrifice short-term revenue to improve it, because you are confident it predicts long-term retention.

Source: Amplitude — North Star Playbook

Facebook

Facebook's NSM in its growth phase was "monthly active users who returned at least 7 days in their first month." This captured social network value (connection with friends) as a leading indicator of long-term retention. The famous "10 friends in 7 days" discovery — that users who connected with 10 friends in their first 7 days were dramatically more likely to become long-term users — was a consequence of orienting growth experiments toward this NSM.

What we take: The NSM should lead teams to discover the specific behaviours that predict retention, not just measure retention itself. When input metrics for the NSM reveal specific usage patterns that correlate with the NSM, those patterns become onboarding goals.

Source: Facebook Engineering Blog

HubSpot

HubSpot uses "weekly active teams" as their NSM — a team in the product that performed at least one significant action (created a contact, ran a campaign, set a deal). They decompose this into input metrics for each product surface: marketing hub input metrics, sales hub input metrics. Quarterly product reviews are structured around NSM movement and which inputs contributed.

What we take: For multi-product companies, the NSM must still be a single number that captures the whole platform's value. Product-line metrics are inputs to the NSM, not separate North Stars. Having five North Stars is having no North Star.

Source: HubSpot Product Blog

Anatomy

The NSM itself

One metric. Captures customer value, not company activity. Moves before revenue moves. Every squad can explain how their work influences it.

Input metrics (3–5)

Leading indicators that directly predict the NSM. Each squad owns one or two input metrics. If all input metrics improve, the NSM should improve.

Counter-metrics

Metrics that must not decrease as the NSM improves. Prevents optimising the NSM at the expense of quality or retention.

Weekly review cadence

NSM and all input metrics reviewed weekly at the product leadership level. Anomalies investigated within 48 hours.

Common pitfalls

PitfallWhy it happensWhat to do instead
Revenue chosen as the NSMIt's the metric leadership cares most about.Revenue is a lagging outcome, not a customer value metric. Use a metric that moves before revenue — and that you'd sacrifice short-term revenue to improve.
Five different North Stars for five squadsEach squad wants their own metric.Squad-level metrics are inputs to the NSM, not separate North Stars. One NSM, multiple input metrics. The NSM is a company-level metric, not a squad metric.
NSM chosen but never reviewedTeams track it but don't make decisions based on it.If features ship without asking "will this move the NSM?", the metric is decorative. Make NSM impact a required field in every product brief.
