Group A · 5 practices

Customer Discovery & Intelligence

How we continuously understand customers, their struggles, and the opportunities worth solving. When this group is weak, teams build features nobody asked for, roadmaps drift from real user pain, and every product review becomes a debate about opinions rather than evidence. Strong discovery turns customer insight into a renewable resource the whole team can draw from.

Group avg score: AS-IS 0.0→0.0 / 10
01

Continuous Discovery — weekly customer touchpoints

2.0→8.0

What it is. The team conducts at least one customer interview per week, every week, without exception. Insights feed directly into an Opportunity Solution Tree so that every roadmap item traces back to a real customer opportunity rather than an internal assumption.

Why it matters. Discovery is not a phase — it is a continuous muscle. Teams that interview only at the start of a project ship products shaped by month-old assumptions. Weekly touchpoints keep the product anchored to current customer reality and compress the feedback loop from quarters to days.

How we will roll it out. Block a recurring 60-minute interview slot each week. Record sessions with consent and share a three-bullet summary in Slack by Friday. After four weeks, map insights onto an OST and make the OST the first slide in every roadmap review.

02

Jobs-to-be-Done — outcome-driven requirements

3.5→8.5

What it is. Requirements are written as jobs: "When [situation], I want to [motivation], so I can [expected outcome]." The product satisfies the job, not a demographic segment. JTBD switches the unit of analysis from users to the progress they are trying to make.

Why it matters. Features built around personas often miss the point — the persona is a proxy for the actual progress a customer seeks. JTBD finds the stable, enduring job that survives changes in technology or demographics, and that is the job worth solving well.

How we will roll it out. Rewrite the top five user stories as JTBD statements. Run two "switch interviews" with customers who recently adopted or abandoned the product. Present JTBD findings before the next sprint planning and compare to current roadmap priorities.

03

Opportunity Solution Tree — from outcome to experiment

1.5→7.5

What it is. A visual artifact that maps one desired outcome → a set of customer opportunities → potential solutions → experiments to test those solutions. Every branch is traceable. The OST makes it impossible to work on a solution that has no customer opportunity behind it.

Why it matters. Without an OST, teams jump from a vague goal to specific features with no visible path between the two. The OST surfaces assumptions ("we believe this opportunity is real") and turns them into testable hypotheses rather than decisions made in a conference room.

How we will roll it out. Draw the first OST from the current quarter's OKR key result. Populate at least three opportunity nodes from recent interview data. Present the OST at the next product review and commit to running one experiment per opportunity per sprint.

04

User Research Synthesis — from interviews to insights

4.0→8.0

What it is. A repeatable process for turning raw interview notes, usability recordings, and survey responses into structured insights. The output is an affinity diagram, an empathy map, and an insight repository with severity tags. The process runs within 48 hours of each research session.

Why it matters. Research data without synthesis is just stories. Synthesis turns individual anecdotes into patterns the entire team can act on. A shared insight repository stops the same question from being re-researched every quarter and gives PMs evidence instead of opinions in design reviews.

How we will roll it out. Set up a shared Notion database for insights with tags: severity, segment, theme. After every interview, add at least three insight cards within 48 hours. Run a monthly synthesis session to cluster and reprioritize the insight backlog.

05

Customer Journey Map — end-to-end experience

3.0→7.5

What it is. A visual map of every touchpoint a customer has with the product, from awareness through activation, engagement, and retention. Each stage shows actions, emotions, pain points, and opportunities. The map is grounded in real interview data, not internal assumptions.

Why it matters. Teams building individual features often miss the seams between steps — the places where the customer falls out of the journey or becomes frustrated. A journey map forces the team to see the whole experience and prioritize the moments that matter most to the customer.

How we will roll it out. Map the current as-is journey for the top user persona using data from five recent interviews. Highlight the top three pain points with severity scores. Pin the map to the team workspace and reference it in every sprint review.

Strategy & Roadmapping

How we set direction, communicate it, and decide what to build next. When this group is weak, teams execute tasks with no shared vision, every stakeholder pulls the roadmap in a different direction, and prioritization becomes whoever shouts loudest. Strong strategy gives the team a single coherent answer to "why are we building this?"

Group avg score: AS-IS 0.0→0.0 / 10
06

OKRs — aligned goal-setting

5.0→8.5

What it is. Objectives and Key Results: one qualitative goal (the Objective) and three to five measurable outcomes that define what success looks like (Key Results). OKRs run on a quarterly cycle. They are set bottom-up, aligned top-down, and reviewed weekly.

Why it matters. Without OKRs, teams optimize for output — shipping features — rather than outcomes — customer behavior change. OKRs shift the conversation from "did we ship it?" to "did it work?" and make every roadmap trade-off a debate about which key result it moves.

How we will roll it out. Write one draft OKR for this quarter using the "We will [Objective] as measured by [KR1], [KR2], [KR3]" format. Review with the team in a 45-minute workshop. Commit to a weekly 15-minute check-in scored 0.0–1.0 per KR.

07

Product Vision & Narrative — the northstar

4.5→9.0

What it is. A written product vision that describes the world three to five years out, the customer we serve, and the unique value we deliver. The vision is one paragraph, free of jargon, and readable by an engineer and a CFO alike. It acts as the litmus test for every major roadmap decision.

Why it matters. A team without a written vision makes every roadmap debate a power contest. With a clear vision, disagreements become "does this bring us closer to the vision?" — a productive, resolvable question. The vision also recruits talent and aligns investors without a deck.

How we will roll it out. Write a first draft using Amazon's Working Backwards template (one-page press release, five years out). Review with three stakeholders. Publish in the PM portal. Cite the vision in every roadmap document by linking to the canonical page.

08

RICE Prioritisation — scoring over gut feel

5.5→8.0

What it is. Each candidate feature is scored on four dimensions: Reach (customers per quarter), Impact (1–3 scale), Confidence (percentage), and Effort (person-weeks). RICE = (Reach × Impact × Confidence) / Effort. Items are ranked by score and discussed from the top down.

Why it matters. Gut-feel prioritization gives the highest-paid voice in the room the roadmap. RICE externalizes the assumptions behind every ranking so the team can argue about the numbers rather than the conclusions. Confidence in particular forces explicit acknowledgment of uncertainty.

How we will roll it out. RICE-score the current backlog top 20 items. Run a 90-minute calibration session to agree on Reach and Impact definitions. Commit to re-scoring monthly and posting the ranked list in the team channel before every sprint planning.

09

Now-Next-Later Roadmap — outcome over timeline

3.0→8.0

What it is. A three-column roadmap where items are sorted by proximity: Now (in progress), Next (ready to start once Now is done), and Later (directional, not committed). Each item describes an outcome, not a feature. There are no dates in the Later column.

Why it matters. Date-based roadmaps force false precision and destroy trust when dates slip. Now-Next-Later communicates direction and priority without making promises the team cannot keep. It invites stakeholders to question what is in Now and Next — where the answer is based on real evidence — rather than debating dates.

How we will roll it out. Convert the current quarterly roadmap into three columns. Remove all dates from Later. Add one-sentence outcome descriptions to each item. Publish in Notion and share with all stakeholders as the canonical source of truth for what is being worked on.

10

Working Backwards — PR/FAQ before you build

1.5→7.5

What it is. Before any significant feature is designed or estimated, the PM writes a mock press release (one page) and a FAQ (one to three pages). The press release describes the finished product as if it shipped. The FAQ answers every hard question a customer, engineer, or executive might ask. Writing comes before building.

Why it matters. The hardest problems in product development are not technical — they are clarity problems. Working Backwards forces clarity before the team sinks months into the wrong solution. If you can't write a coherent press release, the product idea isn't ready to build. Amazon uses this for every significant launch.

How we will roll it out. For the next three major roadmap items, require a PR/FAQ before design work begins. Use the standard template: headline, customer problem, key benefit, call to action, FAQ with at least five hard questions. Review in the product review meeting before proceeding.

Delivery & Collaboration

How the PM, design, and engineering teams work together to ship outcomes reliably. When this group is weak, discovery and delivery happen in separate silos, engineers build the wrong thing because requirements were unclear, and the team ships features that were never validated with customers. Strong delivery practices keep discovery and execution in sync.

Group avg score: AS-IS 0.0→0.0 / 10
11

Dual-track Agile — discovery and delivery in parallel

4.0→8.0

What it is. The team runs two parallel tracks simultaneously: a Discovery track where PMs and designers validate solutions before engineering begins, and a Delivery track where engineers build already-validated work. Only work that passed discovery enters the delivery sprint.

Why it matters. In a single-track process, engineers wait for PMs to figure out what to build, or PMs throw requirements over the wall without validation. Dual-track keeps engineers fully utilized on validated work while PMs stay two to three sprints ahead in discovery, eliminating both waste and surprises.

How we will roll it out. Designate the first half of each sprint as discovery time for the PM/design pair. Define a "discovery done" criteria: customer tested, confidence ≥ 70%, acceptance criteria written. Only work meeting this bar enters sprint planning. Review the ratio at retrospectives.

12

User Story Mapping — build shared understanding

4.5→8.0

What it is. A wall-sized map where user activities form the horizontal spine and user tasks hang vertically beneath each activity. The team draws a horizontal line for each release, showing which tasks are in scope. The map is built collaboratively and stays visible in the team space throughout the quarter.

Why it matters. A flat backlog hides context — it is impossible to see the user journey from a list of user stories. A story map restores narrative: the team can see at a glance which part of the experience is being improved, what is being left out deliberately, and what a minimum viable release looks like.

How we will roll it out. Run a half-day story mapping workshop for the next major feature. Invite PM, design, engineering, and QA. Use sticky notes on a wall or Miro. Define the walking skeleton release on the first horizontal cut. Commit to maintaining the map through the delivery cycle.

13

Impact Mapping — connect goals to deliverables

2.0→7.5

What it is. A mind-map style artifact with four levels: Why (the goal), Who (the actors who can affect it), How (the behavior changes needed), and What (the deliverables that create those changes). Impact maps are generated in a two-hour workshop and reviewed before roadmap commits.

Why it matters. Impact mapping makes the chain of assumptions between a goal and a feature explicit. If the "How" assumption is wrong ("increasing email open rate won't actually drive activation"), the "What" is waste. The map forces the team to validate each link in the chain before committing engineering resources.

How we will roll it out. Run a 90-minute impact mapping session for this quarter's primary OKR. Identify the top two actors and the one behavior change that would most move the KR. Challenge the team to find one assumption per "How" node and identify how to test it in under two weeks.

14

Product Brief / PRD — lightweight written spec

6.0→8.5

What it is. A one-to-three page document written by the PM before engineering begins. It covers: the problem being solved, the target customer, success metrics, scope (in and out), key design decisions, and open questions. It is a living document reviewed weekly during the delivery cycle.

Why it matters. Engineers who start without a brief make implicit product decisions throughout the build — and many of those decisions differ from what the PM intended. A brief externalizes those decisions before engineering begins, reduces mid-sprint surprises, and creates a shared artifact the team can hold the PM accountable to.

How we will roll it out. Add a brief template to the team Notion. Require a brief for any work estimated at more than three days. Enforce a "no brief, no sprint entry" rule. Review briefs in a 30-minute weekly PM sync and track open questions to resolution.

15

Definition of Ready & Done — shared quality bar

5.0→8.0

What it is. Two explicit checklists. Definition of Ready: the criteria a story must meet before it can enter a sprint (brief written, acceptance criteria clear, design approved, dependencies identified). Definition of Done: the criteria that must be true before an item is considered complete (tests written, analytics instrumented, docs updated).

Why it matters. Without a shared Definition of Done, engineers ship code and PMs declare features "done" before analytics are wired or edge cases are handled. Without a Definition of Ready, engineers start work on unclear stories and waste time in mid-sprint clarification loops. Both lists cut waste and rework.

How we will roll it out. Co-create both definitions in a 60-minute team workshop. Post them in the team Jira board description and the sprint planning template. Review compliance at each retrospective. Update the definitions when the team identifies a pattern of rework caused by a missing criterion.

Metrics & Experimentation

How we measure product health, run evidence-based experiments, and make decisions from data rather than opinion. When this group is weak, teams debate which features to build instead of testing them, success is declared at launch rather than at outcome, and the same flawed assumptions persist quarter after quarter. Strong measurement practices turn the team into a learning machine.

Group avg score: AS-IS 0.0→0.0 / 10
16

North Star Metric — single key value signal

3.0→8.5

What it is. One metric that best captures the value the product delivers to customers. It sits at the top of a metric tree: the NSM is decomposed into three to five input metrics, each of which can be influenced by specific product areas. The NSM is reviewed weekly by the full team.

Why it matters. Without a shared north star, each team optimizes for its own local metric — conversion, support tickets, uptime — and those metrics can all improve while the product loses its core value. The NSM aligns the whole organization on one definition of winning and makes every feature proposal answer the question "how does this move the NSM?"

How we will roll it out. Workshop the NSM with PM, engineering leads, and design in 90 minutes: list five candidate metrics, score each on "does it reflect customer value?" and "can the team influence it?". Pick one, decompose into input metrics, and add all to the weekly dashboard. Review the first data cut in the next product review.

17

HEART Framework — UX quality at scale

1.5→7.5

What it is. Google's framework for measuring user experience across five dimensions: Happiness, Engagement, Adoption, Retention, and Task success. The team selects the two or three dimensions most relevant to their product area and defines Goals, Signals, and Metrics for each.

Why it matters. A/B tests measure specific feature impact but miss the cumulative UX quality of the whole product. HEART gives teams a structured vocabulary for measuring experience quality across diverse products — from a single flow to an entire platform — and makes UX quality a first-class team metric alongside business KPIs.

How we will roll it out. Select the two HEART dimensions most relevant to the current product area. Define one Goal, two Signals, and one Metric per dimension using the GSM table format. Add the metrics to the weekly dashboard. Review at the end of Q and decide whether to adjust the dimensions next quarter.

18

A/B Testing — evidence over opinion

3.5→8.5

What it is. A controlled experiment where users are randomly assigned to a control group (current experience) or one or more treatment groups (changed experience). Statistical significance is calculated before the experiment ends. The team commits to following the data regardless of prior conviction.

Why it matters. Most product intuitions are wrong. Amazon, Netflix, and Booking.com run thousands of experiments per year because each one discovers something that would have been impossible to predict in a conference room. Without A/B testing, the highest-paid opinion always wins — and that opinion is frequently incorrect.

How we will roll it out. Identify one live feature where the team disagrees on whether a change would help. Run a 50/50 split test for two weeks with a pre-registered hypothesis and primary metric. Share results in the next all-hands. Establish a standard experiment template in the PM Notion.

19

Funnel & Cohort Analytics — retention and activation

4.0→8.5

What it is. Funnel analysis tracks conversion from one step to the next (signup → activation → first value → retention). Cohort analysis groups users by the week or month they joined and tracks their behavior over time, revealing whether product improvements actually increase retention for new cohorts.

Why it matters. A product with a leaky funnel and invisible retention problems will grow until it runs out of new users to acquire and then collapse. Funnel analysis finds the drop-off points worth fixing. Cohort analysis proves whether the fixes are working over time — the only metric that cannot be gamed by an acquisition spike.

How we will roll it out. Build the activation funnel in Amplitude or Mixpanel this week. Identify the single step with the highest drop-off rate. Add a cohort retention chart to the weekly PM dashboard. Set a target retention rate improvement for the next quarter and track it weekly.

20

Product Health Dashboard — real-time signal board

2.0→7.5

What it is. A single-page dashboard visible to the whole team showing the NSM, input metrics, retention, error rate, and support ticket volume — updated daily. Each metric has a target and a traffic-light status. The dashboard is the first thing reviewed at the weekly team meeting.

Why it matters. Teams without a shared dashboard operate on lagging information — metrics from last sprint, or from the quarterly review. A daily dashboard catches regressions within 24 hours of a release, surfaces silent churn before it becomes a crisis, and aligns the team on current reality without a reporting meeting.

How we will roll it out. Build the dashboard in Amplitude, Grafana, or Notion in a two-day sprint. Agree on five metrics and their weekly targets. Display it on the team TV or pin it as a tab in every browser. Open each weekly team meeting with a three-minute dashboard walkthrough.

21

Product Review Cadence — structured decision meetings

4.0→8.0

What it is. A recurring weekly meeting (60 minutes) where the PM presents current metrics, active experiments, and upcoming roadmap decisions to engineering leads and stakeholders. Every item requires a pre-read distributed 24 hours in advance. Decisions are documented before the meeting ends.

Why it matters. Without a structured review cadence, critical product decisions happen in ad-hoc Slack threads or are deferred indefinitely. Amazon runs weekly Business Reviews for this reason: a known venue for hard decisions creates accountability and a predictable rhythm that stakeholders can plan around.

How we will roll it out. Schedule the first Product Review for next week. Use a fixed agenda: five minutes on metrics, 20 minutes on active decisions, 30 minutes on roadmap, five minutes on blockers. Distribute a written pre-read the day before. Capture all decisions in a shared Notion doc within two hours.

22

Stakeholder Alignment — demos, reviews, 1:1s

5.5→8.5

What it is. A structured set of rituals — monthly stakeholder demo, written update every two weeks, and monthly 1:1 with each key stakeholder — that keeps decision-makers informed and gives the PM early warning of changing priorities or new constraints before they become blockers.

Why it matters. The most common reason good products fail internally is not technical debt or bad design — it is stakeholder misalignment that hardens into political opposition at the worst possible moment. Regular, structured communication converts potential opponents into informed sponsors who feel ownership over the roadmap.

How we will roll it out. Map stakeholders by Power/Interest. Set up a monthly 30-minute demo for high-power stakeholders. Write a two-week status update in a consistent format: three bullets on progress, one risk, one ask. Start monthly 1:1s with the two most influential stakeholders immediately.

23

Go-to-Market Playbook — launch coordination

3.0→8.0

What it is. A written playbook that the PM owns and every team contributes to before a significant feature launch. It covers: launch goals, target segments, pricing and packaging, messaging hierarchy, sales enablement, support training, rollout plan (percentage-based), and success metrics with a 30-day review date.

Why it matters. Technical readiness and product readiness are not the same as market readiness. Features shipped without coordinated GTM often fail not because the product is bad but because customers don't know it exists, support teams give wrong answers, and sales leads with no materials. GTM turns a ship into a launch.

How we will roll it out. Create a GTM playbook template in Notion. For the next significant feature, require the playbook to be complete 10 days before launch. Run a GTM readiness review with sales, marketing, support, and PM one week before. Assign DRI for each section of the playbook.

24

Product Postmortem — outcome retrospective

2.0→7.5

What it is. A structured retrospective run four to eight weeks after a major launch, after metrics have stabilized. The PM leads the team through four questions: What did we commit to achieve? What did we actually achieve? What did we get right? What would we do differently? Action items are numbered and assigned.

Why it matters. Teams that don't run postmortems repeat the same mistakes: overconfident estimates, ignored early signals, and launch criteria set by engineers rather than customers. The postmortem is the mechanism for converting expensive learning into institutional memory that prevents the next team from paying the same tuition.

How we will roll it out. Run the first postmortem for the most recent significant feature using a fixed template. Invite PM, engineering lead, designer, and two customers (via recorded call clips). Timebox to 90 minutes. Publish the output in the PM Notion and link it from the original brief.

25

Decision RACI — accountability for product choices

3.5→7.5

What it is. A RACI matrix that defines who is Responsible, Accountable, Consulted, and Informed for the core product decisions: roadmap priorities, scope cuts, go/no-go launches, pricing changes, and major UX rewrites. The matrix is reviewed quarterly and embedded in the team charter.

Why it matters. In fast-moving product teams, the cost of ambiguous decision ownership is enormous: decisions stall because nobody owns them, or get made twice because two people thought they were both accountable. A clear RACI eliminates these coordination taxes and lets the team move at startup speed without organizational chaos.

How we will roll it out. List the ten most frequent and impactful product decisions. Map each against PM, engineering lead, design lead, and CPO. Find any cell where two people are both marked A — that is the most expensive ambiguity to fix first. Publish the matrix and link it from the team charter.
