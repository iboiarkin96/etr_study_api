Designing DX. My experience.

Several months of building a documentation portal for the ETR Study App API — what I thought the work was, what it actually turned out to be, and a rubric for what mature documentation includes at each level.

I have been building a documentation portal for the ETR Study App API for several months. I had a backend service that deserved a credible front door, I knew HTML/CSS/Markdown well enough, and I had read enough portals to think I knew what good ones do. That was the premise. It turned out to be wrong in a specific way, and the wrongness is what this post is about.

The short version: documentation is not mostly a design problem with a bit of writing at the end. It is closer to the opposite. The visible portal — fonts, navigation, search, breadcrumbs — is the easy part. The hard part is the content the portal delivers: the architecture story, the real trade-offs behind each decision, the runbooks/specs/ADRs people have actually used, and the reading paths that send different users to different first pages. I spent month on the easy part before I noticed the hard part existed.

Here is what I eventually came up with after going down that path. It covers ten dimensions, each one asking a question a mature portal should answer but that most portals do not. For every dimension I lay out three maturity levels — A, B, and C — and what each level looks like in practice.

The vocabulary

Each dimension has three consistent levels. Across all ten dimensions, the grades mean the same thing:

A

Mature. The artefact exists, is complete, and has been tested in real situations — incidents, audits, onboarding, and growth.

B

In progress. The artefact has the right structure, but parts of its content are still incomplete. The skeleton is in place; some sections are thin.

C

Absent. The artefact is missing, or it exists as an empty template, or it is so thin that a senior reader cannot tell whether the work behind it has been done.

Ten dimensions of mature documentation

The dimensions below are not invented. I took them from the portals that experienced readers call useful, not just polished in terms of design. Each dimension is a question, and a mature portal has a clear answer to each one.

1 — Architectural narrative

The question. Can the reader understand why the system is built this way and not some other way from a single page? Does the portal tell a clear story that links each decision to the constraint behind it?

A

Mature

One top-level page tells the story of how the system reached its current shape, in the order the constraints appeared. Each major module has its own narrative page that continues the same story. Every important decision is linked to the constraint that caused it.

B

In progress

A top-level architecture story exists and reads well, but per-module stories are incomplete or missing. Constraints are often implicit, so the reader has to guess at the «why» instead of finding it written down.

C

Absent

Decisions are scattered across many ADRs and README files, with no single story to connect them. The reader has to build the picture from twenty separate pages, and usually does not try.

What does «architectural narrative» look like in the industry?

Public examples worth opening:

  • CockroachDB Architecture — a multi-page narrative that walks from a system overview through replication, distribution, transactions, and the SQL layer. Each page names the constraints behind the design.
  • GitLab Handbook — the entire company handbook is public. Engineering and infrastructure pages open with the rationale before the rules, so a new reader can reconstruct the «why» without asking anyone.
  • Backstage Architecture Decisions — Spotify's open ADR log, linked from the architecture pages it shaped. A direct example of «every decision is reachable from the architecture story».
  • Stripe engineering blog — long-form posts on idempotency, online migrations at scale, and rate-limiting. Each post reads as a single-decision narrative with the trade-off named honestly, not as an announcement.
  • Netflix Tech Blog — recurring «How we redesigned X» pieces (Mantis, Hollow, the playback pipeline) follow the same constraint → options → choice → consequences arc.
What is hard about it?
  • Keeping the narrative in sync with the code. Every architectural change should travel with an update to the story. If it does not, the page becomes a museum — confident-sounding and wrong. The pre-commit gate (something like docs-check) catches autogen drift but cannot catch a narrative that quietly stops matching reality.
  • Naming the constraints honestly. The lazy version is «we chose X because X is best». The honest version is «we chose X because we had no time to operate Y, and accepted cost Z». The second version is harder to write and much more useful six months later.
  • Per-module stories drift. Each service owner has to keep their architecture page current. Under pressure to ship, this is the first thing skipped — and the gap is invisible until a new reader hits it.
  • Vocabulary cohesion. The top-level story and each per-service story must share the same named constraints and the same terminology. If they do not, the reader sees three different systems instead of one.
  • It is not a writing task, it is a thinking task. Writing «why this and not that» forces you to admit you did not always know the answer. Most of the work is the thinking; the prose is the cheap part.
What do I have right now? Honest self-grade.

My narrative layer is built around two pieces:

  • One top-level architecture-narrative page that walks the reader from journey → what the system is → why → shape → what we did not build → trade-offs → lessons → next step.
  • Per-service architecture pages that continue the same story for each module, with most decisions linked to the ADR that owns them.

Current grade: B — In progress. What keeps it from A: per-service pages are uneven in depth, and a few constraints are still implicit rather than written.

2 — Trade-offs exposure

The question. Are the rejected options visible on the page? Are their real costs stated honestly? Does the reader see the option that was not chosen and the reason for that — or only the final answer?

A

Mature

Every decision page lists the alternatives that were considered, with an honest cost for each one (latency, complexity, vendor lock-in, operations effort). The page also states the specific cost of the chosen option. A reader six months later can review the decision without doing the analysis again.

B

In progress

Alternatives are listed, but only briefly, without a real cost analysis. The downsides of the chosen option are hidden or removed. The reader can guess at the trade-offs but cannot verify them.

C

Absent

Decisions are presented as final answers. Alternatives are not shown. The decision page reads as «we chose X», not as «we chose X over Y because of Z, and accepted cost W».

What does «trade-offs exposure» look like in the industry?

The pattern: every decision document has a section that names the alternatives, attaches an honest cost to each, and states the loss the chosen option accepts. The reader does not have to re-run the analysis to understand the choice. The packaging differs (RFC, PEP, ADR, design doc), the shape is the same: option A vs. B vs. C, each with named costs → choice → cost we accept.

Public examples worth opening:

  • Rust RFCs — every RFC has explicit «Drawbacks», «Rationale and alternatives», and «Unresolved questions» sections. Decisions are not approved without them.
  • Python PEPs — each PEP includes «Rejected ideas» and «Open issues»; mature PEPs trace how alternatives were eliminated.
  • Kubernetes Enhancement Proposals (KEPs) — «Alternatives considered» is a required section; KEPs without it do not advance.
  • joelparkerhenderson/architecture-decision-record — a curated collection of ADR templates and real examples, including Michael Nygard's original «context · decision · consequences» shape.
What is hard about it?
  • Naming alternatives honestly, not as straw-men. The lazy version is «we considered X but X was bad». The honest version makes the rejected option strong enough that a reader could disagree with the choice.
  • Stating the cost of the chosen option. It is easier to celebrate the win than to write «we accept latency overhead Y and operations effort Z». The cost is the part the next reader needs most.
  • Going back to update «consequences». A decision page becomes useful six months later only if someone returns and writes what actually happened. Almost nobody does.
  • «Obvious» decisions. When a decision feels obvious, alternatives don't get written. Six months later it stops being obvious and there is no record of why.
  • Avoiding revisionism. Rewriting an old ADR to «sound smarter» destroys its value. The discipline is to leave the original honest and add a follow-up.
What do I have right now? Honest self-grade.

My trade-off layer is built around two pieces:

  • 34 numbered ADRs in the ADR catalog; every one has at least two rejected alternatives with concrete costs plus a closing «trade we accept by choosing X» line.
  • Pages that miss the shape fail the docs-check gate before commit.

Current grade: A− — close to A. What keeps it from A: the «consequences» sections are not consistently revisited after time has passed.

3 — Diátaxis discipline

The question. Are the four content types — tutorial, how-to, reference, explanation — actually kept separate? Does every page belong to one of them, without mixing into the others? Are the links between types complete (every tutorial points to the related references; every reference points to the related how-tos)?

A

Mature

Every page declares its type clearly. A template exists for each type and is used. The links between types are complete: references point to the how-tos that use them, and tutorials cite the explanations they depend on. Reviewers catch any mixing of types.

B

In progress

The team knows the four types and most pages belong to one of them, but the type is often not declared. Templates exist but are not always followed. A few pages mix types — reference pages with how-to steps, tutorials that turn into explanations.

C

Absent

Pages mix types without warning. No one follows templates. The reader cannot tell whether a page is a tutorial to follow, a how-to to use during an incident, or an explanation to read for understanding.

What does «Diátaxis discipline» look like in the industry?

The pattern: every page belongs to exactly one of four types (tutorial, how-to, reference, explanation). Each type has its own template. Cross-quadrant loops are closed — references cite the how-tos that use them; tutorials cite the explanations they assume. Mixed pages are caught at review, not after.

Public examples worth opening:

  • Diátaxis — the framework itself, by Daniele Procida. Reading the four quadrant pages is the cheapest way to internalise the model.
  • Django docs — the redesign Procida led; tutorials, topic guides, reference, and how-to are clearly separate sections of the same docs site.
  • Vue.js docs — explicit «Guide», «Tutorial», and «API Reference» entry points, each with its own shape.
  • Astro docs — recent and aggressively Diátaxis-aligned; a good example of the model applied to a young project.
  • MDN Web Docs — at massive scale, the Learn / Reference split holds. A good demonstration that the model survives 10×.
What is hard about it?
  • Pages drift between quadrants over time. A reference page starts accumulating how-to steps because «it was easier here». By the third commit, the type label is wrong.
  • Templates exist but get skipped under pressure. When the deadline is tight, the page goes up without the template; the type drift starts there.
  • Cross-quadrant links rot. A how-to cites a reference; the reference is renamed; nobody updates the how-to. Six months later the loop is open.
  • Folder name and quadrant disagree. A folder called reference/ starts hosting Explanation-shaped pages. The rule «folder = quadrant» quietly stops being true.
  • Detection is human. A page declaring data-page-type="explanation" but reading as a how-to passes every CI gate. Only reviewer attention catches this — the most expensive check in the system.
What do I have right now? Honest self-grade.

My Diátaxis layer is built around three pieces:

  • Every page declares its type with data-page-type="tutorial|how-to|reference|explanation" in the <body>.
  • Per-quadrant templates live in Templates catalog and are linked from authoring how-tos.

Current grade: B+ — close to A in three quadrants. What keeps it from A overall: some defects remain across pages and the foundations/reference/sa/ folder still holds a few pages whose location can be improved.

4 — Operational maturity

The question. Are there real runbooks, real postmortems, and a real burn-rate history for the SLOs? Or are the runbooks empty templates and the SLOs targets that no incident has ever tested?

A

Mature

Runbooks have been used during real incidents and improved afterwards. A library of completed postmortems exists, and each postmortem links back to the runbook page that produced it. SLO dashboards show burn-rate history, not just target values. The reader can see at a glance that the team has been on call.

B

In progress

Runbooks have the right structure — preconditions, actions, recovery steps, escalation — and a few have been used in real situations. Some postmortems have been started. SLOs are defined, and burn-rate dashboards exist, but the history is short.

C

Absent

Runbooks are empty templates. There are no completed postmortems. SLOs are target numbers with no measurement history. A senior reader cannot tell whether the team has ever been paged, and will assume the answer is no.

What does «operational maturity» look like in the industry?

The pattern: runbooks have actually been used; each one carries the date it was last walked. Postmortems link back to the runbook page they came from. SLO dashboards show burn-rate history, not just target values. The reader can tell, in a glance, that the team has been on call and learned from it.

Public examples worth opening:

  • Google SRE Workbook — chapters with worked examples of runbooks, error-budget policy, and on-call practices. The companion to the SRE book, but more operational.
  • GitLab Infrastructure Handbook — public runbooks, incident reviews, and SLO definitions for a production company.
  • Cloudflare postmortems — long-form incident reports published after major outages. A model for how to talk about failure in public.
  • AWS Service Health Dashboard — public SLO surface with incident history per service.
  • danluu/post-mortems — a curated index of public postmortems across the industry; useful as a reading list of «what real ones look like».
What is hard about it?
  • Runbooks are aspirational until used. A page that has never been touched during a real incident is fiction, no matter how clean it looks.
  • Postmortems need psychological safety and time. A blameless culture is a precondition, and writing a good postmortem takes a day, not an hour.
  • Closing the loop. Every postmortem should update the runbook it came from. If the postmortem is filed and the runbook stays unchanged, the next incident repeats.
  • SLO burn-rate history needs infrastructure. The dashboard is the easy part; collecting the data, tagging it, and keeping it for months is the hard part.
  • Drift between runbook and prod. A runbook written six months ago references a script that no longer exists. The fix is a regular re-drill, not a one-time write-up.
What do I have right now? Honest self-grade.

My ops layer is built around two pieces:

  • SRE runbooks with the right shape (preconditions, actions, recovery, escalation) — none have been walked in a real production incident yet.
  • SLO and observability policies set in their own ADRs; burn-rate dashboards exist but the history is short.

Current grade: C+ — heading to B. What keeps it from B: no real incidents yet (single-author project), so no completed postmortems and no live burn-rate signal.

5 — Contracts as first-class

The question. Are the API specifications complete in every part — endpoints, error contract, observability hooks, test plan? Are data models written down clearly? When several services interact, is the cross-service contract documented on the page?

A

Mature

Every endpoint includes the full set of sections: request and response schema, error contract, observability hooks (which log events appear, which metrics change, which alerts fire), and a test plan (unit, integration, contract, end-to-end). Data models are written down explicitly. Cross-service contracts have their own page, with a versioning and deprecation policy.

B

In progress

The OpenAPI structure is in place and the data model is documented, but many endpoints have empty observability and test-plan sections. Cross-service contracts are implied but not collected on a single page.

C

Absent

There is only an endpoint list, possibly a Swagger dump. No error contract, no observability hooks, no test plan. Data models live only in the code. Contracts between services exist only in people's heads.

What does «contracts as first-class» look like in the industry?

The pattern: every endpoint page carries the full envelope. Request and response schema. Error contract. Observability hooks (log events, metrics, alerts). Test plan (unit, integration, contract, end-to-end). Data models are explicit. Cross-service contracts get their own page with versioning and deprecation policy.

Public examples worth opening:

  • Stripe API reference — the canonical example. Each endpoint shows the full envelope, with versioned changelogs and a documented deprecation policy.
  • GitHub REST API — explicit versioning (REST vs. GraphQL), error responses, and rate-limit headers documented per endpoint.
  • Twilio docs — webhook contracts treated as first-class; retry, signing, and idempotency semantics on every endpoint.
  • AsyncAPI — the OpenAPI equivalent for event-driven systems. Useful for teams whose contracts are not all HTTP.
  • Google API Design Guide — opinionated reference on contract shape, naming, versioning, and error models.
What is hard about it?
  • The observability + test-plan sections quietly stay empty. The schema is fun to write; the «which alert fires when this endpoint regresses» line is not. The empty section reads identical to a populated one until the reader looks.
  • Schema drift from code. The spec was right at v1; by v3 the code has moved and the spec has not. Contract tests catch some drift but not all.
  • Cross-service contracts. A contract that lives between two services tends to live in neither — it ends up tribal until someone explicitly assigns it a home page.
  • Versioning and deprecation policy. Easy to write once, hard to follow under pressure. Without a written policy, every deprecation becomes a debate.
  • Error contract. Most teams document the success shape and leave error shapes to «whatever HTTP returns». A real error contract takes deliberate effort to define and enforce.
What do I have right now? Honest self-grade.

My contract layer is built around two pieces:

  • OpenAPI spec generated from code and validated in CI; error contract, versioning, and idempotency policies set in their own ADRs.
  • Every endpoint spec has §14 (observability hooks) and §15 (test plan) slots, enforced by a lint script.

Current grade: B — In progress. What keeps it from A: §14 and §15 are filled in for new endpoints but uneven across the older surface, and cross-service contracts are not yet on a single consolidated page.

6 — Quality of decision records

The question. Do ADRs and RFCs read as decisions made under specific pressure — or as final answers presented later? Can a reader review a decision six months later without doing the analysis again?

A

Mature

Every ADR opens with the constraint that forced the decision, and lists the alternatives considered, with an honest cost for each one. Consequences are tracked over time — the «six months later» update is on the page. Each ADR has a date and an owner, and is linked from the architecture page it affects.

B

In progress

ADRs are written, dated, and use the standard structure, but the alternatives sections are thin and consequences are not always tracked. The frame is solid; the core content needs more honesty.

C

Absent

ADRs have the right shape but read as final answers. The constraints are missing. The alternatives are missing. The consequences are not recorded. A reader cannot tell why this option and not the other one.

What does «quality of decision records» look like in the industry?

The pattern: each ADR opens with the constraint that forced the decision, names the alternatives with honest costs, declares the choice, and reserves a section for consequences that gets updated over time. Every record is dated, owned, and has an explicit lifecycle state (Proposed / Accepted / Superseded). The format is small enough to be cheap to write and stable enough to be re-read years later.

Public examples worth opening:

  • adr.github.io — the canonical hub for the ADR format, with template variants (Nygard, MADR, full) and adoption notes.
  • joelparkerhenderson/architecture-decision-record — a large curated collection of real-world ADRs and templates across many companies.
  • AWS Prescriptive Guidance — ADRs — Amazon's published guide on how they write and review ADRs internally.
  • Rust RFCs — heavier than ADRs, but the same idea: every decision carries its alternatives, drawbacks, and unresolved questions, all in public.
  • ThoughtWorks Technology Radar — short-form decision records at the technology-choice level (Adopt / Trial / Assess / Hold), kept current twice a year.
What is hard about it?
  • «Consequences» needs time. The section can only be honestly filled in months after the decision. Without a reminder mechanism, it stays empty forever.
  • Constraints fade. Six months later you remember the choice but not the pressure that forced it. If the constraint was not written down then, it is gone.
  • Ownership when reality changes. When the world moves and the decision is no longer right, someone has to decide whether to update, supersede, or accept the divergence. Without an owner, nothing happens.
  • Lifecycle discipline. «Accepted» is easy to mark; «Superseded by ADR 0027» requires you to remember to go back and link it. The graph of decisions rots without explicit care.
  • Resisting revisionism. Rewriting an old ADR to sound smarter destroys its value. The discipline is to leave the original honest and add a follow-up record.
What do I have right now? Honest self-grade.

My decision-record layer is built around two pieces:

  • 34 numbered ADRs in the ADR catalog, each with the constraint → alternatives → consequences shape.
  • A lifecycle policy and a clickable status pill, so the state of every ADR is visible at a glance.

Current grade: B — In progress. What keeps it from A: the «consequences» sections are still thin on older ADRs, and the periodic re-read («is this still right?») is not yet on a calendar.

7 — Performance · capacity · security as design forces

The question. Are non-functional concerns visible on the design pages — not as a side note but as forces that shaped the design? Capacity projections, security defaults, threat models?

A

Mature

Non-functional concerns are treated as forces, not as appendices. A capacity plan exists — for example, «at what scale does the current schema break, and what is the next step?» A threat model with named adversary levels (script kiddie, motivated insider, nation-state level) shapes the security pages. SLOs are linked to specific drills.

B

In progress

SLO numbers exist (latency, error budget). Security defaults are written down in ADRs and runbooks. A capacity plan and a named threat model are still missing — the team's security posture lives in defaults, not in an analysis.

C

Absent

Non-functional concerns appear as a checklist — «we use TLS, we have rate limits» — with no link to how the system was actually designed. No capacity projections. No threat model.

What does «design forces» look like in the industry?

The pattern: performance, capacity, and security are not appendices to the design — they are forces that shape it. Capacity plans say at what scale the current shape breaks and what the next step is. Threat models name adversary tiers (script kiddie, motivated insider, nation-state level) and trace each defence back to a specific tier. SLOs are connected to drills that test them.

Public examples worth opening:

  • AWS Well-Architected Framework — six pillars (operational excellence, security, reliability, performance efficiency, cost optimisation, sustainability) treated as design forces with explicit questions per pillar.
  • Google SRE book — capacity planning, SLOs, and error budgets as first-class design inputs, with worked examples.
  • Microsoft Threat Modeling (STRIDE) — Microsoft's canonical method for structured threat models, with a free tool that walks the analysis.
  • OWASP Threat Dragon — open-source threat-modelling tool, lighter-weight than STRIDE for teams that want to start.
  • Netflix Chaos Engineering posts — long-form pieces showing how a real team built drills that exercise the failure modes their SLOs depend on.
What is hard about it?
  • Capacity is hypothetical until you hit it. Projections are guesses; the only honest plan names the trigger and the next-step shape, not the absolute number.
  • Threat models age fast. Adversaries and integrations both change; a threat model written a year ago is half-fiction unless someone re-runs it.
  • Security defaults are not security analysis. «We use TLS, we hash passwords with bcrypt» is hygiene; the analysis is «here is the adversary we are designing against and why these specific defaults block them».
  • Connecting non-functional reqs to design choices. The SLO and the schema choice should both point to the same constraint. Most docs name them in different sections and never link.
  • Drills cost time and require coordination. A drill that is never rehearsed is a runbook in disguise. Real drills need calendar slots and review afterwards.
What do I have right now? Honest self-grade.

My non-functional layer is built around two pieces:

  • SLO targets, security defaults, dependency hygiene, and observability shape captured in dedicated ADRs.
  • A single capacity snapshot for the API service — one moment in time, not a series.

Current grade: C+ — heading to B. What keeps it from B: no explicit threat model with named adversary tiers, capacity is a snapshot rather than a plan with a trigger, and SLO drills are not yet on a calendar.

8 — Tooling-as-documentation

The question. Does the tooling document itself? Are the Make targets self-describing? Can someone discover the workflow without reading separate documentation? Does the same tooling also catch documentation drift?

A

Mature

make help is the entry point, and every target describes itself. Workflows are discoverable through the tooling, not through README files. A docs-check-style check runs on every PR and blocks merges when pages are stale or out of sync with autogenerated content. Once the tooling is in place, it stays correct because drift is caught by the same checks.

B

In progress

Some self-documentation exists. Most workflows are reachable through Make. A few scripts still require reading the source code to understand what they do. A drift-catching check exists but covers only part of the portal.

C

Absent

The tooling is hard to understand. Workflows live in people's heads or in scattered README files. Documentation drift is caught only by reviewers, not by CI — if it is caught at all.

What does «tooling-as-documentation» look like in the industry?

The pattern: the tooling explains itself. There is one entry-point command («help», «list», «cargo --help») that lists every workflow with a one-line description. Drift between tooling and documentation is caught by the same tooling, not by reviewers. A new contributor discovers the workflow through the CLI, not through a README file that is six months out of date.

Public examples worth opening:

  • just — a Make-alternative that treats every recipe as self-documenting; just --list is the canonical entry point.
  • Cargo (Rust) — every subcommand documents itself through cargo <cmd> --help; the help text is the contract.
  • Backstage TechDocs — Spotify's «docs-like-code» pattern where every service's docs live next to its code and are built by the same CI.
  • dbt — analytics tooling that auto-generates documentation from the same schema definitions that drive transformations.
  • asdf — a runtime-version manager whose plugin model makes the available toolchains discoverable through the CLI itself.
What is hard about it?
  • «make help» becomes outdated. A new target is added and the help line is forgotten. Without a check that fails on missing help, the help drifts silently.
  • Drift between Make and CI. The Make pipeline runs locally; CI runs a slightly different sequence; the two diverge. The only fix is making CI call the same Make targets.
  • The tooling itself needs documentation. «How does docs-fix work, step by step?» is a meta-meta question. Auto-generated docs cannot answer it; a real explanation has to be written and maintained.
  • Drift gates only catch what they were taught. A new kind of drift (a new auto-generated artefact) needs a new check, or it slips through.
  • Auto-generated docs can mask quality issues. A pdoc page covering a half-built module looks complete; the page exists, but the substance does not.
What do I have right now? Honest self-grade.

My tooling layer is built around two pieces:

  • make help at the repo and portal roots — every target has a one-line description.
  • make docs-check drift gate, run in CI and pre-commit, with sub-checks for HTML, design, feedback, spec, and sidebar coverage.

Current grade: B+ — close to A. What keeps it from A: a few scripts still need source-reading to fully understand, and there is no narrative explainer of the pipeline as a whole.

9 — IA scalability

The question. Will the information architecture still work at ten times the current size? Is there a per-service structure that will not need to be reorganised when a second service is added? Are the taxonomies clear and limited?

A

Mature

The IA scales to 10× its current size without reorganisation. A per-service structure follows the patterns shared between services. Taxonomies (page type, owner, lifecycle) are explicit and limited. New services and new domains can be added without changing the top level.

B

In progress

The IA works at the current scale and is known to need reorganisation at the next scale step. The team has a plan for that reorganisation and a clear trigger for when to start it.

C

Absent

The IA is ad-hoc. Taxonomies are not limited. Every new service adds noise to the top level. Reorganisation happens only as a reaction to pressure.

What does «IA scalability» look like in the industry?

The pattern: the information architecture has a spine that does not change when a new service is added. Per-service structures follow shared templates so the reader's path is the same in each module. Taxonomies (page type, ownership, lifecycle) are explicit and bounded — there is a fixed list, not a growing one. The team knows the trigger that would force the next reorganisation, before that trigger fires.

Public examples worth opening:

  • Backstage Software Catalog — a bounded entity model (Component, System, Domain, API, Resource) that lets the IA scale to thousands of services without reshaping the top level.
  • Diátaxis — four-quadrant content model that holds at every scale because the quadrants themselves are fixed and exhaustive.
  • GOV.UK Design System — a documentation IA that has survived for years across hundreds of government services; the patterns / components / styles split is the spine.
  • Django docs — Diátaxis applied to a 20-year-old project; the same four-section IA has absorbed Django's growth without reorganisation.
  • Wikipedia category system — an extreme stress test: millions of pages, a bounded taxonomy model, drift handled by community process.
What is hard about it?
  • New services add noise to the top level. Without a per-service spine, each new service creates a new top-level entry and the sidebar grows linearly.
  • Taxonomies grow unbounded. Page types, tags, lifecycle states — each one tends to grow by accretion. A bounded taxonomy needs explicit policy and review.
  • Reorganisation is reactive. The trigger to reorganise («we crossed 500 pages») is rarely defined in advance; reorganisation happens only after pain.
  • Per-service spine drifts. Three services start with the same template; six months later they each have their own structure. The cost lands on the reader who switches services.
  • The audit costs time. Finding the actual shape of the IA (vs. the intended shape) requires walking every page. Without a periodic audit, the gap is invisible.
What do I have right now? Honest self-grade.

My IA layer is built around two pieces:

  • A full IA audit done in June 2026, with a Diátaxis-spine target chosen and a migration playbook in place.
  • Bounded taxonomies on every page (page-type, service, lifecycle, role), enforced by a lint check; a sidebar coverage check fails CI when a new page falls outside the IA.

Current grade: B — In progress. What keeps it from A: the migration is planned but not finished, and the trigger for the next reorganisation is known but not yet written as a contract.

10 — Cold-start productivity

The question. Can a new senior reader become productive in one day by reading only the portal? Is there a clear, curated path — «read these N pages, in this order, in this much time» — for at least the main user personas?

A

Mature

Each main persona has its own curated path: a senior architect doing a cold-start review, a software engineer shipping their first endpoint, an SRE triaging an SLO breach, a newcomer in their first week. Each path lists N pages in a fixed order, with a time budget and a one-line summary of «what you will be able to do after».

B

In progress

Onboarding paths exist for some personas — usually the newcomer. Paths for senior architects or oncall engineers are missing. A new senior reader can find their way around, but the portal does not plan the journey for them.

C

Absent

There are no curated paths. The reader arrives on the homepage and has to build their own route through the portal. The most common silent failure: the senior reader closes the tab.

What does «cold-start productivity» look like in the industry?

The pattern: a new reader (architect doing due diligence, engineer shipping the first endpoint, on-call engineer triaging an alert, newcomer in week one) finds a curated path on the homepage. The path lists N pages in a fixed order, with a time budget and a one-line «what you will be able to do after». The path is per-persona, not a single «getting started» page that tries to serve everyone.

Public examples worth opening:

  • Stripe — Getting Started — a short, ordered path with a clear «what you'll be able to do after»; the canonical example of a 5-minute onboarding.
  • HashiCorp Developer — explicit «learning paths» per product (Terraform, Vault, Consul), each with a time budget and a sequence.
  • Vercel — Getting Started — a stepwise path with a stated outcome, optimised for the «I want to deploy in 10 minutes» reader.
  • Kubernetes Tutorials — multiple curated tracks, each calibrated for a different starting skill level.
  • Datasette docs — getting-started routes that explicitly name the persona (analyst, developer, data publisher) at the top of each track.
What is hard about it?
  • «What you'll be able to do after» is hard to commit to. Writing a measurable outcome forces you to define «success» for the path. Most teams default to vague descriptions to avoid the commitment.
  • Persona paths drift as docs evolve. A page on the senior-architect path gets renamed; the path is not updated; the reader hits a 404.
  • Reading-time budgets get optimistic. The author who wrote the path knows the material; the new reader does not. The honest budget is usually 2-3× the author's estimate.
  • Cross-persona overlap creates redundancy. The SWE and SRE paths share three pages; if they diverge, you have two sources of truth for the same content.
  • The senior-reader path is rarely written. Onboarding is usually written for the newcomer. A senior architect doing due diligence in one day is the silent failure mode no one designs for.
What do I have right now? Honest self-grade.

My cold-start layer is built around two pieces:

  • An onboarding cold-start page with per-role paths — Architect, SWE, SA, QA, SRE, Manager — each with its own onboarding page.
  • A Tutorials quadrant with «your-first-X» tutorials per role: first ADR, first endpoint, first runbook drill, first sprint slice.

Current grade: B+ — close to A. What keeps it from A: the per-persona paths do not all carry a time budget or a clear «what you will be able to do after», and the senior-architect path is the thinnest.

The page that made the bar visible

The dimensions above did not appear as a list. They arrived one at a time, over several months. Each one took the same form: a question I could not have answered the day before, and could not stop noticing the day after. What turned them from a set of scattered corrections into a working rubric was building one section of the portal: Handbook.

That section is the authoring contract for the whole portal. It contains the principles, the substance contract, the style guide, the page skeleton, the authoring procedures, and templates for each Diátaxis type. It is a meta-layer — documentation about how to write the documentation. Writing it forced me to name, in one place, the dimensions I had been correcting in my own work without ever stating them out loud. Once they were named, I could grade pages against them instead of judging each page by feel. The contract also became clear to a future me, or any future author, in a way that habit alone could not provide.

Before the handbook section existed, the work on the portal was private self-improvement: corrections I made without ever publishing the rule behind them. After it existed, the corrections had a home. A weak page could be checked against a specific principle and fixed. A new page could open the right template and inherit the right shape. The rubric was no longer tied to my mood on a given day.

None of this is finished. Most of the ten dimensions are not at level A. Many are not even at level B. But the fact that the levels are named at all — that there is a vocabulary for the gap between a portal that looks impressive and one that earns trust — is what these past few months produced. This work is much slower than polish work, by a factor I keep underestimating. It is also the only work that compounds.

If I had to reduce this post to one sentence: mature documentation is not a writing problem; it is a willingness to be open about what you chose, what you did not choose, what you tried and failed at, and what you have not yet measured. The ten dimensions are ten ways of being open. The authoring section is the page that holds the rubric, so I can keep it in front of me. The rest is doing the work.
