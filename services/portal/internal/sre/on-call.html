On-call

What "on-call" means here

On-call is the agreement that one named person reacts within an agreed time when an alert fires. It is not "the person who reads chat sometimes" — it is a defined role with a clear time budget.

Today this is a single-maintainer project. The on-call person is Ivan Boyarkin. The structure on this page describes the roles we will use later, when the team grows or a tool like PagerDuty or OpsGenie is added (queued in backlog item-48).

Roles

Primary on-call
Receives the page first. Owns the response: triage, mitigation, communication. If the alert can be silenced because it is a known false positive, the primary still records why.
Secondary on-call
Backup. Paged automatically if the primary does not acknowledge within a defined time (today: 10 minutes). The secondary also covers planned absences.
Incident commander (during a real incident)
Coordinates the response when more than one person is involved. May be the primary or someone else if the primary is the one fixing.

In a one-person project the same human plays all three roles. The labels still matter — they remind you which "hat" you are wearing, and they make it easier to grow the team later.

"Mitigate" means the user-visible problem stops, even if the root cause is still being investigated. Restoring service is a separate goal from explaining what happened.

Alert routing

  1. An alert rule in Prometheus fires.
  2. Alertmanager groups related alerts and sends one notification to the channel for that severity.
  3. The on-call person acknowledges in the channel within the response target.
  4. If no acknowledgement within the target, the alert escalates to the secondary.
  5. If the alert is a SEV-1 or SEV-2, declare an incident (see incident response).

Routing rules live in the Alertmanager config tracked in the repository. PagerDuty/OpsGenie integration will replace the chat-channel hop once there is more than one on-call person.

What a shift looks like

A normal week of on-call should be quiet. The shift is not "watch dashboards 24/7"; it is "be reachable when something fires". The expected weekly tasks:

  • Day 1 of the shift: open the main dashboard for 5 minutes, confirm there are no silenced alerts you do not understand, review the previous shift's handoff note.
  • During the week: respond to pages within the targets above. Keep notes; you will need them for the postmortem.
  • End of shift: write a one-paragraph handoff: what fired, what is still open, anything fragile to watch.

If you fixed a problem permanently, also delete the alert that was firing for the wrong reason. The point of on-call is to reduce future toil, not to prove you can endure it.

Handoff template

Copy this into the chat at the end of each shift.

On-call handoff — week of YYYY-MM-DD
Outgoing: <name>
Incoming: <name>

Alerts that fired this week:
- YYYY-MM-DD HH:MM <alert> — outcome (resolved / mitigated / open)

Open items:
- <short description and link to ticket>

Fragile / watch closely:
- <short description, why fragile, what to do if it breaks>

Silenced alerts (and reason):
- <alert name> — <reason, until when>

Self-care rules

  • If you are not in a state to respond (sick, on a plane, asleep after a hard shift), tell the secondary before the alert fires, not after.
  • After a paged night, take time off the next day. Tired engineers cause incidents; that is well documented.
  • If the same alert keeps waking you up, the alert is broken or the system is broken — file a ticket the next morning, do not just snooze.
