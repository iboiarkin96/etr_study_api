On-Call Handbook — every question answered before the page fires

A written guide that answers every question an on-call engineer has before their shift starts — what to do when paged, how to escalate, which tools to use, and where the runbooks live.

TL;DR

The on-call handbook is the single document an engineer reads before their first solo on-call shift. It covers: escalation paths (who to call and how), tools and access (what dashboards, VPN, kubectl, runbook index), incident declaration process (when to declare, what severity, what channel), communication templates (status page update, internal stakeholder message), and handover protocol (what to document in the shift log). Updated after every P0/P1 incident. Reviewed by every new on-call engineer during shadow rotation.

What it is

The on-call handbook is the primary reference document for on-call engineers. It is written collaboratively by the team, updated after every incident that reveals a gap, and reviewed by every new on-call engineer before their first solo shift. It covers:

  • Escalation paths: Who to call for which type of problem, how (PagerDuty, phone, Slack), and what information to have ready when escalating.
  • Tools and access: The specific dashboards to check first (linked), the kubectl commands for common operations, VPN credentials, and where the runbook index is.
  • Incident declaration: When to declare an incident (specific criteria, not gut feel), what severity to assign (linked to the severity framework), and which Slack channel to use for each severity level.
  • Communication templates: Pre-written status page update templates and internal stakeholder message formats. On-call engineers should not write communications from scratch during an incident.
  • Handover protocol: What to document in the shift log, how to communicate open items to the incoming on-call, and what "clean handover" looks like.

Why we need it

Every minute an on-call engineer spends searching for the escalation path or finding the right dashboard is a minute the customer-facing incident continues. The handbook eliminates the institutional knowledge problem: a new engineer on their first solo shift can respond effectively because the process is documented.

The handbook also transfers knowledge across team members without requiring direct mentorship. Without it, on-call competency is unevenly distributed — the engineers who've been on-call longest are effective; newer engineers are slower and more anxious. The handbook levels the playing field.

How others do it

PagerDuty

PagerDuty publishes an open-source "Incident Response Guide" that includes a chapter on on-call best practices. Their handbook structure: pre-shift checklist (review alerts, check dashboards), during-shift norms (response times per severity, communication frequency), and post-shift handover (shift log entries, escalation of unresolved items). They specifically recommend a "shadow rotation" for new on-call engineers — 2 shifts observing, then 2 shifts with a senior engineer available, then solo.

What we take: The shadow rotation is the most effective on-call onboarding practice. New engineers read the handbook, but they learn from shadowing. Two shadow shifts before solo eliminates the "first shift panic" that degrades incident response for new team members.

Source: PagerDuty Incident Response Guide

Stripe

Stripe's on-call handbook includes a "first 5 minutes" checklist for every page — a specific sequence of steps to take immediately after acknowledging a page before any diagnosis begins: declare an incident bridge, post to the #incidents channel, check the SLO burn rate dashboard, and find the runbook for the firing alert. The 5-minute checklist prevents the most common first-response errors: starting diagnosis before communication, or communicating before having any data.

What we take: A "first 5 minutes" checklist embedded in the handbook (or linked from every PagerDuty alert) converts on-call muscle memory from improvised to procedural. Sequence matters under stress — the checklist removes the cognitive load of "what do I do first?"

Source: Stripe Engineering Blog

Anatomy

Escalation matrix

Named people, their expertise, and how to reach them. Phone numbers, not just Slack handles — phones work when networks are degraded. Updated when team members join or leave. Reviewed at each on-call rotation change.

Tools and access checklist

Every tool an on-call engineer needs: dashboards (linked), CLI commands (pre-formatted, tested), access paths (VPN profile, kubectl context, cloud console URL). Validated during shadow shifts — if the new engineer can't access something, fix it before their solo shift.

Communication templates

Status page update template, internal #incidents Slack message template, executive summary template. Fill-in-the-blank format with placeholders for service name, impact, time of first detection, and estimated resolution. Never improvise communications during an incident.

Post-incident update cadence

For P0/P1 incidents: update the #incidents channel every 30 minutes with current status. Not when there's news — every 30 minutes regardless. Stakeholders in a long incident need time-stamped updates to calibrate their escalation decisions.

Common pitfalls

PitfallWhy it happensWhat to do instead
Handbook written once, never updatedWriting the handbook is the goal; keeping it current is work.The handbook update is part of the incident retrospective. Every gap found during an incident produces a handbook update within 48 hours. The retrospective action item is: "update handbook section X to cover scenario Y."
Handbook not reviewed during onboarding"They'll pick it up when they're on-call."Handbook review is part of the shadow rotation. New engineers read the handbook before their first shadow shift and flag anything that is unclear or missing. Their fresh-eyes perspective is the most valuable handbook feedback.
Escalation paths list roles, not people"Contact the security team" without specifying who or how.Escalation paths list named individuals with multiple contact methods. Roles change; people are reachable. "Contact Alice Chen (alice@company.com, +1-555-0142) for security escalations" is actionable at 3 am. "Contact the security team" is not.
