Capacity Planning — know when you'll hit the wall before the wall hits you

A quarterly process that projects resource consumption 6–12 months forward based on traffic growth models and feature launch calendars, translating projections into provisioning decisions before headroom runs out.

TL;DR

Capacity planning projects the consumption of 5 key resource dimensions (CPU, memory, DB connections, storage, external API quota) 6–12 months forward using historical growth trends and upcoming feature launch data. The quarterly capacity review produces three outputs: reserved capacity purchase decisions, architectural changes to remove hard limits, and load tests that validate projections against actual system behaviour. Cloud elasticity handles provisioning lag but not budget surprises, connection pool limits, or third-party rate limits.

What it is

Capacity planning is the quarterly process of projecting forward resource consumption and translating projections into decisions before limits are reached. It covers five resource dimensions:

  • Compute (CPU/memory): Pod/instance count and sizing projections based on traffic growth.
  • Database connections: Connection pool saturation under projected traffic — often the first hard limit hit in a scale event.
  • Storage: Data growth rate, retention policy impact, backup storage requirements.
  • External API quota: Third-party rate limits (Stripe, Twilio, Sendgrid) that don't scale with your cloud spend.
  • Network egress: Bandwidth cost and CDN capacity projections for media-heavy services.

The output of the quarterly capacity review is three types of decisions: reserved capacity purchases (commit discounts), architectural changes (shard the DB, add read replicas, implement caching), and updated hard limits in configuration.

Why we need it

"We'll add more servers when we need them" is true for CPU — cloud autoscaling handles provisioning lag. But it's not true for DB connection pool limits (architectural change required), third-party API rate limits (requires vendor negotiation with lead time), or reserved capacity discounts (committed upfront). Capacity incidents in these dimensions happen because no one modelled the growth ahead of time.

Load tests are the validation step that catch the gap between the model and reality. A connection pool limit that looks fine under current load may be hit at 2× traffic — and the load test surfaces that finding in a controlled environment, not at 2 am during a marketing campaign that tripled traffic.

How others do it

Google SRE

Google's SRE book dedicates a chapter to capacity planning. Their model: every service has a "demand forecast" based on 6 months of historical traffic and an "expansion plan" based on the forecast. Forecasts are reviewed quarterly. Services that exceed their forecast by 20% trigger an unplanned capacity review. Google also runs annual "data center failure" simulations to validate that capacity commitments can be met from remaining capacity after a zone failure.

What we take: Capacity planning for failure scenarios — not just growth — is essential. "We can handle 2× normal traffic" is only useful if you know you can handle 2× traffic from 2 of 3 availability zones, not all three.

Source: Google SRE Book — Software Engineering in SRE

Shopify

Shopify runs capacity planning explicitly for Black Friday / Cyber Monday, the single most important traffic event of their year. Each Black Friday, Shopify traffic can spike 10–15× normal. Their capacity planning process starts 3 months before the event: model the expected peak, run graduated load tests at 25%, 50%, 75%, and 100% of the projected peak, identify and fix limits discovered during each test, and document the final capacity state before the event.

What we take: Plan explicitly for your peak traffic event (a product launch, a sale, a seasonal spike) with graduated load tests, not just for average growth. The assumptions that break under peak load are different from the assumptions that break under steady growth.

Source: Shopify Engineering Blog

Anatomy

Five resource dimensions

CPU, memory, DB connections, storage, external API quota. Track current headroom (current consumption / limit) for each dimension per service. Headroom below 40% triggers a capacity review.

Growth model

Linear regression on 6 months of resource data plus planned feature launch multipliers. Expressed as "current headroom × expected at + 6 months." If headroom drops below 20% before the next quarterly review, act now.

Load test

Validate the growth model against actual system behaviour at projected peak load. Use k6, Locust, or Gatling. Run against production-like staging. Any limit hit during the load test is an architectural change item, not an alert threshold adjustment.

Quarterly review meeting

30-minute meeting: infrastructure lead + product lead + on-call lead. Three outputs: (1) reserved capacity purchase decisions, (2) architectural change items for the next quarter, (3) hard limit updates. Action items go to the engineering backlog with named owners.

Common pitfalls

PitfallWhy it happensWhat to do instead
Planning only for compute, not for hard limitsAutoscaling makes compute planning feel solved.DB connection pools, third-party API quotas, and storage limits don't autoscale. Model all five dimensions, not just the ones that are easy to change.
No load test — model assumed correctLoad tests take effort and the model "looks right."Run at least one load test per quarter against the service closest to its capacity limit. Models diverge from reality over time; load tests catch the divergence.
Capacity review produces no decisionsReview becomes a status report meeting, not a decision meeting.End every capacity review with at least one concrete action: a purchase order, a Jira ticket for an architectural change, or an explicit "we're fine until next quarter" sign-off. No output = no value.
