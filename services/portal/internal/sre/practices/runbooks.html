Runbooks — senior engineer reasoning encoded for 3 am

Per-alert, per-symptom operational guides that tell an on-call engineer exactly what to do when a specific alert fires — what it means, how to diagnose, the ordered remediation steps, and when to escalate.

TL;DR

A runbook is a decision tree for a specific alert or failure mode, optimised for 3 am use by an engineer who may not be familiar with the service. It covers: what the alert means, the exact commands to run to confirm the diagnosis, the ordered remediation steps, the expected outcome of each step, and the escalation trigger if steps fail. Runbooks are the Pareto priority: write them for the 10 most frequent alerts first (they account for 80% of pages). They are a Definition of Done requirement for any new alert and are updated after every incident where they failed or were missing a step.

What it is

A runbook is an operational decision tree for a specific alert or failure mode. Unlike a general how-to guide (which describes how a system works), a runbook answers one specific question: "This alert just fired at 3 am. What do I do?"

A runbook for a specific alert has five sections:

  • Alert context: Which alert fired, what the metric measures, and what typical causes are. Links to the dashboard panel showing the alerting signal.
  • Diagnosis steps: The exact commands to run to confirm what's wrong. Not "check the database" — the exact kubectl exec or SQL query, pre-formatted, ready to copy.
  • Remediation steps: The ordered list of actions to take to fix the problem, with the expected outcome of each step. "If step 3 produces output X, proceed to step 4. If it produces output Y, skip to step 6."
  • Verification: How to confirm the fix worked — which metric should return to normal, which log event should appear, what the dashboard should look like after resolution.
  • Escalation trigger: The specific condition under which to stop following the runbook and call a senior engineer. Who to call, and what information to have ready.

Why we need it

The best engineers on the team can diagnose any alert from memory. But at 3 am on a Saturday, during a P0 incident affecting thousands of users, you don't want the response to depend on which engineer is on-call. Runbooks encode the senior engineer's diagnostic reasoning so any on-call engineer can execute it. Every minute saved in diagnosis is a minute less downtime for users.

Runbooks also democratise on-call. Without them, on-call effectiveness is inversely proportional to how long the engineer has been on the team. With them, a 6-month engineer can resolve 90% of alerts without waking anyone up — and knows exactly when to escalate for the 10% they can't.

How others do it

Google SRE

Google maintains runbooks for every alert in their internal monitoring system, linked from the alert annotation. The runbook development process: a senior engineer writes the first version from memory; it's validated by a junior engineer who follows it blindly on the first incident where it applies; any step that fails or is ambiguous is updated immediately after. The "junior engineer validation" step is the most valuable — it surfaces the implicit knowledge assumptions in the senior engineer's mental model.

What we take: Validate every runbook with a junior engineer before it's relied on in production. Steps that seem obvious to the author are frequently ambiguous to someone without context. The validation session is also an onboarding opportunity — the junior engineer learns the system by following the runbook.

Source: Google SRE Workbook — On-Call

Shopify

Shopify embeds runbooks directly in PagerDuty alert annotations as a link that opens with one click during an incident. Their runbook format is unusually terse: each step is a single line with a copy button for the command. No prose explanation between steps — the explanation is a collapsible "Why this step?" section that's hidden by default. Engineers who know what they're doing follow the steps; engineers who don't expand the explanation.

What we take: Optimise runbook UX for speed under stress. Copy buttons for commands, numbered steps, no long paragraphs. The engineer's cognitive bandwidth during an incident is limited — every word that isn't essential is a word that slows them down.

Source: Shopify Engineering Blog

Anatomy

Pareto prioritisation

Write runbooks for the 10 most frequent alerts first. Use the on-call ticket history to rank by frequency — the top 10 alerts almost always account for 80% of pages. Writing runbooks for long-tail alerts before the high-frequency ones is the opposite of the correct priority.

Pre-formatted commands

Every command in a runbook is pre-formatted for the production environment — correct namespace, correct service name, correct flags. Never write "run the kubectl describe pod command" — write the exact command with all parameters. The on-call engineer should be able to copy and paste without editing.

DoD requirement

No new alert ships to production without a linked runbook. The runbook link is a required field in the alert YAML. CI fails if the link is missing or returns 404. The "write the runbook" task is part of the same sprint as the alert development.

Post-incident update

If an engineer follows a runbook and it fails or is missing a step, the runbook update is the first action after the incident is resolved. Not a postmortem action item — an immediate update. A runbook that failed in production must be fixed before the next time the same alert fires.

Common pitfalls

PitfallWhy it happensWhat to do instead
Runbooks that say "contact [service team]"The alert owner doesn't know how to fix the problem.If the runbook's only step is "escalate," it's not a runbook — it's an escalation policy. The diagnosis steps (what to check, what to report to the escalation target) should still be in the runbook even if the fix requires someone else.
Runbooks with stale commandsThe system changed; nobody updated the runbook.Quarterly runbook review: test every command in staging. A command that doesn't work in staging won't work at 3 am. Stale runbooks are worse than no runbooks — they waste time and erode trust.
General operational guides instead of alert-specific runbooks"We have a general troubleshooting guide."A general troubleshooting guide is a reference document for learning; an alert-specific runbook is a decision tree for responding. Both are useful; only the alert-specific runbook helps at 3 am. Write both — link the specific from the general, not the other way around.
