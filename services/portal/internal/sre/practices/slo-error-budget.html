SLO & Error Budget — reliability defined in numbers

A Service Level Objective is the team's contract with itself about what "reliable enough" means, expressed as a measurable target so every deployment decision can be made with data, not gut feel.

TL;DR

An SLO (Service Level Objective) is a reliability target expressed as a percentage of good events over a rolling window — for example, 99.9% of HTTP requests return 2xx in ≤ 300 ms over 28 days. The error budget is what remains: 0.1% of bad events the team is allowed to spend. When the budget is full, teams ship fast; when it is nearly exhausted, reliability work takes priority over features. SLOs convert the perennial product vs. engineering tension into a shared, pre-agreed data frame.

What it is

An SLO consists of three parts:

  • SLI (Service Level Indicator): The metric being measured — typically a ratio of good events to total events. Good events for an HTTP API might be "requests that return 2xx or 3xx in under 300 ms."
  • Target: The percentage of good events required over a rolling window (28 days). 99.9% is a common starting point for user-facing services.
  • Error budget: 100% minus the target. For 99.9%, the budget is 0.1% of requests, which equals approximately 43 minutes of complete downtime per month. The budget can also be spent on slowness — 10× as many slow requests as failed ones might exhaust it equally fast.

A burn-rate alert fires when the error budget is being depleted faster than the rolling window allows — specifically when 1-hour or 6-hour burn rates indicate the 30-day budget will be exhausted ahead of schedule.

Why we need it

Without SLOs, "reliability" is a feeling and every reliability conversation is a negotiation based on instinct. With SLOs, every deployment decision is data-driven: "do we have enough error budget to risk this release?" The team can answer that in seconds, not meetings.

SLOs also remove the perennial product vs. engineering conflict. Both sides agree in advance on what "reliable enough" means. Product can push for velocity when the budget is healthy; engineering can push back with data when the budget is nearly exhausted. Neither argument depends on who shouts loudest.

How others do it

Google SRE

Google invented error budgets as the mechanism to balance reliability and velocity. The key rule: when the error budget for a quarter is exhausted, the SRE team and product team halt feature launches and focus exclusively on reliability. The budget resets at the start of the next quarter. Google also publishes explicit SLO targets for every user-facing service internally.

What we take: The policy that links exhausted error budget to halted feature launches is what makes SLOs real. An SLO with no consequence for breaching it is a dashboard decoration. Define the policy before the first budget is exhausted — not during the crisis.

Source: Google SRE Book — SLOs

Atlassian

Atlassian defines SLOs for every Jira and Confluence service tier and publishes burn-rate dashboards to all engineering teams. Their SLO review cadence is weekly: teams with a burn rate above 2× in the past 7 days bring a root cause and a remediation plan to the weekly reliability review. SLO data is also used in quarterly planning to allocate reliability work vs. feature work proportionally.

What we take: Weekly SLO review with a burn-rate threshold forces teams to act before the budget is exhausted rather than scrambling after the breach. 2× burn rate as the trigger is a practical early-warning system.

Source: Atlassian — SLI/SLO/SLA

Honeycomb

Honeycomb uses SLOs as the primary input to their on-call paging policy — they page on SLO burn rate, not on raw metric thresholds. An alert fires when the 1-hour burn rate indicates the 30-day budget will be exhausted in less than 24 hours. This dramatically reduces alert noise while ensuring the team is paged before a breach, not after.

What we take: Burn-rate alerts on SLOs outperform threshold alerts on raw metrics in almost every scenario. Fewer pages, higher precision, earlier warning. The multi-window approach (1h + 6h burn rate) is the correct default.

Source: Honeycomb SLO documentation

Anatomy

SLI definition

A precise, measurable ratio: good events / total events. Avoid SLIs that depend on infrastructure availability (CPU, memory) — use user-visible outcomes (request success, latency). One SLI per critical user journey.

SLO target and window

28-day rolling window is the standard. Start at 99.9% for user-facing services. Set the target based on historical data — if you currently achieve 99.5%, set the target at 99.7%, not 99.99%. Aspirational targets produce permanent budget exhaustion.

Burn-rate alert

Two-window alert: 1h burn rate × 14.4 and 6h burn rate × 6. Both must fire together to page. Single-window burn rate alerts produce false positives from transient spikes.

Error budget policy

Written agreement between product and engineering: when budget drops below X%, all risky deployments pause until the budget recovers. Without a written policy, the SLO is advisory and ignored when it matters most.

SLO review cadence

Weekly review of burn rate and budget remaining. Quarterly review of whether the target is still appropriate — services mature, user expectations change, and a target set 12 months ago may be wrong today.

Common pitfalls

PitfallWhy it happensWhat to do instead
SLO target set by aspiration, not measurement"We should be 99.99%" without checking current actuals.Set the SLO at current P50 performance + 10% stretch. Review and raise it as the system improves. An SLO you currently breach is useless as a decision tool.
No error budget policyThe SLO is defined but there's no rule about what to do when the budget is exhausted.Write the policy before the first breach. "When budget drops below 20%, deployments pause" is a simple, effective starting policy.
Paging on threshold, not burn rateEngineers alert on "error rate > 1%" instead of SLO burn rate.Threshold alerts are noisy and don't tell you whether the budget is at risk. Burn-rate alerts tell you exactly how fast the budget is draining and how urgent the response is.
