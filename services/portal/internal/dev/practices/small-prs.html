Small PRs — < 400 LOC, < 24h to review

Every change small enough for a reviewer to hold in working memory; review turnaround under one working day.

TL;DR

Google's internal code-review research, published in google.github.io/eng-practices, found that defect-detection drops sharply once a change exceeds roughly 200 lines of code; the industry rule-of-thumb is < 400 LOC. Reviews finish in under 24 hours so the author is never blocked over a working day. Stacked PRs (Graphite, git-spice, Phabricator-style diffs) let one logical feature flow through review as a sequence of small reviewable units instead of one indigestible dump.

What it is

Small PRs is the practice of sizing every change to fit comfortably inside a reviewer's working memory — under roughly 400 changed lines of code, ideally under 200 — and getting it reviewed and merged within one working day. The norm originated at Google as "small CLs" (change lists) and is documented at google.github.io/eng-practices, which is the most-cited reference on code-review craft.

The practice has two halves:

  • Sizing. Before the PR is opened, the author slices the work. A logical feature rarely fits in one small PR; it fits in a sequence of small PRs. The first PR adds a primitive; the second adds a caller; the third wires it into the UI. Each is independently mergeable.
  • Stacking. Tooling — Graphite, git-spice, Sapling, Phabricator — represents the sequence as a stack of diffs, each with its own review thread. The author can keep building on top of unmerged PRs without waiting; the reviewer reads each unit independently. Merging happens bottom-up.

"Small" is a property of cognitive load, not just LOC. A 300-line PR that changes 50 files is large; a 600-line PR that adds one new file with a single responsibility may be small.

Why we need it

Big PRs get rubber-stamped because the reviewer cannot hold the change in working memory. After roughly 200 changed lines, the reviewer's attention drops and the comment density per line plummets. By 1,000 lines the review is a courtesy stamp; defects pass through unobserved and surface in production weeks later as the "we reviewed that PR" mystery bug.

Small PRs make review meaningful. The reviewer actually reads, actually understands, actually catches the bad idea before merge. The author actually rewrites in response. The codebase improves through review rather than degrading with each merge.

The secondary cost of big PRs is review latency — the author waits a day, then two, then a week, and switches contexts to something else. By the time the review comes back, the author has paged out the change. Re-engagement costs more than the original write. Small PRs that round-trip in under 24 hours keep the context fresh.

How others do it

Google — Engineering Practices documentation

Google's public engineering-practices guide documents the small-CL norm in detail. The guide states the size target explicitly ("small CLs that do a single thing"), discusses how to split a large change, and quantifies the reviewer-attention drop above ~200 lines. Internally, code-review SLAs are tight: reviewers respond within hours, and the median CL is merged within a working day. The practice is enforced socially through code-review culture and tooling defaults.

What we take: Adopt the Google guide as the team's review handbook. The small-CL section is short, prescriptive, and immediately actionable.

Source: google.github.io/eng-practices — Small CLs

Graphite — stacked PR workflow

Graphite is one of several tools (alongside git-spice, Sapling, ghstack) that brings the Phabricator-style stacked-diff model to GitHub. The Graphite engineering blog has extensively documented the practice: how to slice a feature, how to keep the stack rebased, how reviewers should approach a 5-PR stack. The core insight is that the unit of code review is the PR, but the unit of feature delivery is the stack — separating these unblocks the author and gives the reviewer digestible units.

What we take: Pick one stacking tool (Graphite or git-spice for GitHub) and adopt it on a single epic to evaluate. Stacks work best for backend / library work; UI work tends to slice less cleanly.

Source: Graphite blog — stacked PRs

Atlassian Engineering

Atlassian's engineering blog and developer experience writing have published guidance on PR size and review SLAs based on their own internal data: PRs over 400 lines see significantly longer cycle time and more rework. Atlassian recommends a 24-hour review SLA and provides team-level dashboards showing PR-size distribution and review latency. Their guidance is widely cited because Atlassian sells the tooling (Bitbucket) and has visibility into aggregated review patterns at scale.

What we take: Publish a weekly PR-size histogram alongside the review-latency chart. Both numbers move together; making them visible drives both down.

Source: Atlassian Engineering blog

The pattern: small PRs are not a politeness; they are an empirically-supported optimisation of the review process. Every public study points the same way.

Anatomy

400-LOC ceiling

CI check (danger.js or similar) flags any PR above 400 changed lines, excluding generated files and lockfiles. The flag is a warning, not a hard block — sometimes a single-file rename legitimately exceeds it — but the warning prompts a conversation.

24-hour review SLA

A PR receives a first review within 24 working hours of being marked ready. Re-reviews after pushed changes target 4 hours. Reviewers' calendars include "review block" slots; first-review responsibility rotates daily.

Stacked-PR tooling

A single tool (Graphite, git-spice, or ghstack) installed team-wide for stack management. The tool handles rebasing the stack when an earlier PR is merged or amended. Without tooling, stacks turn into a manual rebasing nightmare and die.

"What this PR does" template

PR description template with three required fields: what changed, why now, what the reviewer should check. A small PR with a clear description is reviewed in 10 minutes; a small PR with no description still costs 30.

Single responsibility per PR

One PR does one thing. A rename and a behaviour change in the same PR are two PRs. Drive-by formatting fixes go in a separate "formatting only" PR. Reviewers can trust the description because the scope is bounded.

Weekly size/latency dashboard

A chart in the engineering channel: PR-size distribution (median, p90), review latency (median, p90), aged-PR count (open > 48h). Visible numbers drift toward improvement; hidden numbers drift toward decay.

Common pitfalls

PitfallWhy it happensWhat to do instead
PRs balloon because slicing is "premature optimisation"Slicing a feature into small PRs feels like extra work upfront; the author optimistically believes the reviewer will keep up with one big PR.Make slicing a planning artifact, not an afterthought. The first deliverable of an epic is a PR-stack plan listing the 3–6 small PRs that will compose the feature.
Reviews stall because reviewers wait for "a good time"Reviewers treat review as a deep-focus task and batch it for the afternoon. The PR ages, the author switches contexts, the round-trip cost doubles.Schedule short, named review blocks twice a day. Rotate first-review duty. Treat a PR untouched after 4 hours as a team-level signal, not an individual one.
Stacked PRs collapse when an early PR changesManual rebasing of a 5-PR stack after an early review comment is hostile. Authors give up and collapse the stack back into a big PR.Standardise on one stacking tool that handles rebasing automatically. Without tooling, stacks are theoretical; with tooling, they are routine.
