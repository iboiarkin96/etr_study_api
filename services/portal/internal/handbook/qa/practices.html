Group A · 5 practices

Test Strategy & Risk

How we decide what to test, how much, and against what risk profile. Without risk-based prioritisation, every test suite grows linearly until it becomes unmaintainable. Strong strategy creates a coherent testing vision — so the team knows which areas need deep coverage and which can be sampled.

Group avg score: AS-IS 0.0→0.0 / 10
01

Risk-based Testing — allocate effort by risk

3.5→8.0

What it is. Risk-based testing (RBT) is a strategy that allocates test effort in proportion to the risk of each feature — where risk = likelihood × impact of failure. Originally formalised by Dorothy Graham and Mark Fewster in the late 1990s, it replaces random or time-ordered test selection with a risk matrix that guides coverage depth.

Why it matters. Without risk weighting, teams spend equal time on a read-only tooltip and a payment flow, then ship with both over- and under-tested areas.

How we will roll it out. Every sprint begins with a 10-minute risk assessment of new features; the highest-risk areas get deep coverage, lowest-risk areas get a single happy-path smoke.

02

Test Pyramid Design — shape the automation portfolio

4.0→8.5

What it is. The test pyramid (Mike Cohn, 2009) divides automated tests into three layers: many fast unit tests at the base, fewer integration tests in the middle, and a thin layer of slow end-to-end tests at the top. Ownership is explicit: developers own unit + integration; QA co-owns contract and system layers.

Why it matters. Inverted pyramids (too many E2E, too few unit tests) produce test suites that take 40 minutes, flake constantly, and give no signal on which line broke.

How we will roll it out. The pyramid shape is documented in a one-page ADR; layer ratios are reviewed quarterly; new features must ship with unit and integration tests from day one.

03

Shift-left Testing — quality starts at requirements

2.5→8.0

What it is. Shift-left means involving QA in requirements and design phases, before code is written. The term was coined by Larry Smith in 2001. QA reviews specs, writes acceptance criteria, and joins planning ceremonies so that testability is a design constraint, not an afterthought.

Why it matters. Finding a defect in production costs 100× more than catching the same flaw in a spec review. Ambiguous acceptance criteria discovered in implementation cause rework and scope creep.

How we will roll it out. QA joins Three Amigos sessions for every story; acceptance criteria are signed off by QA before a ticket enters the sprint.

04

Test Oracle Definition — know what correct looks like

3.0→8.0

What it is. A test oracle is the mechanism or source used to determine whether a test has passed or failed. Oracles can be explicit (a spec page), implicit (a prior version's behaviour), or heuristic (a domain expert's judgement). Explicitly documenting the oracle chain prevents "we don't know what correct looks like" during test execution.

Why it matters. Without a defined oracle, testers report observations but cannot call pass/fail — every ambiguous result goes to a developer who has to re-read the spec, doubling verification cycle time.

How we will roll it out. The oracle chain for each test area is listed in the test strategy; new features must declare their oracle before test planning starts.

05

Test Environment Design — reliable infrastructure for tests

4.5→8.5

What it is. Test environment design is the deliberate planning of the infrastructure, data, and configuration needed to execute tests reliably. Environments are classified by purpose: local (free mutation, developer-controlled), CI (ephemeral, no side-effects), staging (config mirrors prod), and prod-like (for release rehearsal).

Why it matters. Flaky tests caused by environment differences (database state, network timeouts, seed data drift) erode trust in the test suite and produce false negatives.

How we will roll it out. Each environment's purpose, owner, and reset procedure is documented in the test environments reference page; no new test tier is added without an ADR.

Test Design

How we derive effective test cases from requirements, specifications, and domain knowledge. Techniques like boundary value analysis and equivalence partitioning turn guesswork into systematic coverage. Good test design catches bugs before code review, not after production.

Group avg score: AS-IS 0.0→0.0 / 10
06

Boundary Value Analysis — test the edges

3.0→8.0

What it is. Boundary Value Analysis (BVA) is a black-box technique developed by Glenford Myers in 1979. It tests values at and immediately around the edges of input partitions — the minimum, maximum, and the values one step below and above each boundary. BVA covers the class of bugs that arise from off-by-one errors and boundary conditions.

Why it matters. Without BVA, off-by-one errors and boundary edge cases are systematically missed. A field that accepts 1–255 characters typically fails at 0, 256, and 255 — not at 128.

How we will roll it out. Every field with a numeric or length constraint has a BVA test set covering min, max, min-1, and max+1 at minimum.

07

Equivalence Partitioning — reduce redundant tests

2.5→7.5

What it is. Equivalence Partitioning (EP) divides the input space into classes (valid and invalid partitions) where all values within a partition are expected to behave identically. Testing one representative value per partition gives the same defect coverage as testing all values in that partition. The technique was formalised alongside BVA by Glenford Myers.

Why it matters. Without partitioning, testers either test too few values (missing whole classes of failures) or too many (wasting cycles on redundant inputs). Both outcomes reduce defect detection per hour of test effort.

How we will roll it out. Each API field's partition map is documented in the test case for that endpoint; invalid partitions receive at least one negative test.

08

Decision Table Testing — model business rule combinations

2.0→7.5

What it is. Decision tables map combinations of conditions to expected actions or outputs. Each column in the table is one combination (test case); each row is one condition or action. Decision tables are ideal for business rules with multiple interacting conditions.

Why it matters. Business logic that involves multiple simultaneous conditions produces combinatorial test cases that cannot be managed by intuition alone.

How we will roll it out. Every endpoint or UI path with more than two interacting conditions has a decision table; tables are reviewed with the developer during Three Amigos.

09

Exploratory Test Charters — structured unscripted testing

4.0→8.5

What it is. An exploratory test charter (James Bach, 1996) is a structured guide to an unscripted test session: "Explore [target] with [resources], looking for [risk], in [duration]." Unlike scripted testing, exploratory testing uses the tester's skill to simultaneously design and execute tests in real time, guided by the charter's scope.

Why it matters. Scripted tests can only catch bugs the author anticipated. Exploratory testing finds the bugs nobody thought to write a test for — the class of defects most likely to escape to production.

How we will roll it out. Every sprint's QA cycle includes at least one 60-minute exploratory session with a written charter; session notes are filed in the defect tracker as potential follow-up items.

10

Scenario-based Testing — validate end-to-end user journeys

3.5→8.0

What it is. Scenario-based testing derives test cases from end-to-end user journeys rather than individual functions. Each scenario represents a realistic user goal and validates the system from the outside in.

Why it matters. Unit and integration tests verify components; scenario tests verify that components compose correctly. A system can pass all unit tests and still fail the most common user workflow due to integration gaps.

How we will roll it out. Each user-facing feature has at least one happy-path scenario and one failure-path scenario documented before implementation; scenario tests run as part of the system test layer in CI.

Automation & Tooling

How we encode quality gates into automated pipelines that run on every commit. Contract tests prevent integration drift; CI gates stop regressions from merging; visual regression tests catch UI breakage invisible to unit tests. This group turns repetitive manual checks into fast, reliable robots.

Group avg score: AS-IS 0.0→0.0 / 10
11

Contract Testing — prevent API drift between services

2.0→8.5

What it is. Contract testing (introduced at scale by Pact in 2013) verifies that a consumer and provider agree on the shape of an API interaction. The consumer records its expectations as a "pact"; the provider verifies it can fulfil them. Unlike integration tests, contract tests run independently, without both services deployed simultaneously.

Why it matters. Without contract tests, API drift between provider and consumer goes undetected until a broken deployment, often in staging or production.

How we will roll it out. Every public API endpoint has a Pact or OpenAPI-based contract test; the provider's CI runs consumer pacts on every PR; any contract drift fails the build immediately.

12

API Acceptance Testing — systematic per-endpoint sign-off

4.5→9.0

What it is. API acceptance testing is a structured pass that verifies every HTTP endpoint against its specification. The pass covers: contract conformance, happy-path responses, error codes (4xx/5xx), authentication gates, idempotency behaviour, and observability signals (X-Request-ID in headers and logs). It is the QA sign-off gate for each endpoint.

Why it matters. Without a systematic per-endpoint acceptance pass, endpoints routinely miss authentication edge cases, return incorrect error shapes, or drop correlation IDs — defects that surface only after the endpoint is integrated by a consumer.

How we will roll it out. Every endpoint must pass checklist 0002 (API endpoint acceptance) before it is marked "QA verified"; the checklist is annotated with evidence (request/response captures, X-Request-ID trace).

13

Visual Regression Testing — catch pixel-level UI breakage

1.5→7.5

What it is. Visual regression testing captures pixel-level screenshots of UI pages or components and diffs them against a baseline on every build. Tools like Percy (Browserstack), Chromatic (Storybook), or Playwright screenshots detect unintended visual changes — broken layouts, missing elements, colour drift — that functional tests cannot catch.

Why it matters. CSS changes frequently cause visual regressions that pass all functional tests: a layout breaks at 768px, a button loses its label, a dark-mode colour becomes illegible. Without pixel-level diffs, these reach production.

How we will roll it out. Key portal pages have baseline screenshots in CI; any PR touching shared CSS or layout templates triggers a visual diff; approvals require an explicit human review of the diff.

14

Accessibility Test Automation — embed a11y in CI

2.5→8.0

What it is. Accessibility test automation combines axe-core (automated rule engine) with keyboard-only and screen-reader testing against WCAG 2.1 Level AA. Axe runs in CI on every build and catches ~30–40% of WCAG failures automatically; the remaining 60–70% require manual verification guided by checklist 0005.

Why it matters. Accessibility failures are legal liabilities in most jurisdictions and affect 15–20% of users globally. Automated scanning catches rule violations (missing alt text, duplicate IDs, contrast failures) before any human tester runs a session.

How we will roll it out. axe-core runs on every CI build of the docs portal; critical violations fail the build; checklist 0005 is completed for every major docs section before release.

15

CI Quality Gates — automated guards on every PR

3.5→8.5

What it is. CI quality gates are automated pass/fail checks that run on every pull request and block merging when quality criteria are not met. Gates include: unit test pass rate, contract test pass, linting (Spectral for OpenAPI, axe for a11y), coverage thresholds, and visual regression approval.

Why it matters. Without automated gates, quality checks become optional and inconsistent. PRs merge with missing tests, failing contracts, or accessibility regressions because the review process relies on human memory.

How we will roll it out. Every PR must pass all CI quality gates before merge; gate failures include actionable error messages linking to the failing checklist or rule; no bypasses without a documented exception.

Group avg score: AS-IS 0.0→0.0 / 10
16

Defect Severity Triage — classify and prioritise every bug

4.5→8.5

What it is. Defect severity triage is the structured process of classifying every found defect using a severity scale (S1–S4: Critical, Major, Minor, Trivial) and a priority scale (P1–P4: Now, Soon, Backlog, Someday), then assigning it to an owner with an SLA. Severity measures impact; priority determines urgency. The two dimensions are independent.

Why it matters. When all defects are "high priority," nothing is effectively prioritised. Teams that lack a triage matrix have debugging queues negotiated ad hoc, producing inconsistent resolution times and hidden S1-level issues.

How we will roll it out. Every defect is triaged within 1 working day of filing; the severity matrix (reference/bug-severity-and-priority.html) is the canonical source; no defect is marked "closed" without passing through the verified state.

17

Escape Rate Tracking — measure what gets past QA

2.0→8.5

What it is. Escape rate (defect escape rate) measures the proportion of defects that pass through QA and are discovered in production or staging by end users, monitors, or downstream consumers. Expressed as: escapes / (total defects found + escapes). Popularised as a key metric by Capers Jones in software measurement research.

Why it matters. Teams without escape tracking cannot distinguish between "we have good quality" and "we don't know about our defects." High escape rates indicate insufficient coverage; consistently zero rates may indicate under-reporting.

How we will roll it out. Every production defect is tagged "escape" in the tracker; escape rate is calculated weekly, reviewed in the QA retrospective, and plotted on the quality dashboard.

18

Root Cause Analysis — prevent defect recurrence

2.5→8.0

What it is. Root cause analysis (RCA) is a structured investigation of recurring or high-impact defects to identify their systemic causes, not just their surface symptoms. The most common technique is the 5-Whys (Sakichi Toyoda, Toyota Production System). In software QA, RCA sessions are held for every S1 defect and every recurring S2.

Why it matters. Fixing symptoms without addressing root causes leads to recurrence. A team that patches the same type of bug six times in a year is spending engineering time on a systemic problem they haven't named.

How we will roll it out. Every S1 defect triggers a 30-minute RCA session; findings are filed as a ticket with a preventive action; outcomes are presented in the next QA retrospective.

19

Quality Dashboarding — make quality visible to the whole team

1.5→8.0

What it is. A quality dashboard is a real-time or near-real-time view of the health of the quality programme, visible to the entire team. Metrics tracked: defect escape rate, golden-path test coverage, flaky test count, time-to-verify, S1/S2 open count, and QA cycle time per sprint.

Why it matters. Without visibility, quality is perceived as a black box — "is QA a bottleneck?" becomes a recurring argument without data to resolve it. Dashboards turn quality into a shared team conversation rather than a function-specific concern.

How we will roll it out. A quality dashboard is maintained in the QA portal; all metrics are updated at the end of each sprint; the dashboard link is included in every sprint review.

20

Release Sign-off — evidence-based go/no-go decisions

4.0→9.0

What it is. Release sign-off is the formal go/no-go decision made by QA before a software release is tagged and deployed. The sign-off document answers four questions: what was in scope, what was found (findings), what was deferred (deferrals with owner), and what is the recommendation (GO / CONDITIONAL GO / NO-GO). This practice was standardised in the IEEE 829 test completion report.

Why it matters. Without a formal sign-off, releases happen informally — "I think it looks OK" — with no audit trail. When a defect escapes, there is no record of what was known and accepted at release time.

How we will roll it out. Every release requires a sign-off document filed in the defect tracker; the sign-off uses the release smoke checklist (0003) as evidence; GO requires all critical items green; CONDITIONAL GO requires named owners for each deferral.

21

Three Amigos Sessions — align before coding

2.0→8.0

What it is. Three Amigos (also "Specification Workshop") is a meeting between a developer, a QA engineer, and a product manager to discuss a user story before implementation begins. The format was popularised by George Dinwiddie and is a core practice in Behaviour-Driven Development (BDD). The session produces shared acceptance criteria and identifies ambiguities before coding starts.

Why it matters. Without Three Amigos, developers implement one interpretation of a story, product managers expect another, and QA discovers the gap during verification. Rework from late defect detection costs 5–10× more than catching the ambiguity in a 30-minute conversation.

How we will roll it out. Every story with an acceptance criterion enters the sprint only after a Three Amigos session; the session output is appended to the ticket; QA signs off on the criteria before implementation starts.

22

Test Plan Authoring — make testing scope visible

3.5→8.5

What it is. A test plan is a document that defines the scope, approach, resources, and schedule for a testing activity. Modelled on IEEE 829, it includes: test items, in-scope/out-of-scope areas, risk analysis, entry/exit criteria, a RACI, and a delivery schedule. A lightweight version (1–2 pages) is required for any non-trivial feature or release.

Why it matters. Without a test plan, QA efforts are invisible to stakeholders, scope creep goes unmanaged, and the team discovers coverage gaps only after a failed release. Plans make commitments explicit and auditable.

How we will roll it out. A test plan is written for every feature that touches more than one service or that introduces a new user flow; the test-plan-template.html provides the canonical structure; plans are reviewed by the developer and PM before test execution.

23

Regression Suite Management — keep the suite healthy

3.0→8.0

What it is. Regression suite management is the ongoing practice of maintaining the set of automated tests that verify existing functionality has not been broken by new changes. It includes adding new tests for fixed bugs, removing or quarantining flaky tests, and periodically reviewing coverage to eliminate dead tests. Google's "Beyoncé Rule" — if you liked it you should have put a test on it — captures the addition discipline.

Why it matters. Regression suites accumulate technical debt like production code. A suite with 20% flaky tests and 15% dead tests creates noise, erodes trust, and slows CI by minutes. Unmanaged suites eventually become impossible to maintain.

How we will roll it out. Flaky tests are quarantined within 24 hours of detection; the regression suite is reviewed for dead tests every quarter; every fixed defect (S2 and above) ships with a regression test.

24

QA Retrospective Review — learn from every escape

2.0→7.5

What it is. A QA retrospective is a structured learning review focused on the quality process: what defects escaped, why they escaped, which practices prevented escapes, and what process changes will improve outcomes next cycle. Adapted from the Agile sprint retrospective format (Ken Schwaber and Jeff Sutherland, Scrum Guide), but scoped to quality metrics rather than team dynamics.

Why it matters. Without a quality-specific retrospective, quality improvements are ad hoc and untracked. Escape events repeat because the team discusses them informally once and moves on — systemic causes go unaddressed.

How we will roll it out. A 30-minute QA retrospective is held after every sprint; the escape rate, top defects by severity, and open S1/S2 count from the previous sprint are reviewed; action items are assigned owners and tracked in the defect tracker.

25

Quality Advocacy — embed quality into team culture

2.5→8.0

What it is. Quality advocacy is the ongoing practice of embedding quality thinking into the product team's culture — not as a QA-owned function but as a shared team responsibility. It includes: leading quality workshops, writing quality-first engineering guidelines, making quality metrics visible, and celebrating defect prevention rather than defect detection. Popularised in the testing community by Lisa Crispin and Janet Gregory in "Agile Testing" (2009).

Why it matters. When quality is owned exclusively by QA, developers ship code with implicit quality debt and QA becomes a bottleneck. Advocacy moves quality left — into design, into daily code review, into stand-up conversations — where it costs less to apply.

How we will roll it out. The QA engineer leads one quality workshop per quarter; quality guidelines are posted in the developer handbook; quality metrics are reviewed in every sprint review, not just QA-internal meetings.
