Lessons from production — patterns the postmortem corpus will teach us

This page is the pattern layer above the postmortem corpus: rules that hold across incidents, citable at design time. The corpus is currently empty — no real incident has happened yet — so this page holds the editorial contract for how patterns will earn their place, and nothing else.

Why this page exists

A postmortem describes one incident in detail and produces SMART action items that prevent its exact recurrence. That is the load-bearing work; without it the next incident has nothing to point at. But a postmortem corpus is also a corpus — patterns repeat, and the second time a class of incident appears it is too late to call it a surprise. This page is where the cross-cutting patterns will live so they can be cited at design time, not at the next on-call shift.

Current state — no incidents on file

The postmortem corpus is empty. An earlier version of this page extracted five patterns from synthetic example incidents; they were retired on 2026-07-14 because a pattern grounded in an invented incident is fiction with citations. The service postmortem indexes (api · datastore · portal) stay honest-empty until the first real incident.

The first pattern lands here when at least two real postmortems support it — see the bar defined under Forbidden patterns.

How a pattern earns its place

  1. An incident happens and gets a postmortem — written from the template within 48 hours of resolution, with a timeline, root cause, and SMART action items.
  2. The postmortem is compared against the corpus. If a second incident shares the same failure class, the shared rule is drafted here with links to both postmortems as evidence.
  3. The rule states its "would have" test — what design-time behaviour change would have prevented both incidents. A rule that cannot be applied as a check at design time does not qualify.
  4. The rule is re-reviewed after every major architectural change, and narrowed or removed when a new postmortem contradicts it.

Forbidden patterns

  • Reading the postmortem instead of citing it. A postmortem describes the specific incident; this page describes the rule. Both are useful; conflating them turns one-off lessons into vague generalities.
  • Adding a pattern from a single incident. The bar is "this rule, applied at the time, would have prevented at least two postmortems on file (or one + a plausible counterfactual that we can name)". Single-incident rules go in the postmortem's action items, not here.
  • Lessons without a "would have" test. A lesson that does not name what behaviour change it asks for is a thought, not a lesson. Every rule on this page can be applied as a check at design time.
  • Ignoring a contradiction. When a new postmortem produces evidence that contradicts a listed pattern, the contradiction is data. Either narrow the pattern's scope or remove it. Patterns that survive contradiction become dogma.
