Three Amigos — Dev × QA × PM session before every story

A 30-minute structured conversation where developer, QA engineer, and product manager align on acceptance criteria and edge cases before implementation begins.

TL;DR

Three Amigos is a pre-implementation session (30 min max) where developer, QA, and PM answer three questions about a story: what does done look like? What edge cases must be handled? What could go wrong? The PM provides business context; the developer provides technical constraints; QA provides test scenarios and edge cases. Misalignments discovered here cost 30 minutes to fix; the same misalignment discovered during testing costs days. The session output is a completed acceptance criteria list that serves as the test oracle for the story.

What it is

Three Amigos is a structured pre-implementation session involving three perspectives on every user story: the developer (how will we build it?), the QA engineer (how will we test it?), and the product manager (what does done look like for the user?). The session takes 30 minutes maximum and happens during sprint planning or refinement, before the story moves into "In Progress."

The session has three explicit goals:

  1. Align on acceptance criteria: What specific outcomes must be true for the story to be considered complete? Acceptance criteria are written in plain language, not technical terms, so the PM, developer, and QA engineer all mean the same thing when they say "done."
  2. Identify edge cases: What inputs, states, or conditions might cause problems? QA engineers are trained to think about edge cases; the session surfaces them before implementation, when they are cheap to address.
  3. Expose missing requirements: QA's questions often reveal requirements the PM assumed were obvious but did not document. These gaps, discovered in Three Amigos, cost 30 minutes to clarify; discovered in QA testing, they cost days of rework.

The output of Three Amigos is a completed acceptance criteria list attached to the story ticket. This list serves as the test oracle for QA — the criteria define what QA will test against. Stories that have not gone through Three Amigos do not move into "In Progress."

Why we need it

The most expensive defects are requirement defects — where the implemented feature matches the specification but the specification was wrong or incomplete. These defects are invisible during implementation and unit testing because the tests verify the (incorrect) implementation against the (incorrect) specification. They surface only when QA tests against user expectations or when users encounter the feature in production.

Three Amigos catches requirement defects before implementation. A 30-minute conversation before development costs less than a 2-day rework cycle after QA testing reveals a misalignment between what was built and what was needed.

How others do it

George Dinwiddie (Three Amigos originator)

George Dinwiddie coined the "Three Amigos" term in the BDD community around 2010. Dinwiddie's insight was that each of the three perspectives brings information that the other two lack: the PM knows the business context the developer and QA may not know; the developer knows the technical constraints the PM and QA may not know; the QA engineer knows the edge cases and failure modes that the developer and PM have not considered. No two-party conversation covers all three information types. The third party is always the one who spots what the other two missed.

What we take: Three is the minimum — not two. A Dev-PM conversation without QA misses the edge case perspective. A Dev-QA conversation without PM misses the business context. All three perspectives are required.

Source: Agile Alliance — Three Amigos

Gojko Adzic (Specification by Example)

Gojko Adzic's "Specification by Example" (2011) formalises the Three Amigos concept as the foundation of BDD specification. Adzic documents case studies from organisations that adopted specification sessions before implementation and found 30–60% reductions in rework caused by misunderstood requirements. His key finding: the value of the session is not the document produced but the conversation that produced it — the shared understanding of what the system must do and, critically, what it must not do. The document is just the record of the shared understanding.

What we take: The primary output of Three Amigos is shared understanding, not a document. If the session produces a document but the participants leave with different mental models, the session failed.

Source: Gojko Adzic — Specification by Example

Pivotal Labs (story inception)

Pivotal Labs' agile consulting practice extends Three Amigos into "story inception" sessions for complex features. Where standard Three Amigos is 30 minutes for a single story, story inception for a complex feature may be a half-day session involving QA, development, product, and UX. Pivotal's approach generates example scenarios for every acceptance criterion, turning the acceptance criteria into executable tests before implementation begins. This "test-first" specification approach means the test cases exist before the code exists — the code is written to pass the tests, not the tests written to match the code.

What we take: For complex features, extend Three Amigos to include UX and generate example scenarios from the acceptance criteria. For simple stories, keep it at 30 minutes — the value is in the conversation, not the duration.

Source: Tanzu (Pivotal) — Three Amigos in BDD

Anatomy

Three participants

Developer, QA engineer, and product manager. All three must be present. A two-party session is not Three Amigos — it misses one perspective.

30-minute time box

Hard cap. If the session runs over, the story is too large or too ambiguous to proceed — split it or return it to the backlog for clarification.

Acceptance criteria output

The session produces a written acceptance criteria list attached to the story ticket. This list is the test oracle for QA — the criteria define what will be tested.

Pre-sprint gate

No story moves to "In Progress" without a completed Three Amigos session. This is enforced in the sprint planning ceremony — stories without completed criteria are blocked.

Common pitfalls

PitfallWhy it happensWhat to do instead
QA absent from the session Three Amigos is treated as a refinement session for the PM and developer. QA is considered overhead and removed to save time. QA is the most valuable participant in Three Amigos — the edge case and failure mode perspective is exactly what the session exists to surface. QA participation is mandatory, not optional.
Session runs past 30 minutes regularly The stories are too large, too ambiguous, or the team is resolving design decisions in the session rather than using it to clarify agreed requirements. Enforce the 30-minute cap strictly. A story that requires more than 30 minutes to align on criteria is too large — split it before the session, not during it.
No written output The session produces a verbal agreement that is not recorded. Team members remember the conversation differently; the verbal understanding degrades over the sprint. Require written acceptance criteria attached to the ticket before the session ends. The PM or QA engineer writes while the session proceeds. If there is no written output, the session did not happen.
