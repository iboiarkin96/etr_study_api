Quality Dashboarding — make quality metrics visible to the whole team

A shared, always-current view of five quality metrics that makes quality the team's concern rather than a QA-only afterthought.

TL;DR

A quality dashboard displays five metrics updated each sprint: escape rate, open defect count by severity, code coverage, mean time to triage, and CI pass rate. When quality metrics are visible to the whole team — not just QA — quality becomes a shared concern. Developers who see their feature's open defect count rise are more likely to prioritise bug fixes. The dashboard is published on the internal portal; each metric has a target and a trend indicator. A metric below target for two consecutive sprints triggers a retrospective action item.

What it is

A quality dashboard is a shared, always-current view of quality metrics that is visible to the entire team — not just to QA. It is published on the internal portal and updated at the end of each sprint. The five metrics tracked are:

  1. Escape rate: The fraction of defects found in production rather than in QA. Target: <10% overall; 0% for Blocker/Critical. Trend direction: down.
  2. Open defect count by severity: How many open defects are there at each severity level? Target: 0 Blockers, 0 Criticals at release. Trend: decreasing or stable.
  3. Code coverage: Line coverage percentage across the test suite. Target: ≥80%. Trend: stable or increasing.
  4. Mean time to triage: The average hours from defect filing to completed triage (both severity and priority assigned). Target: <24 hours. Trend: stable or decreasing.
  5. CI pass rate: The fraction of CI runs that pass all gates without any manual bypass. Target: ≥95%. Trend: stable or increasing.

Each metric has a target value and a trend indicator (↑/↓ from the previous sprint). A metric below target for two consecutive sprints automatically generates a retrospective action item.

The key design principle of the dashboard is that it is a team artefact, not a QA performance report. The goal is to make quality everybody's concern. When developers can see their feature's open defect count and the team's overall escape rate, quality becomes a shared responsibility rather than a QA afterthought.

Why we need it

Quality metrics that are visible only to QA are not team metrics — they are QA reporting. The team does not act on information it does not see. When the escape rate is 20% and developers do not know this, they do not invest in fixing the gap. When they can see the number on the shared dashboard, the conversation changes from "is quality good enough?" to "the escape rate went up this sprint — what happened?"

A dashboard also creates accountability for improvement. When a metric target is set and the team can see whether it is being met, the target is real. Without visibility, quality goals are aspirational; with a dashboard, they are measurable commitments.

How others do it

DORA (metrics framework)

DORA's Four Keys (deployment frequency, lead time, change failure rate, MTTR) are the most widely adopted shared quality metrics in the industry. DORA's research shows that high-performing organisations make these metrics visible to the entire engineering organisation — not just to the SRE team. The key insight from DORA's research: organisations that do not measure do not improve. The act of measurement and visibility itself drives improvement, independent of the specific metric values.

What we take: Publish quality metrics to the whole team, not just to QA. The DORA principle: what is measured is managed; what is visible is improved.

Source: DORA — Four Keys metrics

Spotify (squad health checks)

Spotify's Squad Health Check model uses a visible, team-owned dashboard of health indicators across multiple dimensions: testing, deployment, process health, and team morale. Each indicator is RAG (red/amber/green) rated by the squad, not by management. The visibility model is key: the health check is displayed in the team's area and reviewed at a monthly rhythm. Spotify's approach makes quality a squad-level concern rather than a central QA function concern — squads own their quality metrics and their improvement trajectories.

What we take: Make the dashboard a squad-level artefact that the squad owns, not a QA report that QA produces. The team should feel ownership of the metrics, not measured by them.

Source: Spotify — Squad Health Check

Grafana Labs (dashboard culture)

Grafana Labs publishes their engineering quality dashboards using their own Grafana product. Their engineering culture document describes "Big Visible Charts" — quality metrics displayed on large monitors in the engineering area so they are always visible without active inspection. Key metrics include test suite health, pipeline success rates, and open issues by priority. Grafana's approach demonstrates that the tooling used to build the dashboard is secondary to the cultural practice of making metrics visible and acting on them.

What we take: The dashboard does not need to be technically impressive — it needs to be always visible and always current. A simple portal page updated each sprint is sufficient if the team actually looks at it.

Source: Grafana Labs Engineering Blog

Anatomy

Five metric tiles

Escape rate / open defects by severity / code coverage / mean triage time / CI pass rate. Each tile shows current value, trend vs previous sprint, and target.

Target and alert rule

Each metric has a documented target. Two consecutive sprints below target automatically generates a retrospective action item assigned to QA lead.

Sprint update cadence

Dashboard updated within 24 hours of sprint close. QA lead is responsible for data collection and update. The sprint review includes a 5-minute dashboard walk-through.

Team ownership

The dashboard is a team artefact shown in sprint review. It is not a QA performance report — all metrics reflect the team's output, not the QA engineer's output alone.

Common pitfalls

PitfallWhy it happensWhat to do instead
Dashboard not visible to developers The dashboard is produced by QA and shared only with the QA team or QA management. Developers never see their feature's quality metrics. Publish the dashboard on the internal portal and include a 5-minute walk-through in every sprint review. Quality metrics must be part of the sprint narrative, not a separate QA report.
Too many metrics The dashboard tracks 15+ metrics. Nobody can hold 15 metrics in mind; the dashboard becomes background noise. Track five metrics maximum. More metrics dilute attention. If a metric does not drive a decision or an action, remove it.
Metrics without targets The dashboard shows numbers but not whether those numbers are good or bad. Teams see 83% coverage but don't know if that is excellent or concerning. Every metric has a target. The dashboard must show whether the current value is above or below target — not just the current value. RAG status (green/amber/red) makes this instant to read.
