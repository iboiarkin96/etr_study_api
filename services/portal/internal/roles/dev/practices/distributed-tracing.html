Distributed Tracing — OpenTelemetry spans on every call

The engineer wraps the interesting business operation in a tracer.start_as_current_span("compute_quote") and adds attributes for the inputs that matter — and gets, for free, an end-to-end waterfall showing exactly which call burned the 800 ms.

TL;DR

OpenTelemetry is the CNCF-incumbent standard for distributed traces; it was the merger of OpenTracing and OpenCensus in 2019 and is now the universally accepted wire format. The intellectual ancestor is Google's Dapper paper (Sigelman et al., 2010), which Charity Majors and Honeycomb turned into product. The developer's job is small but non-negotiable: auto-instrumentation gives you HTTP/DB spans for free; manual spans on the interesting business operations are what convert "the request was slow" into "the third call to pricing.compute_quote took 820 ms because the FX cache missed."

What it is

Distributed tracing records, for one user request, every operation it caused across every service — as a tree of timed spans. The set of spans that share a trace_id is the trace. Each span has a parent, a start/end, attributes (key-value tags), and events (point-in-time logs).

The vocabulary the engineer uses:

  • trace_id — a 128-bit ID generated at the request boundary; propagated through every downstream call.
  • span — one operation: an HTTP call, a DB query, a unit of business logic. Has a name, a duration, attributes, and a parent.
  • context propagation — the W3C traceparent header carries the trace + parent span across HTTP / gRPC / queues so the next service knows where to attach its spans.
  • sampling — head sampling (decide at the root: keep 5% of normal traffic, 100% of errors) or tail sampling (keep traces matching a rule). 100% sampling crushes the backend and the wallet.
  • exporter — the SDK pushes spans via OTLP to a collector, which fans out to Tempo / Jaeger / Honeycomb / Datadog APM.

The developer's surface is small: install the OpenTelemetry SDK, enable auto-instrumentation for the HTTP/DB/queue libraries, and add manual spans around the business operations that matter — pricing.compute_quote, fraud.score_user, image.transcode.

Why we need it

In a system with five services and three databases, a slow request can be slow in any of them. Logs and metrics alone make the diagnosis a scavenger hunt across five tabs, five dashboards, and five clock-skewed timelines. A trace collapses the same investigation to one waterfall: the longest bar is the answer, and its attributes tell you why. The diagnostic speedup is empirically two orders of magnitude — Honeycomb's customer studies and Google's Dapper paper both put it around 30-second answers vs 30-minute hunts.

For the engineer writing the code, the value is also forward-looking: a manual span around compute_quote with currency and line_items as attributes is the production-only profiler you wish you'd had when designing the function. You see latency by input shape, with zero local-repro effort.

How others do it

Honeycomb (Charity Majors)

Honeycomb's product thesis is "observability is high-cardinality, high-dimensionality tracing." Their guide is the canonical industry reference; the team's editorial line is that aggregates lie and that the unit of analysis must be the individual event (span) with rich attributes. They push hard on wide events — every span gets every relevant attribute attached so downstream slicing is unconstrained.

What we take: Add attributes generously on spans. Cardinality is a feature, not a bug, of a trace store. The engineer who attached customer_tier and feature_flag_variant to a span gives the future on-call the slicing dimensions they will need.

Source: Honeycomb — Distributed Tracing: A Complete Guide

OpenTelemetry (CNCF)

OpenTelemetry is the merger of OpenTracing and OpenCensus, now CNCF-incubating since 2019 and the default trace API in essentially every modern language stack. The OTel project ships SDKs, an OTLP wire protocol, an exporter pipeline (the OTel Collector), and a growing library of semantic conventions — agreed-upon attribute names so that http.method means the same thing whether emitted from a Java SDK or a Rust SDK.

What we take: Use OpenTelemetry. Follow the semantic conventions verbatim. The day you switch from Jaeger to Tempo to Honeycomb, you change one exporter line and ship — because every span is already in the standard shape.

Source: OpenTelemetry concepts

Google (Dapper paper, 2010)

Sigelman et al.'s Dapper paper is the foundational design: a single trace_id per user request, low-overhead sampled instrumentation, propagation across all internal RPCs. The paper reports <1% overhead at 1/1000 sampling and concrete diagnostic wins. Every modern tracing system is a recognisable descendant of this design.

What we take: Sampling is the only way this is affordable at scale. Head-sample normal traffic at 1–5% and force 100% for errors and high-latency outliers via tail sampling at the collector. Never run 100% sampling in production without a budget and a kill switch.

Source: Dapper — Google Research

Anatomy of a useful trace

Auto-instrumentation, then manual spans

Auto-instrument every HTTP client, server, ORM, and queue library — free. Then add manual spans around 5–15 business operations per service. Auto without manual is a forest of GET /users spans with no business semantics.

W3C traceparent propagation

Every outbound HTTP, gRPC, and queue message carries the traceparent header. Without it, the next service starts a new trace and the waterfall is broken. The OTel SDK does this automatically — but only for libraries it instruments.

Span attributes follow semantic conventions

Use http.request.method, db.system, messaging.destination.name as the OTel spec defines them. Custom domain attributes (customer.tier, quote.currency) live under your own namespace. Never overload a standard name.

Sampling at the right layer

Head-sample at the root (5% of normal traffic). Tail-sample at the collector to force-keep traces with error=true or duration_ms>1000. Budget the spans-per-second the backend can take and set the rate below it.

Error and exception recording

When a span catches an exception, call span.record_exception(e) and set span.set_status(StatusCode.ERROR). The trace UI lights up the failing span; without this the trace is silent about what went wrong.

Linked to logs and metrics

The structured logger reads the current span and stamps trace_id/span_id onto every log line (see practice 16). RED-method metrics use exemplars pointing back to a sampled trace. One click in the dashboard lands on the offending span.

Common pitfalls

PitfallWhy it happensWhat to do instead
Only auto-instrumented spans — no manual business contextThe team turned on the SDK and called it done. The trace has thirty SELECT and GET /v1/... spans but no compute_quote, no score_fraud, no idea what the request was trying to do.Pick the 5–15 business operations per service that mean something to a non-engineer. Wrap each in a manual span with a domain-shaped name and 3–6 attributes. Code review check: "does this PR add or update spans for the new operation?"
100% sampling crushes the collector and the billSampling sounds optional during pilot, "we're not at scale yet." Then a traffic spike pushes the OTel collector to OOM and drops every span — including the ones from the incident you're trying to diagnose.Default head-sample 5% in prod from day one. Tail-sample to force 100% of errors and slow outliers. Sample budget per service is a SLO, monitored, and the kill switch is one config flag.
trace_id is dropped at the queue boundaryAuto-instrumentation covers HTTP and gRPC but not your in-house message format. The producer adds the span; the consumer starts a new trace; the cross-service waterfall ends at the queue.Inject traceparent into message headers explicitly when producing, extract it explicitly when consuming.
