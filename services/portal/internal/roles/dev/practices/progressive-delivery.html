Progressive Delivery — canary, blue-green, ramped

Every deploy reaches a 1% slice first, gets compared to the baseline on automated health metrics, and only proceeds if those gates stay green.

TL;DR

The term "progressive delivery" was coined by James Governor (RedMonk, 2018) as the umbrella for canary, blue-green, and ramped rollout patterns plus the automation that makes them safe to run on every deploy. The new version takes 1% of traffic; automated health gates compare error rate, p99 latency, and a business KPI between new and old; the rollout pauses or rolls back automatically if any gate fails. Argo Rollouts (Kubernetes) and Flagger are the open-source standard; Netflix's Spinnaker is the historical reference implementation. The principle: bugs are revealed by users at 1% scale at a hundredth of the cost compared to 100%.

What it is

Progressive Delivery is the umbrella name for a family of rollout patterns plus the automation that makes them safe: canary (a small slice of users sees the new version), blue-green (an entire new fleet runs in parallel and traffic flips instantly), and ramped/percentage rollouts (traffic shifts gradually). James Governor of RedMonk coined the umbrella term in 2018 to capture the shift from "deploy is a moment" to "deploy is a controlled process."

The mechanism: a controller (Argo Rollouts, Flagger, Spinnaker) takes a deployment and a "promotion strategy" — typically a list of traffic percentages with hold windows: 1% for 15 min → 5% for 30 min → 25% for 1 h → 50% for 2 h → 100%. At each step, an automated analysis runs: it queries Prometheus / Datadog / a metrics provider for the new and old cohorts on a set of metrics (error rate, p99 latency, success rate, sometimes a business KPI). If all metrics are within tolerance, the rollout advances; otherwise it pauses or rolls back.

"Rollback" in this world is not a redeploy — it is a traffic-weight change. The old version is still running; shifting 100% of traffic back to it takes seconds, not minutes. This is the core difference from traditional deploys.

Why we need it

A "deploy to all users" change exposes 100% of traffic to any latent bug simultaneously. A 0.2% regression that was invisible in staging — because staging has 1% of production load and none of the long-tail traffic patterns — becomes immediately visible in production dashboards but at maximum cost: every user is affected, the incident is high-severity, the rollback is a redeploy. With canary, that same 0.2% regression manifests at 1% of users, observable within 15 minutes, with two orders of magnitude smaller blast radius.

Progressive delivery also breaks the false binary between "deploy now" and "wait." A change that is unsafe for 100% of traffic might be perfectly safe for 5%; you discover the safe ceiling empirically rather than by guessing. Combined with the automated health gates, the deploy decision is data-driven on every promotion, not pundit-driven on day one.

How others do it

Netflix — Spinnaker

Netflix built Spinnaker (open-sourced 2015) as the canonical continuous-delivery platform for canary deploys at scale. Their "Kayenta" component does the automated canary analysis: it compares Atlas metrics between the canary and baseline clusters and produces a verdict. Every major service at Netflix deploys through Spinnaker; deploys are continuous and most rollbacks are automated.

What we take: Automated canary analysis (ACA) is a distinct, hard problem from the deploy mechanics. Decide what to compare, over what window, with what statistical test, and what counts as "safe to proceed" — before you wire up the rollout controller. Kayenta and its successors do this part of the work; the deploy controller is the easier half.

Source: netflixtechblog.com

Intuit — Argo Rollouts

Intuit has publicly documented its adoption of Argo Rollouts across its TurboTax and QuickBooks platforms. Their pattern: every service runs canary on every deploy, with hold windows tuned per service criticality (tax-filing days use longer holds and higher thresholds). The analysis template is shared across services so the team uses one set of well-tested metrics rather than reinventing per-service.

What we take: Per-service tuning over a shared template, not per-service from-scratch. The analysis template (error rate, p99 latency, success rate, business KPI) is org-wide; the thresholds and hold windows are per-service. This keeps the rollout system simple to operate while letting critical services demand stricter gates.

Source: argo-rollouts.readthedocs.io

Google — canary across the fleet

Google's SRE Workbook documents a canary process where every binary push goes through a multi-stage rollout: a single task, a single cluster, a small zone, a region, then the fleet. Health checks at each stage gate the next. The principle they articulate clearly: "the goal is not zero defects but bounded blast radius." A bad binary affects N tasks, not the entire planet.

What we take: Multi-stage rollouts (zone → region → fleet) compound the benefit of canary. Even at 100% of one region, you're still at < 30% of total traffic; the gate at "promote to next region" catches issues that didn't show in the single-region canary. Geographic staging is essentially free if your service is already multi-region.

Source: sre.google/workbook

The repeating pattern: predefined ramp steps with hold windows, automated analysis comparing new vs. baseline cohorts, and an explicit rollback as a traffic-weight change. Teams that adopt ramps without the analysis just have slower deploys; teams that add the analysis get the safety story.

Anatomy of a progressive rollout

Ramp schedule

Typical: 1% / 5% / 25% / 50% / 100% with hold windows of 15 min / 30 min / 1 h / 2 h. Configured per service in the rollout CRD; tightens for higher-criticality services.

Analysis template

Shared org-wide: error rate, p99 latency, success rate, one business KPI per service. PromQL or Datadog queries baked into the template; per-service tuning is on thresholds, not on which metric.

Health gates

Concrete numbers, not vibes. Example: error rate < baseline + 2σ over 10 min; p99 < baseline × 1.2; success rate ≥ baseline − 0.5%. Failing any one pauses the rollout.

Auto-rollback path

Failed gate → traffic weight flips to 100% old version within seconds. The new pods stay around for diagnostics; only routing changes. Targets < 60 s for the routing change itself.

Statistical-significance window

First step (1%) needs enough traffic for a detectable effect on the chosen metrics. For low-traffic services, set the minimum first step at "≥ 500 requests in the window" rather than 1%; otherwise the gate is noise.

Manual gate at the riskiest step

Most teams require an explicit human approval to promote from 25% → 100% on changes touching payments, auth, or data writes. The automation is fast; the human is the safety net at the high-cost step.

Common pitfalls

PitfallWhy it happensWhat to do instead
No metric to gate on, just hopeThe team wires up Argo Rollouts with a traffic ramp but never defines the analysis template. The rollout "pauses for 15 minutes" at each step, but nobody is checking anything; it's a slow deploy with theatre.Concrete analysis template before the ramp: error rate, p99 latency, success rate, one business KPI. Specific PromQL queries, specific thresholds, specific windows. If you don't have the metrics, fix that first — the rollout system without metrics is strictly worse than a single-step deploy.
Rollback runs the canary in reverse — double-deployThe team treats "rollback" as "deploy the previous version," which itself goes through the canary ramp. A high-severity incident now takes 30 more minutes to resolve while the rollback canaries safely back through 1% → 5% → 25%.Rollback is a traffic-weight flip, not a deploy. Keep the previous version's pods running until the rollout completes. Define an "emergency abort" path that bypasses the analysis — high-sev incidents trump statistical rigour.
10% of traffic isn't enough for stat-sigThe service handles 50 requests/min. At 1%, that's 0.5 requests/min — not even one in the 10-min window. The gate either passes trivially (no failed requests because no requests) or flaps on a single 500.Set a minimum-traffic floor for the first step: e.g., "wait until 500 requests in the new cohort before evaluating gates." For low-volume services, start the ramp at 25% or 50% — the math of stat-sig doesn't care about your percentage, it cares about the request count.
