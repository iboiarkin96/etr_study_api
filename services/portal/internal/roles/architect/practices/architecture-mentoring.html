Architecture Mentoring — grow the next architects

Deliberately transfer architectural thinking so the team multiplies and the architect stops being a bottleneck.

TL;DR

Architecture Mentoring is a deliberate practice of transferring architectural thinking to senior engineers growing into architect roles. It combines pair-design sessions, architecture reading groups, shadowed design reviews, and 1:1s with explicit growth goals. Codified by Will Larson in Staff Engineer (2021). Architects who do not mentor become bottlenecks; every design decision funnels through one person, slowing the company. A team of ten engineers who think architecturally moves faster than a solo architect with ten implementors.

What it is

Architecture Mentoring is the deliberate practice of building architectural thinking in senior engineers. It is not the same as project mentoring or general career mentoring — it specifically targets the skill set of designing systems at the level of an architect. Four complementary activities cover the territory:

  • Pair-design sessions — the architect and the mentee co-author a design doc together. The mentee drives; the architect coaches. The doc is real work, not a teaching exercise. Weekly 1–2 hour sessions.
  • Architecture reading group — a small group reads canonical texts together (Designing Data-Intensive Applications, Building Evolutionary Architectures, Staff Engineer, Team Topologies). Monthly discussion, 60 minutes, with focused questions.
  • Shadowed design reviews — mentees attend architecture reviews as active participants, not observers. They are expected to give feedback. The architect debriefs afterward.
  • Growth 1:1s — monthly 1:1s with explicit architectural growth goals. Not project status; not career generalities. Specific competencies: "this quarter, lead one design doc end-to-end."

The goal: by the end of the mentoring engagement (typically 6–12 months), the mentee can author and lead design reviews independently, and is recognised by the team as a credible architectural voice.

Why we need it

Architects who do not mentor become bottlenecks. Every significant design decision funnels through one person. The company's growth velocity is capped at the architect's individual throughput. When the architect leaves, takes parental leave, or is reassigned, architectural decisions stall.

A mentored team of 10 senior engineers who think architecturally moves faster than a solo architect with 10 implementors. The throughput scales because decisions can be parallelised — multiple design docs in flight at once, each authored by a competent owner. The mentoring practice also creates succession: the next architect comes from inside the team, with full context, instead of being hired externally with months of ramp-up. Will Larson's "the work of a Staff Engineer is to multiply the team" applies precisely here.

How others do it

Will Larson — Staff Engineer (2021)

Will Larson's Staff Engineer (2021) is the canonical reference for architect/staff engineer growth and mentoring. The book defines four staff archetypes (Tech Lead, Architect, Solver, Right Hand) and includes 14 interviews with practicing staff engineers about how they grew and how they grow others. Larson's core argument: the staff engineer's work is to multiply the team's output, and mentoring is the highest-leverage way to do that. His blog (lethain.com) extends the framework with hundreds of essays.

What we take: Use Larson's archetype framework with mentees. The "Architect" archetype is specific — it is not "senior engineer with more years." Helping a mentee identify which archetype they are growing into clarifies the development path.

Source: StaffEng — Will Larson

Camille Fournier — The Manager's Path

Camille Fournier's The Manager's Path (2017, O'Reilly) is the canonical reference for engineering leadership, including a chapter on the relationship between engineering management and technical leadership. Fournier's guidance applies to architect mentoring: explicit growth goals, regular feedback, and creating "stretch opportunities" where the mentee leads work slightly beyond their current capability. Her blog (elidedbranches.com) and Twitter feed extend the framework with practical scenarios.

What we take: Create stretch opportunities deliberately. The mentee should be leading work slightly beyond their current capability, with the architect as a safety net — not work they could already do alone (no growth) or work they cannot do (failure).

Source: Camille Fournier — elidedbranches

Google — readability and design mentorship

Google formalises peer-mentoring through its "readability" programme (engineers learning to write idiomatic code in a language) and through architecture mentoring via senior staff engineers. The L6+ engineer's responsibility explicitly includes growing L5 engineers into L6 candidates. Google's promotion process surfaces "scope" and "impact" as the primary criteria for senior levels — both of which require mentoring as a multiplier, not just personal output.

What we take: Make mentoring a measured part of the architect role, not a side activity. The architect's performance review should include mentee growth as a primary signal alongside their own design output.

Source: Software Engineering at Google

Anatomy

Mentee Identification

2–3 mentees at a time. Selected for: existing strong engineering judgement, desire to grow into architecture, and current scope adjacent to the architect's. Not all senior engineers want to become architects — that is fine. Mentoring is for those who do.

Pair-Design Cadence

Weekly 1–2 hour sessions co-authoring real design docs. The mentee drives the keyboard and the structure; the architect coaches on what questions to ask, what alternatives to consider, what risks to flag. Over time, the architect's contribution should shrink as the mentee internalises the process.

Explicit Growth Goals

Each mentee has 2–3 named quarterly growth goals. Examples: "lead one design doc end-to-end this quarter", "facilitate one architecture review", "give one tech talk on a deep technical topic." Goals are reviewed at the monthly 1:1.

Structured Feedback

Feedback on every design doc the mentee authors. Format: 3 things that worked, 2 things to improve next time. Specific, not general ("the alternatives section was thorough — consider adding cost comparison next time"). Vague feedback ("good job!") does not transfer learning.

Graduation Criteria

The mentoring engagement has an end: the mentee can author and lead design reviews independently without coaching, and is recognised by peers as a credible architectural voice. Typically reached in 6–12 months. After graduation, the mentee becomes a mentor for the next cohort.

Common pitfalls

Pitfall Why it happens What to do instead
Architect does the work themselves "because it's faster" The mentee struggles, the architect impatiently takes the keyboard, and the design doc gets written quickly. The mentee learns nothing; the dependency on the architect grows. Let the mentee struggle. The struggle is the learning. Set time boxes: "if you're stuck in 30 minutes, ask me a specific question." Coach the question, not the answer.
Mentoring framed as career advice, not technical growth 1:1s become general career conversations: promotions, politics, vague aspirations. The technical mentoring component disappears. The mentee may feel supported but does not grow architecturally. Reserve a separate slot specifically for technical growth. Career conversations are valuable but distinct. The architectural mentoring 1:1 has a technical agenda: which design doc are we working on, what was the last thing reviewed, what is the growth goal this quarter.
Picking mentees who do not actually want the role The architect identifies a strong engineer and starts mentoring them toward an architect role — but the engineer wanted to stay an IC, or grow into management. The engagement fizzles. Have an explicit conversation at the start: "do you want to grow into architecture?" Some engineers are excellent senior individual contributors and do not want the role. Respect the answer; find mentees who want the path.
