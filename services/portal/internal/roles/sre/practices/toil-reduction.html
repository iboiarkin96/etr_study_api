Toil Reduction — automate the repetitive or pay for it in burnout

Toil is manual, repetitive operational work that scales with load and provides no enduring value. Google's SRE model caps it at 50% of working time — the other 50% eliminates it through engineering.

TL;DR

Toil is any work that is manual, repetitive, automatable, tactical, devoid of lasting value, and grows linearly with load. The 50% rule: no SRE should spend more than half their time on toil. Toil must be explicitly tracked (in the on-call log or a shared spreadsheet), the top sources identified monthly, and reduction projects assigned with deadlines. Unchecked toil is the primary driver of SRE burnout and the reason on-call rotations collapse.

What it is

Google's SRE Book defines toil by six characteristics — a piece of work is toil if it is:

  • Manual: A human does it by hand rather than a machine.
  • Repetitive: It recurs; it isn't a unique problem.
  • Automatable: A machine could do it with engineering investment.
  • Tactical (reactive): It's triggered by something happening, not a project the team chose to do.
  • Devoid of lasting value: Completing it leaves the system no better than before it was triggered.
  • Scales with load: If the number of services doubles, the toil doubles too (unlike a one-time architectural improvement).

Classic toil examples: manually restarting a service after every deploy, rotating credentials by hand on a fixed schedule, clicking through a set of dashboards to confirm a deploy is healthy, responding to recurring alerts that are always noise.

Why we need it

Uncontrolled toil is the primary driver of on-call burnout. As the system grows, toil grows linearly with load — unless engineering work systematically eliminates it. The 50% rule is a forcing function: when toil crosses the ceiling, the team must automate rather than just hire more SREs to do more of the same manual work.

Toil also degrades alert quality over time. When engineers spend hours per shift on predictable manual tasks, they start treating everything as noise — including alerts that matter. Eliminating toil sharpens the team's situational awareness because the remaining work is genuinely non-routine and worthy of attention.

How others do it

Google SRE

Google introduced the toil concept and the 50% rule in "Site Reliability Engineering" (2016). Their implementation: every SRE tracks toil vs. project work weekly. Managers review the ratio monthly. Teams above 50% toil are required to submit a toil reduction plan and are not allocated new services to operate until the ratio drops below the ceiling.

What we take: Toil tracking must be explicit — not a feeling, but a number derived from the on-call log or ticket system. Without measurement, the 50% rule is meaningless. Classify every on-call ticket as toil or non-toil at close time; aggregate monthly.

Source: Google SRE Book — Eliminating Toil

Stripe

Stripe's infrastructure team uses quarterly "toil sprints" — one week per quarter dedicated exclusively to reducing the top 3 toil sources identified from the previous quarter's on-call data. During toil week, no feature work is accepted. Engineers who reduce toil by the largest magnitude are publicly recognised in the quarterly all-hands.

What we take: Dedicated toil reduction sprints prevent the "we'll automate it when we have time" trap. Time is never found voluntarily; it must be protected. Quarterly sprints also create a rhythm of measurement-then-improvement that compounds over years.

Source: Stripe Engineering Blog

Anatomy

Toil classification at ticket close

Every on-call ticket is tagged toil or non-toil when it's resolved. The tag can be a field in PagerDuty, Jira, or a simple spreadsheet column. Classification takes 5 seconds per ticket and produces monthly data automatically.

Monthly toil review

Top 3 toil sources by hours consumed. Each source gets a named reduction project with a 90-day deadline and an owner. Not a discussion — a decision list with names and dates.

Toil percentage metric

Total toil hours / total on-call hours per month. Track this as an explicit engineering health metric. Alert when it exceeds 50% for two consecutive months.

Automation investment

Every toil reduction project is an engineering project — it needs design, implementation, testing, and monitoring. Budget 1 sprint per major toil source per quarter. Don't try to automate everything at once.
