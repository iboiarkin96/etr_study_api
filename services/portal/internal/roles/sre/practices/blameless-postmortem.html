Blameless Postmortem — systemic causes, not individual errors

A written retrospective published within 5 business days of every P0/P1, focused on systemic causes and improvement actions rather than individual mistakes — the mechanism that converts incidents into institutional learning.

TL;DR

A blameless postmortem covers five sections: factual timeline, impact (quantified), what went well and why, root causes (5 Whys to systemic cause — never to an individual's error), and action items (named owner + deadline). Published to all of engineering within 5 business days. Blameless means the document never names individuals as causes of failure — it names processes, tools, and systems. When postmortems assign blame, engineers hide problems; when they're blameless, engineers contribute transparently and the organisation learns from every incident.

What it is

A blameless postmortem is a written retrospective conducted after every P0 and P1 incident. It is written by the Incident Commander (with input from all responders), published within 5 business days of incident resolution, and shared across all of engineering. The document has five sections:

  1. Timeline: A chronological, factual account of the incident. What happened, when, in what order. No interpretation, no attribution. "14:32: Alert fired. 14:37: IC declared P0. 14:45: DB failover initiated." Not "14:45: Alice initiated a DB failover without consulting the team."
  2. Impact: Quantified outcomes — users affected, duration, revenue impact, SLO budget consumed. Not estimates — actuals from monitoring data.
  3. What went well: Specific decisions and practices that limited the impact or accelerated recovery. Not a list of everything that wasn't a problem — specifically what decisions made a positive difference and why.
  4. Root causes: For each significant failure mode, a 5 Whys analysis that reaches the systemic cause. The root cause is always a process, a tool, or a system design — never an individual. "The engineer deployed without testing" → "The deployment process does not require integration testing before production deployment."
  5. Action items: One action item per root cause. Named owner. Specific deadline. Tracked in the engineering backlog until closed. A postmortem with no action items is a retrospective, not a learning document.

Why we need it

Organisations that assign blame after incidents produce two outcomes: engineers hide problems and avoid transparency, and the same incident recurs because the systemic cause is never fixed. Blameless postmortems produce three outcomes: the systemic cause is identified and fixed, the team learns faster than organisations that hide failure, and individuals feel safe to surface problems early — which prevents incidents before they happen.

Google's SRE culture treats postmortem publication as the most important artifact of incident response — more important than the fix itself. The fix addresses one instance of the problem; the postmortem prevents the class of problems that caused it.

How others do it

Google SRE

Google pioneered blameless postmortems for software systems in the SRE Book. Their key principles: published to all of engineering (not just the team), within 48 hours for P0 incidents (5 days for others), action items tracked to completion in a public register, and a "postmortem review committee" that approves final postmortems for high-impact incidents. Google treats an incomplete postmortem (missing action items or unresolved root causes) as a higher-severity issue than the original incident.

What we take: Publish postmortems to all of engineering, not just the affected team. When every engineer reads every postmortem, the organisation learns from all incidents, not just the ones they directly experienced. The most valuable postmortems are often the ones that reveal a systemic cause shared across multiple services.

Source: Google SRE — Postmortem Culture

Etsy

Etsy popularised "blameless postmortems" as a cultural practice and added one question to the standard template that they consider the most productive: "What would we have needed to know on day 1 to have avoided this?" This question specifically targets the information gaps that caused the failure — which usually reveals process improvements in discovery, monitoring, or brief quality rather than execution.

What we take: Add the "what would we have needed to know earlier?" question to every postmortem. It's more productive than "what went wrong?" because it focuses energy on preventive process changes rather than retrospective fault assignment. The answer is usually "we needed better monitoring" or "we needed to test X before deploying."

Source: Etsy Code as Craft

Anatomy

5 Whys root cause analysis

For each significant failure: ask "why did this happen?" five times. The fifth answer is almost always the systemic root cause. The first answer is almost always the symptom. "Why did the deploy fail?" → ... → "Why is there no staging environment requirement in the DoD?" That is the root cause.

Blameless language

Review every sentence for individual attribution. "Alice pushed an untested change" → "An untested change was deployed to production." "Bob missed the alert" → "The alert routing policy did not page the on-call for this service type." The document describes systems and processes, never individuals' errors.

Action item tracking

Every action item from every postmortem goes to the engineering backlog with a named owner and a deadline. Monthly postmortem review: how many action items are overdue? Overdue action items from postmortems are the leading indicator for repeat incidents.

Publication within 5 days

The 5-day deadline is enforced. After 5 days, incident memory fades and the timeline reconstruction takes twice as long. An incomplete postmortem published within 5 days and updated once is better than a perfect postmortem published after 3 weeks.
