Product Review Cadence — structured rhythm for decisions and accountability

A deliberate meeting rhythm — weekly, monthly, quarterly — that keeps the team aligned without ad hoc escalations and unplanned interruptions.

TL;DR

Product Review Cadence is a structured meeting rhythm at three levels: weekly (squad-level: sprint review, health dashboard review, discovery update), monthly (product-level: OKR progress, roadmap review, cross-squad dependencies), and quarterly (strategic: OKR setting, roadmap reprioritisation, learnings synthesis). Each level has a fixed agenda, a fixed attendee list, and clear decision authority. Ad hoc escalations are minimised because stakeholders trust the next review will address their concern.

What it is

A Product Review Cadence is a deliberately designed rhythm of recurring meetings that creates predictable decision points for the product team and its stakeholders. Without a cadence, product organisations drift into ad hoc review requests, emergency escalations, and one-on-one stakeholder management that consumes PM time without creating alignment.

The cadence operates at three levels:

  • Weekly (squad-level): Sprint review (what shipped, what didn't), health dashboard review (15 minutes on key metrics), discovery update (what was learned from customers this week). Attendees: squad. Duration: 45 minutes.
  • Monthly (product-level): OKR progress review (are we on track?), roadmap update (what changed and why), cross-squad dependencies (what blocks whom). Attendees: product leadership. Duration: 60 minutes.
  • Quarterly (strategic): OKR retrospective and new OKR setting, roadmap reprioritisation, learnings synthesis from the quarter's experiments. Attendees: full product organisation and key stakeholders. Duration: half-day.

Why we need it

In the absence of a structured cadence, every stakeholder request for information triggers an ad hoc review. The PM spends their week in one-on-one meetings explaining the same roadmap to different people, answering "what shipped last week?" in five separate Slack threads, and escalating urgent issues with no predictable resolution time. A cadence solves all of these by creating a shared expectation: "that question will be answered at the monthly review."

The cadence also creates accountability. When OKR progress is reviewed monthly in front of the product leadership team, the PM cannot avoid acknowledging when a key result is off-track. In the absence of structured review, off-track OKRs are discovered at the quarterly review when recovery is no longer possible.

How others do it

Amazon

Amazon's product review cadence is structured around the PR/FAQ document cycle: new initiatives begin with a PR/FAQ review (on-demand), active initiatives are reviewed monthly in the OP1 (Operating Plan) cycle, and the annual OP2 cycle sets strategy for the coming year. The cadence eliminates the need for ad hoc strategy reviews because every leader knows the OP1 cycle is the appropriate venue for major roadmap changes.

What we take: Coupling the review cadence to a document review (not a presentation) changes the quality of input. Leaders who read the document before the meeting ask substantive questions. Leaders who see a slideshow in the meeting ask clarifying questions. Build reading time into the cadence, not just meeting time.

Source: Amazon Working Backwards

Google

Google's product review cadence includes a weekly "product area review" (PARs) at the VP level, where each PM presents a three-minute update: what shipped, what the metrics show, and what the biggest open question is. The rigid time box prevents the meeting from expanding and forces PMs to prepare the most essential information rather than comprehensive decks.

What we take: A rigid three-minute-per-PM format is more effective than a "as long as it takes" format. PMs who must fit their update in three minutes learn to identify what is essential. PMs with unlimited time fill it with what is interesting, not what is actionable.

Source: Google Products Blog

Anatomy

Fixed agenda per level

Each review level has a documented, standing agenda. The agenda does not change unless the team formally revises it in a retrospective. No agenda drift.

Decision authority documented

Each review level has documented decision authority: what can be decided in this meeting without escalation? Decisions outside authority are escalated to the next level.

Written pre-read

Every review level has a written pre-read distributed 24 hours before. No pre-read = meeting rescheduled. The pre-read is the primary information transfer mechanism.

Action items with DRI and date

Every meeting produces a written list of action items: what, who (DRI), by when. Opened at the next meeting and closed or escalated.

Common pitfalls

PitfallWhy it happensWhat to do instead
Cadence meetings become status updates with no decisionsLeaders attend but do not engage — the meeting is a formality.Every cadence meeting must close with at least one decision or one escalated item. A meeting with no decisions or escalations was either redundant or the agenda was wrong.
Ad hoc reviews bypass the cadenceA stakeholder wants immediate attention.Redirect ad hoc requests to the appropriate cadence level: "This will be covered at the monthly product review on [date]." If the request is genuinely urgent (production incident), handle it outside the cadence but don't let urgency collapse into a new pattern of ad hoc reviews.
No pre-reads distributedPMs are too busy to write them before the meeting.The pre-read is 30 minutes of PM time that saves 60 minutes of meeting time. Cancel the meeting if there is no pre-read — this creates the right incentive to prepare.
