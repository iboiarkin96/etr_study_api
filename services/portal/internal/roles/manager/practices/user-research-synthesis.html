User Research Synthesis — from interviews to insights

A repeatable process for turning raw interview data into structured insights the whole team can act on.

TL;DR

Research synthesis converts raw notes and recordings into structured insight cards, affinity diagrams, and empathy maps. Without synthesis, research produces stories; with synthesis, it produces evidence. The process runs within 48 hours of each session and feeds the OST, the product brief, and the roadmap.

What it is

User Research Synthesis is the structured process of converting raw research data — interview recordings, note transcripts, survey responses, session recordings — into insight cards that are tagged, clustered, and rated by severity. The output is a shared insight repository the whole team can read and act on without being present in the original session.

The process has three stages. In the first 24 hours: write one insight card per significant observation, tagging each with theme, segment, and severity (critical / moderate / minor). In days one to three: run an affinity session where cards are clustered into thematic groups and patterns are named. Monthly: run a full synthesis review where the insight backlog is reprioritised and linked to current OST opportunities.

The canonical output artifacts are: an insight card database (the source of truth for all findings), an empathy map (what users say, think, feel, and do), and an affinity diagram (clusters showing which themes appear across multiple sessions).

Why we need it

Research without synthesis is folklore. The PM who conducted the interviews has all the nuance in their head; everyone else has only the summary they heard in a meeting. When the PM leaves or the memory fades, the research value disappears entirely.

A shared insight repository converts individual research memory into institutional knowledge. New team members can read through the insight database and understand six months of customer pain in two hours. Stakeholders who were not in the room can review evidence before challenging a product decision. And the PM can walk into any prioritisation debate with a severity-ranked list of customer problems rather than "I remember a customer saying..."

How others do it

IDEO

IDEO's design thinking process makes synthesis a formal phase with specific tools: affinity diagrams built on physical sticky notes (or Miro), empathy maps structured around the four quadrants (say/think/do/feel), and "How Might We" reframing that converts observations into opportunity prompts.

What we take: The "How Might We" step — converting "users struggle with X" into "How might we make X effortless?" — is the specific moment where research becomes product direction. Build it into every synthesis session.

Source: IDEO Design Thinking

Google Ventures (GV)

GV's Design Sprint includes a structured synthesis step called "How Might We" note-taking during customer interviews, followed by a dot-voting session where the team votes on the most important insights. This distills five hours of research into a ranked list of design challenges within 60 minutes.

What we take: Timebox synthesis to avoid infinite clustering. The GV method proves that 60 minutes is enough to get from raw notes to a prioritised insight list if the format is right.

Source: Sprint book — Jake Knapp

Airbnb

Airbnb's research team maintains a centralised "Experience Library" — an internal database of every research insight tagged by persona, journey stage, and sentiment. Product teams can search the library before starting new discovery, often finding that a question has already been answered by a previous research session.

What we take: A searchable insight database eliminates redundant research. Before booking any new interview, check whether the question has already been answered. The library pays for itself when teams stop re-researching the same problems.

Source: Airbnb Design — Research

Anatomy of an insight card

Observation

What the user said or did, in their words or a direct description. No interpretation yet.

Insight

What the observation means. One sentence. "Users struggle with X because Y."

Theme tag

Controlled vocabulary: Onboarding / Navigation / Trust / Performance / Pricing / etc.

Severity

Critical (blocks task), Moderate (causes friction), Minor (nice to fix). Not subjective: derived from the user's behavior during the session.

Source link

Link to the specific timestamp in the session recording where the observation occurred.

Frequency

How many sessions surfaced this insight. Updated as new sessions complete.

Common pitfalls

PitfallWhy it happensWhat to do instead
Synthesis happens weeks after the interviewPM is busy; synthesis gets deprioritised.Block two hours the same day as the interview for insight cards. Memories fade fast.
Insights written as solutions"We should add a search bar" is a solution, not an insight.Write the observation first. Derive the insight. Solutions come in the OST layer, not the insight card.
Insight database never revisitedCards are added but never linked to product decisions.Run a monthly synthesis review: cluster, reprioritise, and link to current OST opportunities.

Quality bar — what good looks like

  • ○

    Insight card created within 24h of every interview

    At minimum three cards per session.

  • ○

    Insight database is searchable and shared with all team members

    Not a PM-only Notion page.

  • ○

    Monthly synthesis review produces ranked insight themes

    Top three themes linked to current OST opportunities.
