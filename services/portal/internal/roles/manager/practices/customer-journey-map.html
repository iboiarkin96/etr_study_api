Customer Journey Map — end-to-end experience

A visual map of every touchpoint a customer has, showing actions, emotions, pain points, and opportunities at each stage.

TL;DR

A Customer Journey Map is a visualisation of the end-to-end experience a customer has with a product: from awareness through activation, engagement, retention, and referral. Each stage shows customer actions, emotions, pain points, and opportunities. Built from real interview data, it forces the team to see the full experience rather than optimising individual features in isolation.

What it is

A Customer Journey Map is a visual representation of a customer's end-to-end experience with a product, from the first moment of awareness through post-purchase retention and referral. At each stage, the map documents: what the customer is doing (actions), what they are thinking (thoughts), how they are feeling (emotions), and what is going wrong (pain points).

A journey map is grounded in interview data — it represents what real customers actually experience, not what the team hopes they experience. The map is built from at minimum five customer interviews and updated quarterly as the product evolves.

The most valuable part of the map is the emotion curve — a line that charts customer sentiment across stages. The dips in the curve mark the friction points: the places where customers feel frustration, confusion, or disappointment. Those dips are the prioritised opportunities for the OST.

Why we need it

Teams optimising individual features often miss the seams between stages — the handoffs where customers fall out of the journey or get confused about what to do next. A journey map forces the team to consider the whole experience rather than a single feature in isolation.

The map also aligns cross-functional teams. Sales, marketing, support, and engineering often have different mental models of the customer experience. A single shared journey map replaces five different internal perspectives with one customer-grounded reality.

How others do it

Starbucks

Starbucks maps the customer journey from "pre-visit intent" through "in-store experience" to "post-visit loyalty" at a granular level — including the specific moments where mobile order customers and in-store customers intersect. Each touchpoint has a designated owner and a NPS-derived satisfaction score.

What we take: Assign a DRI to each journey stage, not just to the map itself. If nobody owns a stage, pain points in that stage persist indefinitely.

Source: HBR — Customer Experience Truth

Uber

Uber's early growth work was heavily informed by journey mapping that identified the waiting stage (after requesting a ride) as the highest anxiety moment in the journey. This led to the real-time driver location map feature — not because it was technically impressive but because it reduced anxiety at the most emotionally sensitive stage.

What we take: The highest anxiety moment in the journey, not the most used feature, is often the highest-leverage intervention point. Journey maps reveal this.

Source: Uber Design Blog

NN/g (Nielsen Norman Group)

NN/g's canonical journey mapping template includes six rows per stage: actor (who), phase (what stage), actions, thoughts, emotions, and opportunities. Their research shows that teams who map all six rows per stage identify 40% more improvement opportunities than teams who only map actions and pain points.

What we take: Don't skip the emotions row. Cognitive frustration (confusion) and emotional frustration (anxiety) require different solutions, and the journey map is the tool that distinguishes them.

Source: NN/g — Journey Mapping Guide

Anatomy

Persona

One primary persona per map. Multiple personas need separate maps.

Stages

Five to seven phases covering the full lifecycle. Awareness → Activation → Engagement → Retention → Expansion.

Actions

What the customer does at each stage. Sourced from interview data and session recordings, not internal assumptions.

Thoughts

Direct quotes from customers at each stage. Preserves the customer's voice.

Emotion curve

A plotted line of sentiment (positive/negative) across stages. Dips = opportunities, peaks = strengths to protect.

Opportunities

How Might We questions derived from each dip in the emotion curve. These feed directly into the OST.

Common pitfalls

PitfallWhy it happensWhat to do instead
Map built from internal assumptionsNo time or access to do real interviews first.A hypothetical map is useful as a starting point but label it clearly as "assumed." Replace each row with real data within the first month.
Map covers too many personasStakeholders want every user segment represented.Start with one primary persona. Separate maps for separate personas. A combined map shows nothing useful.
Map is a one-time deliverableBuilt in a workshop, presented once, never updated.Update the emotion curve after every quarterly interview synthesis. A 6-month-old map is worse than no map.

Quality bar — what good looks like

  • ○

    Map built from ≥5 customer interviews

    Every row has at least one supporting quote or observation.

  • ○

    Emotion curve plotted and dips ranked by severity

    Top 3 dips linked to OST opportunity nodes.

  • ○

    Map updated quarterly and pinned in team workspace

    Referenced in every sprint review as the "current experience baseline."
