Opportunity Solution Tree — from outcome to experiment

A visual artifact that traces every experiment back to a customer opportunity and a desired outcome.

TL;DR

The Opportunity Solution Tree (Teresa Torres) is a visual map with four levels: desired outcome → customer opportunities → potential solutions → experiments. Every branch is traceable. The OST makes it structurally impossible to work on a solution without a customer opportunity behind it, and impossible to pursue an opportunity without an experiment validating that the solution works.

What it is

The Opportunity Solution Tree is a visual artifact introduced by Teresa Torres in her Continuous Discovery framework. It has four layers:

  1. Desired Outcome — one metric the team is trying to move this quarter, typically a key result from the OKR.
  2. Opportunities — customer needs, pain points, or desires surfaced through weekly interviews. Each opportunity is a real customer problem, not a solution.
  3. Solutions — potential ways to address an opportunity. Multiple solutions are mapped per opportunity to avoid premature commitment.
  4. Experiments — assumption tests that validate whether a solution will address the opportunity without building the full feature.

The tree is built incrementally from weekly interview data. It is not designed in a workshop and then frozen — it is a living document updated continuously as new opportunities are discovered and old ones are promoted, deprioritised, or invalidated.

Why we need it

Without an OST, product decisions travel through an invisible chain of assumptions. "We should add a guided onboarding wizard" sounds like a solution to a problem, but which problem? How do we know it addresses the most important opportunity? How confident are we that a wizard is better than the two other solutions we didn't evaluate?

The OST makes every step in that chain explicit and visible. When a stakeholder proposes a feature, the first question becomes "which opportunity on the tree does this address?" If there is no opportunity on the tree, the discussion becomes "should we add this opportunity based on customer evidence?" — a much more productive conversation than debating whether the feature itself is good.

How others do it

Teresa Torres / Product Talk

The originator of the OST uses it as the backbone of her consulting work with product teams across the industry. Her canonical advice: start with one OKR key result as the outcome, add at least three opportunities from the first month of interviews, and never add a solution without first placing it under an opportunity.

What we take: The tree's value is in the constraint it creates: no solution without an opportunity, no opportunity without customer evidence. Enforce the constraint even when it is uncomfortable.

Source: Product Talk — OST guide

Amplitude

Amplitude's product team uses an OST-like artifact called an "opportunity map" that connects their North Star Metric to specific user friction points surfaced through session recordings and interviews. Each friction point (opportunity) is linked to at least two solution candidates before one is selected for experiment.

What we take: Having at least two solutions per opportunity forces the team to avoid falling in love with the first solution and gives the PM leverage to deprioritise solutions that look good but don't have evidence.

Source: Amplitude — Product Discovery

Notion

Notion's PM team maintains their OST in — appropriately — Notion itself, using a database with linked pages for each node. Opportunities are linked to interview recordings and insight cards. Solutions are linked to design explorations. Experiments are linked to analytics dashboards. The whole tree is browsable in five minutes.

What we take: The OST should be navigable by any team member without a guided tour. If it requires the PM to explain it, it isn't doing its job.

Source: Notion — OST template

Anatomy

Desired Outcome

One metric, directly tied to a KR. Changes once per quarter.

Opportunity node

A customer need, pain, or desire. Written in customer language ("I can't find X when I need it"), not PM language.

Evidence link

Each opportunity links to the interview session(s) that surfaced it. One opportunity per session minimum before promotion to the tree.

Solution nodes (≥2)

At least two candidate solutions per opportunity considered before one is selected. Prevents premature convergence.

Assumption map

Each solution has 2–3 explicit assumptions listed. The riskiest assumption is tested first.

Experiment node

One test per riskiest assumption. Completed before full-build commitment. Results linked back to the assumption.

Common pitfalls

PitfallWhy it happensWhat to do instead
Solutions without opportunitiesStakeholders propose features that bypass the tree.Ask "which opportunity node does this belong under?" before engaging with the solution.
Opportunity written as a solution"We need a dashboard" is a solution, not an opportunity.Rewrite as "I can't see how my team is performing without navigating five different screens."
Tree frozen after initial workshopThe OST is treated as a one-time deliverable.Update the tree within 24 hours of every interview. Staleness kills its value.

Quality bar — what good looks like

  • ○

    OST has ≥5 opportunity nodes with interview evidence

    Each node links to at least one recorded interview.

  • ○

    Every active solution has ≥2 siblings considered

    Documented in the solution node, not just in the PM's memory.

  • ○

    OST reviewed as first item in every product review

    Makes the tree the team's shared operating model, not the PM's private artifact.
