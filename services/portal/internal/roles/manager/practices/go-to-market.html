Go-to-Market Playbook — the plan between "built" and "adopted"

A structured pre-launch plan that coordinates marketing, sales, support, and customer success so that the product reaches the right users in the right way on launch day.

TL;DR

A Go-to-Market (GTM) Playbook is the operational plan for taking a feature or product to market. It covers: target segment (who), positioning and messaging (why this, why now), launch timing and sequencing, sales and support readiness, and success metrics for the launch itself. It is distinct from the product brief (which defines what to build) — the GTM defines how to take the built thing to market. Without a GTM, launches are under-communicated and under-adopted.

What it is

A Go-to-Market Playbook is the operational document that bridges the gap between "the feature is built" and "the feature is adopted." It is written by the PM with input from marketing, sales, and customer success, and covers:

  • Target segment: Which customers are we launching to first? If it's a phased rollout, what is the sequencing criteria?
  • Positioning and messaging: What is the value proposition? What proof points support it? What is the one-sentence pitch each sales rep and support agent should be able to say?
  • Launch timing: GA (general availability) date, or a phased rollout schedule. What are the go/no-go criteria?
  • Readiness checklist: Is the support team trained? Are the help docs live? Has the sales team had a demo? These must be true before the launch date, not on launch day.
  • Launch metrics: What does a successful launch look like at day 7 and day 30? Adoption rate, support ticket volume, sentiment score from in-app surveys.

Why we need it

The most common product failure mode is not shipping the wrong thing — it is shipping the right thing to no one. A feature that took a quarter to build reaches 3% of the eligible user base in the first month because no one outside the engineering team knew it existed. The GTM Playbook closes this gap by making launch communication and readiness as deliberate as the engineering sprint.

The readiness checklist is particularly valuable. Without it, support agents learn about a new feature from the first confused customer who calls about it. Sales reps discover a new feature in the product changelog rather than a pre-launch demo. Both scenarios damage customer relationships and undo the goodwill of shipping a genuinely useful feature.

How others do it

Intercom

Intercom's PM team writes a "launch brief" for every feature of significant scope. The launch brief includes a "what changed for customers" section (not "what we built"), a "what sales needs to know" section (competitive implications, pricing changes), a "what support needs to know" section (common questions, escalation path), and a "launch risk" section (what could go wrong in the first 72 hours and who is on-call to respond).

What we take: The "what could go wrong in the first 72 hours" section forces the team to prepare for the failure modes they would otherwise discover at 11pm on launch day. Designate an on-call PM for every significant launch — someone available to make decisions if early data shows a problem.

Source: Intercom — Product Launches

Stripe

Stripe's developer-facing features go through a three-stage launch: private beta (5 selected customers, PM-managed feedback loop), public beta (announced in changelog, opt-in), and GA (full rollout with documentation, migration guides, and deprecation timeline for old endpoints). Each stage has explicit graduation criteria that must be met before moving forward — beta graduation is a formal PM decision, not automatic.

What we take: Phased launches with explicit graduation criteria are more informative than GA launches. The beta stage surfaces the edge cases and migration friction before all customers are affected. Define beta graduation criteria before the beta starts — otherwise beta never ends.

Source: Stripe Engineering Blog

Figma

Figma uses a "launch readiness review" 2 weeks before every significant feature GA. The review is a 30-minute meeting with PM, marketing, support lead, and sales lead. Each function confirms readiness against a checklist. If any function is not ready, the launch date is adjusted — not the readiness standard. The meeting is chaired by the PM and the agenda is the readiness checklist, not a feature demo.

What we take: The launch readiness review should be a checklist review, not a feature demo. By the time the readiness review happens, every attendee should already know what the feature does. The review answers "are we ready?" not "what is this?"

Source: Figma Product Blog

Anatomy

Target segment and sequencing

Who gets the feature first? What criteria define the rollout sequence (plan type, geography, account size)? Document this before choosing a launch date.

Positioning and one-sentence pitch

One sentence that any team member can say to a customer. Written by PM, tested with sales. If sales can't use it in a call, it's not ready.

Readiness checklist

Help docs live, support trained, sales demo delivered, changelog written, in-app announcement drafted. All must be true before launch date, with named DRI per item.

Launch metrics

Feature adoption rate at D7 and D30, support ticket volume in first 72h, in-app satisfaction score at D30. Reviewed at the 30-day post-launch review.

On-call PM

Named PM available for the first 72 hours post-launch to make decisions if early data shows a problem. Not a passive observer — they have authority to roll back or restrict rollout.

Common pitfalls

PitfallWhy it happensWhat to do instead
GTM written on launch dayGTM is treated as a post-engineering task, not a parallel workstream.Start the GTM Playbook when the product brief is approved. GTM preparation is a 3–4 week process — it cannot be done in the week before launch.
Readiness checklist is aspirational, not verifiedPM marks items "done" without confirming with the responsible team.Each readiness item requires a sign-off from the named DRI, not the PM. "Help docs live" means the support lead confirms they are live and reviewed — not that the PM believes they should be live.
No post-launch review at day 30Team moves to the next feature immediately after launch.Schedule the 30-day post-launch review on the day the feature ships. It is harder to cancel a scheduled meeting than to schedule one after the fact when everyone is busy.
