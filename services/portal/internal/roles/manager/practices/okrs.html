OKRs — aligned goal-setting

One qualitative objective, three to five measurable key results, reviewed weekly, scored quarterly.

TL;DR

OKRs (Objectives and Key Results) is the goal-setting framework used by Intel, Google, LinkedIn, Twitter, and thousands of other companies. An Objective is qualitative and aspirational. Key Results are quantitative and measurable — not tasks, but outcomes. Teams score KRs 0.0–1.0 each week. A score of 0.7 is a success; 1.0 every quarter means the goals were too easy.

What it is

OKRs (Objectives and Key Results) is a goal-setting framework originated at Intel by Andy Grove and popularised at Google by John Doerr. It separates direction (the Objective) from measurement (the Key Results).

An Objective is qualitative, inspiring, and achievable within a quarter. It answers "where do we want to go?" Examples: "Make the onboarding experience feel effortless," "Become the most trusted analytics product in our category."

Key Results are quantitative, time-bound outcomes that define what it looks like to achieve the Objective. They are not tasks or deliverables — they are outcomes. Examples: "Reduce time-to-first-value from 12 minutes to 4 minutes," "Increase Week-4 retention from 28% to 40%."

OKRs are scored weekly on a 0.0–1.0 scale: 0.0 means no progress, 1.0 means fully achieved. A quarterly score of 0.7 is considered a success. Consistently hitting 1.0 is a sign that the targets were set too conservatively.

Why we need it

Without a goal-setting framework, teams optimise for output — shipping features — because that is what is visible and measurable in a sprint board. OKRs force the team to define success in terms of customer outcomes before any work begins, making output measurements secondary to outcome measurements.

OKRs also solve the alignment problem in organisations with multiple teams. When every team has an OKR that rolls up to a company-level OKR, cross-team trade-offs become about which outcome is more strategically important — a decidable question — rather than which team has more political influence — an undecidable one.

How others do it

Google

Google has used OKRs since 1999. Key practices: OKRs are public — every Googler can see every other Googler's OKRs. Roughly 60% of OKRs are set bottom-up by teams (not handed down). Company OKRs are set by leadership and remain fixed; team OKRs can change quarterly.

What we take: Public OKRs create natural alignment without a top-down process. When a team can see what all adjacent teams are optimising for, conflicts surface early and are resolved through conversation rather than discovery at launch time.

Source: Google re:Work — OKRs guide

LinkedIn

LinkedIn uses OKRs with a modification: each KR has both a "committed" target (0.7 equivalent — must achieve) and a "aspirational" target (1.0 — stretch goal). This removes the incentive to sandbag committed targets and gives the team a clear escalation path when a KR is at risk.

What we take: Committed vs. aspirational targets solve the sandbagging problem that plagues pure 0.7-is-success OKR implementations. If you're missing the committed target, that's a crisis; if you're missing the aspirational target, that's expected.

Source: What Matters — LinkedIn OKRs

Intel (Andy Grove original)

Grove's original formulation at Intel was simpler than modern OKR implementations: one Objective, three KRs, reviewed in weekly one-on-ones. The weekly check-in was the critical element — Grove believed the Objective set the direction but the weekly conversation about KR progress was where management actually happened.

What we take: The weekly score check-in is not optional. An OKR set at quarter start and reviewed at quarter end is a reporting tool, not a management tool. Weekly scores surface problems while there is still time to act on them.

Source: High Output Management — Grove

The pattern across all successful OKR implementations: public visibility, weekly check-ins, and clear separation between output (what we ship) and outcome (what changes as a result). Companies that score OKRs quarterly have a reporting process; companies that score weekly have a management process.

Anatomy of a well-formed OKR

Objective

Qualitative, inspiring, achievable this quarter. Passes the "does it make people want to come to work?" test.

Key Results (3–5)

Outcomes, not outputs. "Ship the redesign" is an output; "Increase activation rate by 15%" is an outcome.

Weekly score

0.0–1.0 per KR, updated by the PM in a standing 15-minute meeting each Monday.

Mid-quarter check

At week 6, if a KR is below 0.3, either fix the approach or mark the KR as at risk and escalate.

Quarter retrospective

Final score + written reflection: what drove the score, what we'd do differently, what we learned.

Common pitfalls

PitfallWhy it happensWhat to do instead
Key Results are tasks, not outcomes"Launch feature X" is easier to write than measuring an outcome.Reject any KR that doesn't include a number. "Launch X" becomes "X drives 20% of new activations by end of quarter."
OKRs set once and reviewed onceWeekly check-ins feel like overhead.15 minutes weekly is the minimum. Missing the rhythm means the OKR is decorative, not operational.
Too many OKRsEvery team adds an OKR for every initiative.Maximum three team OKRs per quarter. If everything is a priority, nothing is.
KRs tied to bonusIncentive design requires measurable targets.OKRs and compensation bonuses must be decoupled. Tying them to money incentivises sandbagging.
