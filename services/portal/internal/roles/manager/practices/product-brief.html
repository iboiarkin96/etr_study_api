Product Brief / PRD — the contract before you build

A one-to-three page document that gives engineering the why, the who, the what, and explicitly not the how — written before sprint planning begins.

TL;DR

The Product Brief (or modern lightweight PRD) is a written contract between PM and engineering that covers: the problem being solved, the customer segment, the measurable success metric, the scope (in and explicitly out), and the acceptance criteria. It is not a technical specification — it describes outcomes, not implementation. The brief is written before any sprint commitment and approved by the engineering lead before work begins.

What it is

The Product Brief is the modern, lightweight successor to the traditional PRD (Product Requirements Document). Where a PRD could run to 50 pages of implementation detail, a Product Brief is one to three pages that answers only the questions engineering needs answered to build the right thing:

  • Problem: What customer pain are we solving? Who has it? What is the evidence for its size and urgency?
  • Outcome: What measurable change will tell us we succeeded? How will we measure it? When?
  • Scope: What is explicitly in scope (expressed as acceptance criteria, not implementation steps)? What is explicitly out of scope?
  • Open questions: What do we not yet know? Who is responsible for resolving each open question, and by when?

The brief is written by the PM, reviewed by the engineering lead, and approved before any sprint commitment. It is not a design document — UX and technical decisions are made by the team during the sprint. The brief defines the problem and the success bar; the sprint defines the solution.

Why we need it

In the absence of a written brief, scope is defined by whoever speaks most confidently in the sprint planning meeting. The PM thinks the feature solves one problem; the engineer builds it to solve a slightly different one; the customer needed something else entirely. All three interpretations are plausible from the Jira story title. The brief eliminates the ambiguity by making the problem statement and success criteria explicit before a single line of code is written.

The explicit "out of scope" section is particularly valuable. Teams under deadline pressure constantly discover that "out of scope" means different things to different people. Writing it down creates a defensible boundary: when a stakeholder requests an addition mid-sprint, the PM can say "that's explicitly out of scope in the brief — we can create a new ticket for it."

How others do it

Stripe

Stripe's PM team uses a Product Brief format that requires four sections: the customer problem, the success metric, a list of what the feature does NOT do, and a section called "riskiest assumptions" — three to five beliefs that, if wrong, would invalidate the entire approach. The riskiest assumptions section must be filled before the brief is approved.

What we take: The "riskiest assumptions" section forces the PM to articulate what they don't know before engineering invests. Any assumption that is risky enough to list is risky enough to validate in discovery before the sprint begins. This prevents building on false premises.

Source: Stripe Product Team Blog

Linear

Linear uses a "spec" format written as a Notion page linked from the GitHub issue. The spec has three headings: "Why we're doing this," "What success looks like," and "What we're not doing." Engineering leads review the spec and add a "technical concerns" section. The spec is never longer than the GitHub issue it links to — if it is, the feature is too big for one issue.

What we take: The length constraint is self-enforcing scope discipline. A brief that needs more than one page to explain the problem probably describes two problems, not one. Split it before writing code.

Source: Linear Blog

Intercom

Intercom's product brief process starts with a "problem statement" reviewed by the product leadership team before any solution is discussed. The brief is written in three drafts: draft 1 defines the problem only; draft 2 adds the success metric; draft 3 adds scope after a 30-minute session with engineering to estimate feasibility. No solutions are written in the brief at any stage.

What we take: Separating problem, metric, and scope into three draft stages prevents premature solutioning. When a PM writes the problem and immediately proposes a solution in the same sentence, the solution crowds out better alternatives the team would have found in sprint planning.

Source: Intercom Engineering Blog

Anatomy

Problem statement

One paragraph: who has the pain, what is the pain, and what evidence proves it is real and large enough to address.

Success metric

One measurable outcome: "X% improvement in Y within Z weeks." If you can't name the metric, you don't understand the problem.

Acceptance criteria

Three to eight testable criteria that define "done." Written in customer-observable terms, not implementation terms.

Out of scope

An explicit list of related things you are NOT building this sprint. This section prevents scope creep by establishing a defensible boundary before the sprint starts.

Open questions

Unresolved decisions with a named DRI and a resolution deadline. Questions not resolved before sprint planning are a risk — flag them.

Approvals

Engineering lead sign-off before sprint entry. PM + engineering lead signatures mark the brief as a shared commitment.

Common pitfalls

PitfallWhy it happensWhat to do instead
Brief written after sprint startsPM is pressured to start building immediately.No sprint commitment until the brief is approved. The brief takes 2–4 hours; mid-sprint ambiguity costs 2–4 days.
Out-of-scope section is emptyPM doesn't want to preemptively close options.Ask the team: "What related things might someone expect to be in this?" Anything mentioned becomes an out-of-scope candidate.
Success metric is a delivery milestone, not an outcome"Feature shipped" is easier to measure than customer behaviour change.Delivery is not success. The metric must describe a customer behaviour change or a business result that happens after shipping.
