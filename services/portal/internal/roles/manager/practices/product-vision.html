Product Vision & Narrative — the northstar

A written product vision that serves as the litmus test for every major roadmap decision.

TL;DR

A product vision is one to two paragraphs describing the world three to five years out: who benefits, what changes for them, and what unique role the product plays. It is written, not presented. It survives team changes, org redesigns, and market pivots. Amazon's Working Backwards method — writing a mock press release before building — is the most rigorous way to create one.

What it is

A Product Vision is a short written statement (one to two paragraphs) describing the future the product is working towards, who benefits, and what changes in their lives as a result. It is written in plain language, readable by any stakeholder, and durable enough to survive a team reorganisation or a market pivot.

The most rigorous method for developing a product vision is Working Backwards (Amazon's PR/FAQ): the PM writes a mock press release dated three to five years in the future, announcing the product's impact. If the PM cannot write a coherent press release, the product vision is not clear enough to make roadmap decisions from.

A vision is not a mission statement ("we empower teams to collaborate") or a values list. It describes a specific future state that the product makes real: "In 2029, every engineering team at a mid-market SaaS company can trace every product decision back to a customer outcome, in real time, without a data analyst."

Why we need it

Without a written vision, roadmap debates are power contests. With a clear vision, every roadmap debate has a resolution mechanism: "does this bring us closer to the vision?" is a question that can be answered. "Should we build this?" cannot be answered without knowing what the product is trying to become.

The vision also functions as a recruiting tool, an investor alignment tool, and a new hire orientation tool — all without requiring the PM's involvement. A new engineer who reads the vision on day one understands the product's trajectory without needing a briefing. A candidate who reads it before their interview can self-select in or out.

How others do it

Amazon

Amazon's Working Backwards process requires a mock press release (one page) before any significant initiative begins. The press release describes the finished product as if it has shipped. It must be comprehensible to a non-technical customer. No slides — only prose. Senior leadership reviews the press release, not a deck.

What we take: Prose clarity is harder to fake than slide clarity. If you cannot write a coherent press release about what you're building, the product idea isn't ready to build. Writing before building is the discipline.

Source: Amazon Working Backwards

Netflix

Netflix's product vision for streaming — "to become the world's best Internet TV network" — was set in 2007 when the company was still primarily a DVD-by-mail business. That single sentence guided every product decision for the next decade, including the decision to build original content, to expand internationally, and to invest in recommendation algorithms.

What we take: A good vision is uncomfortable — it describes a future that doesn't exist yet and requires the company to fundamentally change to reach it. If the vision describes the present state, it isn't a vision.

Source: SVPG — Inspired (vision excerpt)

Slack

Slack's early vision — "making your working life simpler, more pleasant, and more productive" — was narrow enough to be actionable and broad enough to not constrain the product to a specific feature set. Stewart Butterfield sent a memo to the team before launch that described the product's vision and purpose in 1,500 words. It became the founding document for every product decision in the early years.

What we take: A vision memo is the PM's equivalent of a founding document. Writing it is the act of leadership, not just planning. It should be read by every person who joins the team.

Source: Stewart Butterfield — We Don't Sell Saddles Here

Anatomy of a product vision statement

Time horizon

Three to five years out. Far enough to be ambitious, close enough to feel real.

Beneficiary

Specific customer segment in plain language. Not "users" — "mid-market SaaS engineering teams."

Change

What is different in their life/work because the product exists? The more concrete the better.

Unique angle

Why this product and not a competitor? The vision should be defensible, not generic.

Readable by anyone

No jargon, no internal acronyms. A new hire on day one reads it and immediately understands direction.

Common pitfalls

PitfallWhy it happensWhat to do instead
Vision is a values list"We believe in transparency and empowerment" is safe and meaningless.A vision describes a specific future state. Replace values with outcomes: "In 2029, X happens because our product exists."
Vision is too broad"Transform how people work" could describe any B2B SaaS product.A good vision should feel slightly wrong to someone outside the target market. That specificity is the point.
Vision is never updatedSet in year one and forgotten.Review the vision annually. If it still describes a plausible future, keep it. If the market has shifted enough to invalidate it, update it with the same rigour you used to write it.

Quality bar — what good looks like

  • ○

    Written vision published in PM portal

    One to two paragraphs, accessible to all team members.

  • ○

    Every major roadmap decision references the vision

    The product brief template includes a "vision alignment" section.

  • ○

    Vision reviewed annually

    With a written retrospective: is this still the right future to pursue?
