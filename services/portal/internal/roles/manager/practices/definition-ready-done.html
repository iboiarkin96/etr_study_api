Definition of Ready & Done — the quality gates

Two checklists that prevent premature sprint entry and premature sign-off — the team's agreed contract on what "ready to build" and "done" mean.

TL;DR

Definition of Ready (DoR) is a checklist a story must pass before it enters a sprint. Definition of Done (DoD) is a checklist a story must pass before it is marked complete. Both are written and owned by the team, not the PM alone. DoR prevents mid-sprint ambiguity; DoD prevents premature sign-off and accumulation of undetected debt. Together they define the team's quality contract.

What it is

The Definition of Ready is a team-agreed checklist of conditions a user story must satisfy before it can be selected for a sprint. Common DoR criteria include: the story has a written product brief, acceptance criteria are written in testable terms, the engineering team has estimated the effort, all dependencies are identified, and no open blocking questions remain.

The Definition of Done is a team-agreed checklist of conditions that must be met before a story can be marked complete. Common DoD criteria include: all acceptance criteria are verified by PM, automated tests pass, manual testing is complete, code is reviewed and merged, documentation is updated, and the feature is deployed to production (or staging with a scheduled production deployment).

Both definitions are owned by the whole team — PM, engineering, and QA — and reviewed at least quarterly. Neither is a PM document imposed on engineering.

Why we need it

Without a DoR, sprint planning is a negotiation between the PM's optimism and the engineering team's uncertainty. Stories enter the sprint half-baked, and the team discovers the ambiguity mid-sprint when it is most expensive to resolve. A DoR makes the pre-sprint preparation work explicit: the PM's job is to get stories through the DoR before the sprint planning meeting, not during it.

Without a DoD, "done" means different things to different people. An engineer may mark a story done when tests pass locally; the PM may consider it done when the feature is live in production; the customer considers it done when the documentation is updated. The DoD creates a shared, explicit definition that prevents the phantom progress problem — where the sprint board shows 80% complete but nothing is actually shippable.

How others do it

Atlassian

Atlassian's engineering teams post their DoD physically on the wall of the team space and as a pinned message in the team Slack channel. Any team member can call a "DoD check" during sprint review, requiring the presenter to walk through the checklist for each story. Stories that fail DoD are moved back to in-progress, not counted as velocity.

What we take: Making the DoD visible and invokable by any team member — not just the PM — distributes quality ownership across the team. When only the PM checks DoD, it becomes a formality. When any engineer can call a DoD check, it becomes a cultural norm.

Source: Atlassian — Definition of Done

Spotify

Spotify squads maintain separate DoDs for different story types: feature stories, bug fixes, and technical debt stories each have a different DoD. Feature stories require customer-facing documentation and PM sign-off; bug fixes require a regression test added; technical debt stories require a measurable before/after metric. A story's type is set at creation, not retrospectively.

What we take: Type-specific DoDs prevent the common failure mode where a bug fix is held to feature documentation standards (slowing it) or a feature is held to bug fix standards (shipping without documentation). The DoD must match the story type.

Source: Spotify Engineering Blog

Anatomy

DoR — brief approved

PM and engineering lead have signed off on the product brief. No brief = not ready. No exceptions.

DoR — AC written

Acceptance criteria are written in testable form: "Given X, when Y, then Z." Vague AC is a blocker.

DoR — effort estimated

Engineering has provided a story point or t-shirt size estimate. Stories without estimates cannot be committed to a sprint.

DoD — AC verified by PM

PM has personally tested each acceptance criterion against the built feature. Not delegated to QA.

DoD — tests passing

Automated test suite passes in CI. Manual test cases documented and executed. No open P1/P2 bugs.

DoD — production-ready

Deployed to production or staging with a scheduled deployment. Not "merged to main" — code in a branch is not done.
